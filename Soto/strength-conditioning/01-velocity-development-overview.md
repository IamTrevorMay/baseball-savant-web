# What Actually Drives Pitching Velocity — A Component Model and the Evidence Behind It

## TL;DR

- **Velocity is a system output, not a single trait.** Model it as three multiplicative buckets — **output** (how much force/power the body can produce), **transfer** (mechanics/sequencing that route that force into the ball), and **tissue tolerance** (whether the arm can survive the intent). A pitcher can max one and stall on another; the binding constraint moves as an athlete develops. (plausible — practitioner framework, not a validated equation)
- **Weighted-ball programs reliably add velocity but the mean is smaller and the variance larger than marketing implies.** A systematic review of 10 studies found 7 showed gains of roughly **2–11 mph**; Driveline's own 6-week program averaged **+2.7 mph (2016) and +3.3 mph (2017)**; the one high-quality RCT (Reinold 2018) found only **~2.2 mph (3.3%)** — with a **24% injury rate** vs 0% in controls. (proven that they raise velocity; promising-to-cautionary on the risk/reward)
- **Strength training moves throwing velocity with a large pooled effect** (meta-analysis: ES ≈ **1.10**, 16 studies, 424 subjects) — but the effect is far bigger in **adults (ES 1.34)** than **teenagers (ES 0.49)**, and much of the elite-level evidence is handball, not baseball. (proven that strength helps; effect size is age- and training-age-dependent)
- **Mass helps, but "mass equals gas" is a correlation dressed as a law.** Bodyweight correlates with velocity across populations, yet within trained pitchers a single anthropometric predictor (height) explained only **~23% of velocity variance** (PLOS One, n=64), and Driveline found **near-zero** correlation (r² ≈ 0.014–0.022) between vertical-jump force/bodyweight and velocity. Weight gain is a lever, not the lever. (plausible; the naive version is overstated)
- **Realistic velo gains are governed by training age, not calendar age.** Untrained teens in a growth window can add **4–7 mph/year**; a trained college/pro arm celebrates **1–3 mph** from a dedicated block, and full-group program means are often **0–3.5 mph** with a meaningful non-responder fraction (Driveline's own kinematics study: **47% of subjects lost velocity**). (promising)
- **The league keeps moving the target.** MLB four-seam average hit **94.7 mph in 2026**, up for the **6th straight year** from 91.9 mph in 2008 — righties average 95.2. Benchmarks Neptune sets for athletes must be indexed to this rising baseline, not a static chart. (proven — Statcast)
- **Underload (light-ball) work tends to out-produce overload for pure velocity** — Driveline data cited underload ~**+3 mph vs +1.5 mph** overload — while heavy balls (14–32 oz) drive the largest elbow/shoulder torque and most of the injury signal. (promising)
- **Lower-body and rotational power beat vertical jump as velocity correlates.** Sprint acceleration ratios and loaded-CMJ ratios showed up in a predictive velocity model where raw vertical jump did not; pelvis/trunk rotational velocity and hip-shoulder separation are the transfer mechanisms that actually route leg power into the ball. (promising)

## 1. The component model: output × transfer × tolerance

The most useful mental model for velocity is not additive but **multiplicative**, because the whole chain is only as strong as its weakest link. Break it into three buckets.

**Output** is the raw physical engine: rate of force development, rotational power, lower-body drive, lean muscle mass, and the specific ability to accelerate a light implement fast (arm speed). This is the domain of the weight room, medicine-ball and plyo work, sprinting, and throwing intent. Excellent velocity, as Tread Athletics frames it, "is a result of being able to transfer a large amount of force into the baseball as quickly as possible" — but the force has to exist first (plausible).

**Transfer** is mechanics and sequencing: how efficiently the kinetic chain routes hip and trunk power up through the shoulder, elbow, wrist, and ball. The ASMI/Fleisig biomechanics literature identifies the load-bearing transfer variables — pelvis and upper-torso orientation at maximum external rotation, average pelvis velocity during arm cocking, upper-torso rotational velocity during acceleration, and hip-shoulder (pelvis-trunk) separation (proven within-pitcher correlates). A pitcher with a big engine and poor sequencing "leaks" velocity; one with modest output but elite timing can over-perform their physical testing.

**Tolerance** is whether the arm's connective tissue can survive the intent required to express the other two. This is the bucket practitioners skip in marketing and pay for in the training room. Every mph of intent raises elbow varus torque, which stresses the UCL. A velocity program that ignores tolerance produces the Reinold pattern: real gains, real injuries.

The practical value of the model is **constraint identification** — at any moment, one bucket is the bottleneck. A 15-year-old with 55 lb of "projectable" frame is output-limited; a physically mature college arm sitting 88 with textbook testing numbers is usually transfer- or tolerance-limited. Training the non-binding constraint wastes adaptive capacity and injury risk budget.

**For Soto:** This maps cleanly onto a Neptune assessment spine. Output = force plates + med-ball/CMJ + pulldown velocity; Transfer = TrackMan release/extension + (future) markerless mocap of sequencing; Tolerance = arm-care/ROM screen + workload monitoring. Triton already owns the data layer (Compete/TrackMan) to score two of the three buckets today, and Stuff+ inputs (velo, movement, extension) are downstream outputs of this same chain — extension in particular is a transfer/anthropometric proxy that feeds `100 + veloZ*4.5 + moveZ*3.5 + extZ*2.0`.

## 2. Weighted balls: what the evidence actually says

Weighted-ball training is the most-studied and most-oversold velocity intervention, so it deserves the most careful accounting.

**Do they add velocity? Mostly yes, but modestly on average.** A systematic review (PMC6378453) screened 4,119 titles down to **10 studies**: **7 of 10 reported significant velocity gains ranging 2–11.2 mph**, 3 found no significant difference. The headline numbers (Logan +9.28 mph, Litwhiler +11.2 mph) come from older, weaker designs; the modern, better-controlled numbers are smaller. Driveline's own program reporting averaged **+2.7 mph (2016)** and **+3.3 mph (2017)** across a 6-week block (proven that gains occur; the mean is ~2–3.5 mph, not the double-digit outliers).

**The best-designed study is the most sobering.** Reinold et al. (2018, *Sports Health*) randomized **38 healthy HS pitchers (13–18)** into a 6-week program using 2, 4, 6, 16, and 32 oz balls vs a regulation-ball control. The training group gained a statistically significant **3.3%** velocity and **+4.3° shoulder external-rotation ROM** — but the **injury rate was 24%** (four elbow injuries including olecranon stress fractures and a UCL injury referred to surgery) vs **0% in controls** (proven — this is a true RCT). The gain was real; so was the cost.

**Underload beats overload for pure velocity.** Driveline data cited average gains nearly twice as large in the underload condition (**~+3 mph**) vs overload (**~+1.5 mph**). Biomechanically this makes sense: lighter-than-baseball implements let the arm move faster (train arm speed) while *reducing* elbow varus and shoulder IR torque, whereas heavy balls (14–32 oz) drive the **largest** elbow flexion/varus torque — Fleisig found flat-ground throws with 14 and 32 oz balls produced significantly greater elbow flexion torque than 4–7 oz balls (proven). Okoroha similarly showed ball weight correlates with medial elbow torque in youth. So the heavy end of the spectrum is where most of the stress — and most of the injury signal — lives.

**Non-responders are the rule, not the exception.** Driveline's own 6-week kinematics study (PMC6254244, n=17 college/pro) found **no significant velocity change for the full group** — **9 subjects gained, 8 lost**. Interestingly it also showed *no* significant change in elbow valgus torque but *did* show internal-rotation velocity rise (4,527 → 4,759 °/s) and shoulder external rotation at release *decrease* (95° → 86°), i.e. mechanical adaptation without guaranteed velocity payoff (promising, with a strong non-responder caveat).

**Verdict:** Weighted balls are a legitimate, evidence-backed velocity tool with a genuine dose-dependent injury risk. The GRADE quality of the pooled literature is "very poor" due to heterogeneous protocols (debunked as a settled/safe intervention; proven as an *effective-but-risky* one).

**For Soto:** For Neptune, weighted-ball work should be gated behind a tolerance screen and biased toward **underload for velocity, controlled overload for strength/tissue prep**, with heavy implements (>16 oz) reserved for physically mature, well-monitored arms. Workload must flow into Triton's Compete pipeline so ACWR-style monitoring can flag the Reinold-type risk before it becomes an injury. Do **not** sell a "+X mph guaranteed" story — the honest expectation is a mean of ~2–3 mph with a real chance of no gain.

## 3. Strength training: large pooled effect, but age-scaled

Strength training has the cleanest "it works" verdict of any velocity input — with an important asterisk.

A meta-analysis of overhead-throwing strength interventions (Martínez-García et al., 2021; 16 studies, 424 subjects) found a **large pooled effect on throwing velocity: ES 1.10 (95% CI 0.64–1.57, p<0.00001)** (proven). But the effect is **not uniform**: adults ES **1.34** vs teenagers ES **0.49**; males ES 1.12, females 1.22. A separate systematic review (PMC11550301, 17 studies) reported strength-modality gains of **3.7–26%**, with resistance-band and forearm-loading categories most consistent, but noted only about half of studies beat their control group significantly (proven the direction; the magnitude is modality- and population-dependent).

Two caveats keep this honest. First, **much of the elite evidence is handball, not baseball** — the transfer to a 5 oz ball off a mound is assumed, not proven. Second, **strength is necessary but not sufficient**: it feeds the *output* bucket, and if transfer or tolerance is the binding constraint, more squat does nothing for the radar gun. Tread's own messaging — that a college pitcher went "89 to 98 in less than a year" — bundles strength, mass, mechanics, and throwing volume together; you cannot attribute that 9 mph to the weight room alone (plausible).

**For Soto:** Strength is the highest-confidence lever for Neptune's *output* bucket and the safest to prescribe broadly, but program it with age scaling — expect large effects in mature athletes, modest ones in early-teens where the growth window and skill acquisition dominate. This is also where mass and strength blur, which is the next section.

## 4. Body mass and anthropometrics: real, but overstated as a law

"Mass equals gas" is the most repeated slogan in velocity training, and it is a half-truth worth dissecting.

**Across populations, bigger throws harder.** Bodyweight correlates with velocity in collegiate samples, and higher competitive tiers carry more lean mass — Tread's height-based targets (e.g., a 6'3" pitcher near 205 lb at ~12% body fat) reflect a real between-level pattern (plausible). Height, weight, BMI, and shoulder ROM all show up as significant predictors in youth-heavy datasets.

**But within trained pitchers, the anthropometric signal collapses.** The PLOS One tryout study (n=64 adult males) built a predictive model — **velocity = 53.22 + 0.21·height + 14.3·CMJ-ratio + 75.03·sprint-ratio** — with **adjusted R² = 0.230**. Height survived; **weight, BMI, hand size, and wingspan did not** make the model. That is: anthropometrics explain under a quarter of velocity variance among already-selected pitchers, and *height* (unchangeable) mattered more than *mass* (trainable). Most tellingly, Driveline found **near-zero correlation between vertical-jump force normalized to bodyweight and velocity** (r² ≈ 0.014 mound, 0.022 pulldown, n=17–29) — being strong-per-pound does not predict throwing hard (plausible/debunked for the strong naive version).

The reconciliation: **mass is a permissive factor, not a causal dial.** Adding lean mass raises the *ceiling* of force you can produce and gives the tissue more to work with, but it only converts to velocity if transfer and intent express it. A pitcher who gains 15 lb and no velocity has almost certainly hit a transfer or tolerance constraint. The honest framing for an athlete: *mass buys you a bigger engine; it does not drive the car.*

**For Soto:** For Neptune intake, capture height, weight, lean-mass estimate, and wingspan — but weight them as *context and projection inputs*, not as a velocity promise. Track body-composition change alongside velocity so you can show an athlete when mass is or isn't converting. For Triton, note that **extension** (a partial function of height/limb length, feeding `extZ` in Stuff+) is the one anthropometric that mechanically reaches the ball — a tall pitcher's perceived velocity edge is real and already modeled.

## 5. Lower-body power, sprinting, and rotational transfer

If vertical jump is a weak velocity correlate, what lower-body quality actually matters? The answer is **rotational and horizontal power expressed through the pitching-specific chain**, not generic jump height.

The PLOS model above put **sprint ratio** (0–10 m vs 0–30 m timing) and **loaded-CMJ ratio** in the equation while excluding raw jump. The interpretation: pitchers benefit from an **acceleration-dominant** profile — quick to get moving, which mirrors the explosive drive-leg push off the rubber — more than pure top-end speed. Sprint literature is genuinely mixed (some studies find 10 m and 60 yd times uncorrelated to velocity), and the correlation appears **stronger in younger athletes** (promising, not proven).

The transfer mechanism is rotational. ASMI/Fleisig work shows **average pelvis velocity during arm cocking** and **upper-torso velocity during acceleration** are among the strongest within-pitcher velocity correlates, and **pelvis-trunk separation** loads the core "like a torsion spring." Crucially, training axial dissociation "may increase maximal trunk rotation velocity and thereby increase ball velocity **without increasing** training load on the shoulder and elbow" (proven-ish within-pitcher; a rare lever that improves output *and* tolerance simultaneously). OpenBiomechanics-based work (Driveline's open dataset) continues to quantify lower-body kinematic contributions to velocity, and the falling cost of markerless mocap is making this measurable at facility scale (promising).

Ground reaction forces close the loop: the drive leg generates vertical and anterior-posterior force, the stride leg brakes and redirects it, and **braking force correlates with ball velocity** in collegiate pitchers. Force-plate jump testing is more useful as a **fatigue/readiness monitor** (drop in power output flags a fried athlete) than as a velocity predictor.

**For Soto:** Neptune's lower-body assessment should prioritize **rotational/horizontal power and drive-leg braking** (force plates, med-ball rotational throws, short-sprint acceleration) over vertical jump for velocity prediction — but keep CMJ on force plates as a **readiness/fatigue** tool feeding workload monitoring. Hip-shoulder separation and pelvis-trunk sequencing are the highest-value *transfer* targets because they can raise velocity without spending tolerance budget.

## 6. Long toss and throwing intent

Long toss occupies a middle ground between "training tool" and "velocity intervention," and the biomechanics clarify why it's useful but not magic.

There is **no universal definition** — pros treat long toss as ~177 ft arched throws for shoulder stretch; ATs use ~157 ft line throws for rehab. The key biomechanical finding: **hard, on-line throws from 120–180 ft closely resemble mound-pitching mechanics**, while **maximum-distance throws (averaging ~260 ft) significantly alter mechanics** — greater max shoulder external rotation, elbow flexion, shoulder IR torque, and elbow varus torque (proven). So max-distance "pulldown-style" long toss is a higher-stress stimulus, not a low-effort one.

The most practically important finding is the **effort-perception gap**: at 120 ft, pitchers instructed to *decrease* perceived effort actually dropped elbow varus torque only **7%** and velocity only **11%** — meaning "throwing easy" often isn't (proven). This has direct workload-monitoring implications: RPE-based throwing prescriptions systematically underestimate real arm stress.

Pulldowns (running, crow-hop max-effort throws into a net) are Driveline's signature velocity-testing tool — they run about **5.35–5.5 mph faster than mound velocity** on average, and arm stress is **similar to high-intent mound pitching** (promising). They're a legitimate way to train and *measure* peak output, but they carry mound-equivalent stress and should be counted as such.

**For Soto:** Treat long toss and pulldowns as **output-bucket, high-intent stimuli** with real stress cost — not as recovery work. The 7%/11% effort-gap finding is a strong argument for Neptune to measure throws (TrackMan/pulldown velo) rather than trust athlete-reported effort, and to log every high-intent throw into Compete for workload accounting.

## 7. Realistic velocity expectations by age and training age

The single most common expectation-setting error is quoting *calendar-age* gains to a *high-training-age* athlete.

**By calendar age (untrained/developing, 50th percentile, TopVelocity chart):** 12U ~54 mph, 14U ~65, 16U ~74, 18/HS senior ~81, D1 college ~88, Pro/MiLB ~92, MLB ~93.8. Elite (top ~1%) benchmarks run far higher: 14U ~82, 16U ~90, 18U ~94, D1 ~96, MLB 100+ (plausible — aggregated radar/showcase data, not peer-reviewed).

**The growth-spurt window (≈13–16) is where big natural gains live** — 4–7 mph/year is realistic here, driven by height, mass, neural maturation *and* training simultaneously. It is genuinely hard to isolate the training effect from maturation in this band, which is exactly why teen strength-training effect sizes look modest (ES 0.49) — the maturation is doing a lot of the work anyway.

**As training age rises, the marginal gain shrinks hard.** A trained college or pro arm typically celebrates **1–3 mph** from a focused block, and full-*group* program means frequently land **0–3.5 mph** with a substantial non-responder fraction (recall Driveline's 47% who *lost* velocity). Diminishing returns are structural: the closer an athlete is to their genetic/mechanical ceiling, the more each additional mph costs in training stress and injury risk.

**The moving baseline matters.** MLB average four-seam velocity reached **94.7 mph in 2026** (righties 95.2), up for the **6th straight season** from 91.9 mph in 2008. A 90-mph high-schooler in 2010 and 2026 are not the same prospect. Any benchmark Neptune sets should be indexed to current, level-specific distributions, not a fixed number.

**For Soto:** Build Neptune's expectation-setting around **training age and percentile-in-level**, not raw mph promises. Concretely: for a developmental teen, "4–7 mph/year is plausible in the growth window"; for a mature college/pro arm, "1–3 mph from a dedicated block, with real chance of a plateau." Feed these as level-and-age-adjusted percentile bands (Triton already computes 50th-percentile league benchmarks per season/level/role — the same machinery can serve age-band velocity percentiles for the facility). This also protects credibility with Trevor: he has lived the mature-arm, post-TJ reality where 1 mph is a win, not a disappointment.

## 8. Injury risk is part of the velocity equation, not a footnote

Every intervention above trades against tissue tolerance, and the honest velocity conversation prices that in.

The Reinold RCT is the anchor: **24% injury rate** in a 6-week HS weighted-ball program vs 0% control, for a ~2.2 mph gain (proven). Weighted-ball stress scales with implement weight (Fleisig, Okoroha), max-distance long toss elevates elbow varus torque, and pulldowns carry mound-equivalent stress. Meanwhile, the league-wide velocity arms race correlates with the well-documented rise in UCL reconstructions — velocity is the strongest modifiable risk factor for elbow injury, and Trevor's own 2017 Tommy John is a lived example of the arc.

The reconciling principle: **you cannot chase output indefinitely without buying tolerance in parallel.** The rotational-transfer levers (hip-shoulder separation, sequencing) are attractive precisely because they can add velocity *without* adding arm stress. Everything else — heavier balls, more intent, more max-distance throwing — spends tolerance budget and must be monitored.

**For Soto:** This is the differentiator for a "development lab" positioning. Neptune's product spine should make **tolerance monitoring** (ROM screens, workload/ACWR, force-plate readiness) a first-class citizen alongside velocity chasing — and Triton's Compete pipeline is the natural home for it. The sellable story isn't "we'll add mph"; it's "we'll add mph your arm can survive." That framing is both more honest and more defensible to an evidence-literate operator.

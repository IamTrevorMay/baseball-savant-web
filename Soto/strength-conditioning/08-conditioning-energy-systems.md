# Conditioning for Baseball — Energy Systems, the Death of Poles, and Sprint-Based Programming

## TL;DR

- **A pitch lasts ~1–2 seconds with ~15–25 seconds between pitches — this is an alactic (ATP-CP) sport with an aerobic recovery tail, not a glycolytic one.** Potteiger's simulated-game data showed no rise in blood lactate pre- to post-pitching, with VO2 peaking at ~20 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ during work and falling to 4.9 between innings (proven).
- **The single most cited study buries long-slow-distance for pitchers: Rhea et al. (2008), "Noncompatibility of Power and Endurance."** Over one collegiate season, 8 sprinters *gained* ~210.6 W of peak power while 8 endurance runners *lost* ~39.5 W — a swing Cressey summarizes as roughly +20% vs −3% power (proven for the concurrent-training interference effect; promising as a direct pitcher-performance claim given n=16).
- **Running "poles" to "flush lactic acid" is physiologically incoherent** — there is no meaningful lactate to flush, and steady-state jogging trains the wrong energy system while adding pounding volume (debunked). Rabena's NCAA D-II thesis found no velocity/WHIP advantage for distance work and a strong trend (p=0.071) toward *more* soreness in the distance group.
- **But aerobics are not the enemy — the argument is about dose and modality.** Aerobic fitness accelerates phosphocreatine resynthesis (50% of PCr restored in ~30 s, ~100% by ~3 min; fast component half-time 21–22 s), which is exactly the between-pitch recovery a pitcher needs (proven physiology; plausible as a performance lever).
- **Aerobic capacity tracks starter performance.** In 24 pro pitchers (40 season-cases, 2007–2013), VO2max averaged 48.1 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ and correlated with starter ERA (r = −0.68), WHIP (r = −0.69), and K/9 (r = +0.61); above-average VO2max starters posted 3.77 ERA / 1.25 WHIP vs 5.04 / 1.48 below-average — with almost no relationship for relievers (promising, observational).
- **Conditioning is best framed as active recovery, dosed near-daily at low intensity, not as "cardio."** Tread Athletics and the HRV literature converge: moderate aerobic work raises next-morning HRV (parasympathetic rebound), while grinding daily max-effort sprints suppresses it (promising).
- **The 2024 MLB pitching-injury report named a "possible decline in cardiovascular and endurance training" as a contributing factor** alongside max-effort velocity chasing — MLB fastball velo rose 91.3 → 94.2 mph (2008→2024) as elbow IL days climbed 3,940 → 12,185 (2005→2024) (proven trend; plausible causal link for conditioning).
- **Practical prescription: sprint-based power work (10–60 m, 1:3–1:6 work:rest), tempo runs at 70–80% for recovery, and a modest cardiac-output aerobic base (130–150 bpm, 20–45 min) — never steady-state grinding the day after an outing** (promising, practitioner-consensus).

## Why This Debate Matters More in 2026 Than Ever

Pitching has never been more violent. Fleisig's foundational kinetics work put elbow varus torque near **64 N·m** (with the broader literature spanning **64–120 N·m**) and shoulder compressive force at **~1090 N** at ball release — and the ulnar collateral ligament contributes only ~**33 N·m** of resistance against a cadaveric failure threshold of **30–35 N·m**. In plain terms: near-maximal pitches load the UCL to roughly its ultimate tensile limit *every throw* (proven). Velocity has climbed relentlessly — average MLB fastball **91.3 mph in 2008 to 94.2 mph in 2024** — and Tommy John counts across affiliated ball ran **104 (2010) → 314 (2020) → 281 (2024)**, with elbow IL days nearly tripling from **3,940 (2005) to 12,185 (2024)** and more than one-third of drafted pitchers now arriving with a prior UCL reconstruction (proven).

The December 2024 MLB report, built on surveys of team medical and performance staff, fingered max-effort, velocity-and-shape-at-all-costs pitching as the prime driver — and, notably, listed a **possible decline in cardiovascular and endurance base training** and workload structures that reward short max-effort bursts over durable output as contributing factors (proven the report said it; plausible as a mechanism). That single sentence is why the conditioning conversation can't be dismissed as old-school busywork versus new-school sprinting. The correct read isn't "aerobic work is back" or "sprinting won" — it's that **the energy-system logic has to match the actual demand, and the recovery infrastructure of the athlete has to be built deliberately.**

**For Soto:** This is the framing for any Neptune arm-care narrative and any Trevor-facing programming: conditioning is not fitness theater, it's *recovery capacity engineering* sitting underneath a joint-torque problem. Every Neptune athlete dashboard should treat "conditioning" as a recovery/durability input, not a calorie-burn output.

## The Energy Systems, Honestly

Three ATP pathways run simultaneously; the question is only which dominates for a given effort.

- **Phosphagen / alactic (ATP-CP):** Immediate, oxygen-independent, and essentially spent within ~10 seconds of all-out effort. This is the delivery of a single pitch (~1–2 s) and the swing. It is the pitcher's home system (proven).
- **Glycolytic / lactic:** Dominant for sustained hard efforts roughly 15 s–2 min. A pitcher rarely lives here — you don't accumulate meaningful lactate throwing one pitch every 15–25 seconds. Potteiger's classic simulated-game study found **no significant difference between pre- and post-pitching blood lactate** (proven).
- **Aerobic / oxidative:** The recovery and replenishment engine. Between pitches and between innings, the aerobic system rebuilds ATP and phosphocreatine and clears metabolic byproducts (proven).

The linchpin fact — and the reason the "aerobics are useless for pitchers" camp overshoots — is **phosphocreatine resynthesis is an aerobic process**. After an all-out effort, roughly **50% of PCr is restored in ~30 seconds and ~100% by ~3 minutes** of passive recovery. The kinetics are biphasic: a fast component with a half-time of **~21–22 s** and a slow component with a half-time **>170 s**. Critically, **more aerobically fit athletes resynthesize PCr faster** (shorter half-time), because rephosphorylation is oxygen-dependent (proven). So the between-pitch, between-inning, and between-outing recovery a pitcher lives and dies by is *literally aerobically powered*. The aerobic system doesn't throw the pitch; it pays for the next one.

Potteiger's VO2 numbers make the intensity picture concrete: mean VO2 peaked around **20 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹** during the game and dropped to **4.9 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹** between innings — nowhere near a limiting aerobic demand for a fit athlete. VO2max is not the *limiter* of a single pitch; it's the *substrate for recovery* across a 100-pitch outing and a 30-start season (proven).

**For Soto:** This maps cleanly onto Triton's fatigue surfaces. In-game velocity decay (first-inning vs final-inning FB velocity — starters lose nearly a full mph over a 5+ inning start in April) is the observable output of PCr/recovery capacity degrading. A Neptune "recovery capacity" metric could combine aerobic markers (resting HR, HRV trend, a submax aerobic test) with in-outing velo-maintenance from Compete/TrackMan. That's a differentiated, physiology-grounded facility metric.

## The Death of Poles: What the Evidence Actually Says

"Running poles" — jogging foul-pole to foul-pole, often the day after an outing, to "flush lactic acid" — is the practice under indictment. Three lines of evidence sink it.

**1. There's no lactic acid to flush.** Potteiger's finding of unchanged blood lactate pre/post pitching removes the entire rationale. Lactate isn't the villain of arm soreness anyway; DOMS is mechanical/inflammatory, not lactate-driven. The "flush" premise is a physiological myth (debunked).

**2. Concurrent endurance work suppresses power — the interference effect.** Rhea et al. (2008), *Noncompatibility of Power and Endurance Training Among College Baseball Players* (J Strength Cond Res), split a collegiate staff into two matched groups of 8 for a full season. Everything was identical except the running:
- **Sprint group:** 3×/week, **10–30 sprints of 15–60 m with 10–60 s rest**.
- **Endurance group:** moderate-to-high-intensity jog/cycle, **3–4×/week, 20–60 min**.

Result: peak power output **rose ~210.6 W in the sprinters and fell ~39.5 W in the endurance group** over the season. Cressey renders the same effect as roughly **+20% vs −3%** power. Potteiger's earlier training study similarly found sprint work produced significant improvements in pitching velocity and anaerobic power vs aerobic dance training (p<0.05) (proven interference effect; the direct "distance running makes you a worse pitcher" leap is promising given small samples and power-as-proxy).

**3. When you test pitching outcomes directly, distance work doesn't help — and may add soreness.** Rabena's NCAA D-II master's thesis compared steady-state aerobic vs interval training and found **no significant difference in velocity, WHIP, sprint time, or fatigue index** (p>0.05), plus a **strong trend (p=0.071) toward more soreness in the distance group** (promising). Translation: at best distance running is neutral for performance; at worst it steals power adaptation and adds junk pounding to already-loaded legs and connective tissue.

The practical verdict from Cressey, Driveline, Heenan, and the Florida Baseball ARMory converges: **pitchers should essentially never run beyond ~60 yards for "conditioning," and anything past ~150 m is distance running.** The old "10 poles in 30 seconds" or long post-start jogs mismatch the sport's work-to-rest structure and train an adaptation that competes with the one you actually need (promising, strong practitioner consensus).

**For Soto:** When a coach or parent pushes back with "we always ran poles and were fine," the answer isn't ideology — it's the Rhea numbers, the Potteiger lactate finding, and the Rabena soreness trend. Ship those three citations as a one-card explainer.

## But Don't Throw the Aerobic Baby Out

The sprint-only zealotry overcorrected. Three arguments keep a *deliberate, low-dose* aerobic base in the program.

**1. PCr resynthesis is aerobic** (covered above). A bigger aerobic engine = faster between-pitch and between-inning recovery = better velocity maintenance deep into outings (proven physiology).

**2. Aerobic capacity tracks starter performance.** The 24-pitcher pro study (40 season-cases, ≥50 IP, 2007–2013) is the strongest observational data point. Group VO2max averaged **48.1 ± 5.3 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹**; starters ran higher than relievers (**49.5 vs 45.3, p≤0.01**). For *starters*, VO2max correlated with **ERA (r = −0.68), WHIP (r = −0.69), FIP (r = −0.57), K/9 (r = +0.61)** — all p≤0.01. Above-average-VO2max starters posted **3.77 ERA / 1.25 WHIP vs 5.04 / 1.48** for below-average. Relievers showed essentially no such relationships (promising; observational, can't separate "fitter guys throw better" from "better pitchers train more"). The position split is the actionable nuance: **starters need more aerobic base than relievers** because they need recovery capacity across 90–110 pitches and faster between-start turnaround.

**3. Aerobic work is a parasympathetic recovery tool.** Moderate aerobic exercise stimulates parasympathetic (vagal) tone and tends to raise next-morning HRV, whereas blocks of high-intensity conditioning with little low-intensity work drive HRV down. This is why Tread Athletics and others reframe most in-season conditioning as **active recovery** rather than "cardio" or "speed work" — it can be dosed near-daily precisely because it's low-intensity (promising).

The mechanism for building the engine without stealing power is Joel Jamieson's **cardiac output method**: keep heart rate **130–150 bpm (younger) / 120–150 (older)** for **30–90 min**, modality-agnostic (bike, sled drags, jump rope, low-effort circuits), 1–2×/week. The target adaptation is **eccentric left-ventricular hypertrophy** — a bigger stroke volume (chamber size, not wall thickness), which raises the whole aerobic ceiling without the pounding of running or the interference of threshold work (promising). Low-intensity, non-impact modalities are the key — the interference effect in Rhea came from *moderate-to-high-intensity* running, not from easy cardiac-output work.

**For Soto:** For Trevor's own staying-sharp training, a bike-based cardiac-output block (2×/week, HR 130–150, 30–40 min, low impact given TJ/late-career joints) is the highest-value, lowest-risk conditioning input — builds recovery capacity, protects HRV, zero interference with power, zero arm/leg pounding. For Neptune, position-specific aerobic dosing (more for projected starters) is a defensible programming differentiator over commodity "run and lift" barns.

## Sprint-Based Conditioning: The Real Power Work

If long-slow-distance is out, sprinting is the anchor — for pitchers, sprinting is simultaneously power development, alactic conditioning, and athleticism/CNS work.

**Prescription principles (practitioner consensus):**
- **Distances:** 10–60 m. Acceleration work 10–20 m; top-speed exposure up to 50–60 m. Never past ~60 yd for pitchers.
- **Work:rest:** True speed work needs near-full recovery — **1:6 to 1:10+** (i.e., 10–60 s rest per short sprint) so every rep is high-quality and alactic. Repeat-sprint-ability (RSA) or conditioning-flavored work uses tighter ratios (**1:3–1:4**), which intentionally bleeds into glycolytic/aerobic recovery.
- **Volume:** 10–30 reps depending on distance and phase (Rhea's effective protocol lived here).
- **Frequency:** 2–3 quality sprint days/week off-season; reduced and blended with movement/agility in-season.
- **Intent:** Reps must be *fast*. The near-universal in-season failure mode (Tread flags it) is grinding daily "sprints" at 70–80% for high volume — that's neither speed nor recovery, it's junk fatigue.

**Tempo runs** are the bridge tool: **40–60 yards at 70–80% of max**, walk back (roughly **1:2** ratio), repeated for **15–30 minutes**. They deliver a "distance-run feel" for aerobic/recovery benefit and capillary/tendon conditioning **without the joint pounding of poles and without max-CNS cost** — ideal for a recovery day or as aerobic-base volume that won't trip the interference effect (promising).

**For Soto:** Neptune's assessment battery should include a **10-yard and 30-yard sprint (laser/timing gate)** — it doubles as a power-output benchmark (dynamic correspondence to lower-half explosiveness that feeds pitch velocity) and a longitudinal conditioning KPI. TrackMan already captures the throwing side; timing gates close the athletic-testing loop cheaply.

## In-Season Maintenance: The Between-Starts Model

In-season, the goal flips from *building* to *maintaining power while maximizing recovery*. Cressey's between-starts template is the reference implementation.

**5-day starter rotation:**
- **Day 0** — Outing.
- **Day 1** — Lower-body strength + light upper-body (push-ups, horizontal pulls), rotator cuff. (Highest systemic load, farthest from next start.)
- **Day 2** — Movement/sprint day: short sprints (10–15 yd), agility, some top-speed (50–60 yd). Alactic power maintenance.
- **Day 3** — Bullpen, single-leg work, upper-body lift, cuff.
- **Day 4** — Low-intensity dynamic flexibility / recovery circuit only.
- **Day 5** — Next start.

**7-day rotation** adds a second movement day and a full-body lift mid-week, same principles, daily mobility.

**Relievers** can't periodize around a known start, so they need daily readiness: brief alactic touches (a few short sprints or med-ball throws), daily mobility/cuff, and low-intensity aerobic/tempo work as the recovery backbone — dosed so nothing accumulates.

**Intensity guardrails:** low-intensity work sits **under ~70% heart-rate reserve**; true power work is **≥90% effort**. Avoid the mushy middle (steady 20–60 min moderate-hard jogging) that both fails to build the aerobic engine efficiently *and* interferes with power. Driveline's in-season lifting philosophy — **keep 2–4 reps in the tank (RIR)** and cut volume vs off-season because standing, throwing, and fielding already add load — is the right dial for conditioning too (promising).

The **day-after-outing** slot is where poles historically lived and where they do the most harm. Replace with a **recovery circuit or tempo runs**: mobility (shoulder flexion, T-spine rotation), anti-rotation core, soft-tissue work, and light heart-rate elevation (kettlebell swings, goblet squats, sled) in continuous fashion with 10–20 s micro-rests — parasympathetic-biased, blood-flow-driven recovery without pounding or lactate mythology.

**For Soto:** Encode these as reusable Neptune programming templates keyed to role (SP-5day / SP-7day / RP-daily) and to Triton's SP/RP classification (≥3 games with 50+ pitches → SP). The workload/arm-care monitoring spine should flag "day-after high-intensity conditioning" as an anti-pattern automatically.

## A Practical Weekly Conditioning Template

Concrete starting points (individualize by age, level, and monitoring data).

**Off-season pitcher (power + engine build):**
- Mon — Sprints: 8–12 × 20–30 m, 1:8 rest (full recovery, max intent).
- Tue — Cardiac-output aerobic: bike/sled, HR 130–150, 30–40 min (low impact).
- Wed — Med-ball power circuit + short accelerations (6–8 × 10–15 yd).
- Thu — Tempo runs: 10–14 × 50 yd @ 75%, walk-back (~1:2), ~20 min.
- Fri — Sprints/top-speed: 6–8 × 40–60 yd, full recovery.
- Sat — Cardiac-output or recovery circuit, 30 min.
- Sun — Off / mobility.

**In-season starter (5-day, conditioning slice only):**
- Day 1 (post-start) — Recovery circuit or tempo runs, 15–20 min, parasympathetic-biased.
- Day 2 — Short sprints/agility (alactic power touch), 8–10 reps.
- Day 3 — Minimal (bullpen day); mobility.
- Day 4 — Low-intensity flush only (<70% HRR), 10–15 min.
- Day 5 — Start.

**In-season reliever (daily readiness):**
- Daily — Mobility + cuff; 3–5 short alactic efforts (sprints or med-ball); low-intensity aerobic/tempo backbone 10–15 min. Never accumulate glycolytic fatigue on a potential appearance day.

**Youth / developing (Neptune travel/HS bands):** Emphasize general athleticism and sprint mechanics over "conditioning." Med-ball throws, short sprints, tempo runs, play. Skip formal high-volume aerobic blocks; the training age and total throwing volume don't warrant it, and arm-care caution outranks conditioning gains (plausible).

## The Nuances Practitioners Fight About

- **"Aerobic base vs sprint-only" is mostly a strawman.** The real disagreement is *modality and intensity*, not aerobic-vs-anaerobic. Nobody credible advocates threshold jogging; nobody credible advocates zero aerobic capacity. Low-intensity, low-impact aerobic work (cardiac output, tempo) coexists fine with sprint power — the interference effect is dose- and intensity-dependent, and Rhea's harm came from moderate-to-high-intensity running (proven mechanism, nuanced application).
- **Correlation ≠ prescription in the VO2max study.** Fitter starters pitching better could reflect that durable, professional starters simply train more and recover better — not that bolting on cardio raises a given pitcher's ERA. Treat the r = −0.68 as *supportive*, not causal (promising, not proven).
- **Power-as-proxy caveat in Rhea.** The outcome was lower-body peak power, not directly ERA/velocity/health. The interference effect on power is robust; the leap to "distance running makes worse pitchers" is reasonable but not directly measured at scale (honest limitation).
- **Between-innings arm cooling** emerged as a small but real lever: pitchers who cooled arms maintained FB velocity into innings 4–5 while non-coolers declined — a recovery-side intervention adjacent to conditioning (promising, limited samples).
- **The macro trend is the tiebreaker.** With velocity up ~3 mph since 2008, elbow IL days tripled, and a landmark MLB report explicitly naming reduced endurance/cardiovascular base as a plausible contributor, the durability argument for *some* well-chosen aerobic capacity has more weight in 2026 than the pure sprint-only stance did a decade ago (proven trend; the conditioning link remains plausible, not proven).

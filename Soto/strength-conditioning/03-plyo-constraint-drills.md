# Plyo Ball & Constraint Drills — Catalog, Deficiency Mapping, and Transfer Evidence

## TL;DR

- **The Driveline plyo routine is a fixed five-position spine — reverse throws → pivot picks → two individualized drills → walking windups — thrown daily at sub-max intent using balls from 100 g to 2,000 g (≈3.5 oz to 4.4 lb).** Heavier balls slow the arm, which is exactly why they lead the warm-up rather than the velocity block (proven).
- **Plyo/weighted-ball programs reliably move the needle on velocity, but the effect is modest and heterogeneous in controlled work: recent RCTs show roughly +2.1 to +3.3 mph (3.3%) over 6 weeks, versus the exaggerated +9 to +11 mph of 1960s–70s uncontrolled studies** (promising). Systematic-review GRADE quality is rated "very poor."
- **The best-run replication of Driveline's own protocol (Marsh et al. 2018, n=17) found NO net velocity change — 9 gained, 8 lost — despite real kinematic adaptation** (internal-rotation velocity +232°/s, IR range of motion +7°). Individual response dominates group means (proven).
- **The safety signal is real and directional: Reinold's 2018 RCT logged a 24% injury rate in the weighted-ball group (4 elbow injuries in 19 HS pitchers) vs. 0% in controls; a MiLB field study found weighted-ball users lost 44.4 vs. 23.2 days and carried arm-injury rates of 11.8 vs. 7.5 per 1,000 exposures** (promising — underpowered but consistent).
- **Counter-intuitively, heavier balls REDUCE peak elbow varus torque during the throw itself — Fleisig's biomechanics work shows torque and arm velocity fall monotonically from 5 oz up to 32 oz.** The injury risk lives in the *high-intent light-ball and flat-ground-hold* work, not the heavy plyo warm-up (proven).
- **Constraint drills work by self-organization, not verbal cueing: change the implement and the environment, and the body discovers the target pattern — "the weighted ball is its own pitching coach."** External-focus constraints beat internal ("get your arm up") cues for full-body ballistic skills (proven).
- **Motor-learning transfer favors variable/self-organizing methods: in a baseball hitting study, constraints-led and differential-learning groups both beat prescriptive instruction, and differential learning won the transfer test to novel conditions** — the drill only counts if it survives the jump to the mound (promising).
- **The same plyo ball is a warm-up tool, a mechanical-change tool, and a recovery/decel tool depending on intent and drill selection — reverse throws and rebounder catches train the decelerators post-throw; roll-ins and janitors reshape the pattern; walking windups blend intent back to the delivery** (plausible).

## 1. What a plyo ball actually is, and why the weight range matters

Driveline "PlyoCare" balls (and their PRP/Tread equivalents) are soft, sand/gel-filled polyurethane balls sold in a color-coded weight ladder — commonly 100 g (green, ~3.5 oz), 150 g (black), 225 g (blue, the "control" plyo), 450 g (red), 1,000 g (yellow), and up to 2,000 g in the heaviest overload sets. Some catalogs describe the full range as roughly **3 oz to 4 lb** (proven). They are distinct from *weighted baseballs* (hard, seamed, thrown off a mound in the 3–7 oz range) — plyos are thrown into a wall or rebounder without the accuracy anxiety of a real throw, which is central to their function as constraint tools.

The load matters mechanically. Fleisig et al.'s biomechanics lab work established that as ball weight climbs from a standard 5 oz toward 32 oz, **arm and trunk angular velocities decrease steadily, and peak elbow varus torque and shoulder internal-rotation velocity fall with them** (proven). The Driveline routine article states the practical corollary plainly: "heavier balls… make the arm travel slower, thus making them great for a warm-up." So the counter-intuitive truth is that the *heavy* plyo throws that look scary are the low-torque end of the spectrum; the dangerous end is *light* implements thrown at max intent, where arm speed and stress climb above game levels.

**For Soto:** this is the single most important framing to bake into any Neptune programming template — a plyo drill's stress is governed jointly by *ball weight × intent × drill constraint*, not by the ball being "weighted." A 1,000 g reverse throw at 40% is a recovery input; a 3.5 oz run-and-gun at 100% is a velocity stressor. Neptune's intake and monitoring should tag every throwing input by that triad, not by implement alone.

## 2. The core Driveline plyo drill catalog and intent of each

The published Driveline plyo routine is a **fixed sequence** with an individualized middle (proven):

**1. Reverse throws (always first).** Kneeling on the throwing-side knee, the athlete holds the ball low, rotates the torso and arm back, and throws the ball into a wall *behind* them. It is a posterior-shoulder and deceleration/eccentric conditioning drill — it loads the decelerators (external rotators, posterior cuff, scapular stabilizers) that absorb the follow-through. Reinold-style clinicians class this as an eccentric/decel input, which is why it also appears in *post*-throw recovery blocks (proven for posterior-chain loading; plausible for injury protection).

**2. Pivot picks / pivot pickoffs (always second).** From a counter-rotated, hand-hung-back position the athlete drives the wrist over the elbow into the throw. Trains **forward rotation, medial-forearm strength, pronation timing, and connected arm action** — a low-complexity patterning drill that grooves the layback-to-release transition (plausible).

**3–4. Two individualized drills (athlete-specific).** Chosen from a menu — **scap retractions, roll-ins, step backs, drop steps, janitors, rockers** — based on the athlete's assessed movement deficiencies. This is where the routine becomes a prescription rather than a warm-up (see §3).

**5. Walking windups (always last).** A progression off drop-step throws: a walking step into the leg kick with momentum directed at the target, thrown with higher intent. It reintegrates the individualized pieces into a full-body, momentum-driven delivery and is the bridge back toward mound intent (plausible).

Marsh et al. (2018) confirm this exact five-exercise spine — "reverse throws, pivot pickoffs, roll-in throws, rockers, and walking windups" — as the plyometric component of the studied six-week program, thrown across six training days per week at graded intensities (high/moderate/recovery) alongside long toss, lifting, and a rebounder + kettlebell-carry recovery circuit (proven).

## 3. Drill-to-deficiency mapping

The individualized middle of the routine is the mechanical-change engine. The mapping practitioners use:

- **Roll-ins** — for pitchers who over-rotate the hips early or fly open. The back foot stays pointed at the target and does *not* pivot, keeping the hips open while the athlete *feels the arm keep the upper body closed against open hips*. Constrains hip-shoulder separation timing so the trunk learns to stay stacked and delayed (plausible).
- **Janitors** — a constrained drop step: start closed, let the lower half rotate while the **upper half stays quiet and delayed**. Directly targets early trunk rotation / poor "hip-shoulder separation." Used as Caleb Thielbar's offseason drill of choice (plausible).
- **Drop steps / step backs** — for pitchers who can't feel or sequence the back leg. The step-back makes the backside load and drive easier to sense, addressing weak or early lower-half engagement (plausible).
- **Rockers** — weight shift, front-leg block, and timing. Feet near stride length with the front foot slightly closed; rock back and forth to build momentum, then throw. For athletes who leak forward early or lack a stable front side (plausible).
- **Scap retractions / "Marshalls"** — arm action and scap load with glove-side integrity; the Marshalls drill (Wolforth-origin, Driveline-popularized) trains scap load and connection. For pitchers with a disconnected or short arm action (plausible).
- **Reverse throws** — posterior-shoulder conditioning; prescribed heavier for pitchers who need decel capacity or are in a recovery emphasis (plausible).
- **Pivot picks / quick picks** — pronation timing and medial-forearm patterning; for late or supinated release feel (plausible).

The honest evidence grade on *specific* drill-to-fault claims is **plausible, not proven** — these are practitioner heuristics validated by motion-capture assessment on the individual, not by controlled drill-isolation trials. What *is* well-supported is the meta-principle: individualized programming built off an assessment outperforms a one-size template. Marsh et al. explicitly note the program "tailors the routine to the individual athlete" via motion capture.

**For Soto:** this maps cleanly onto Neptune's assessment→programming→monitoring spine and onto Triton metrics. A TrackMan/Compete-derived flag (e.g., low extension, poor VAA consistency, release-point scatter) can drive a drill prescription, and re-assessment after a plyo block closes the loop. The drill catalog is essentially a *lookup table keyed on assessment deficiency* — a natural fit for a Triton "prescription engine" tile. ## 4. Constraint drills: mechanical change vs. warm-up vs. recovery

The same implement serves three distinct jobs; the *intent and drill* decide which (plausible, well-established in practice):

**Warm-up.** Heavier plyos thrown sub-max at a wall slow the arm and pattern the movement without accuracy stress. Reverse throws → pivot picks are a near-universal on-ramp. PRP prescribes ~5 minutes of basic shoulder activation/arm patterning pre-throw. The physics (heavier = slower arm) makes overload plyos a natural warm-up load.

**Mechanical change.** The individualized middle drills, thrown with enough intent to matter but constrained enough to force the target pattern. This is *constraint-led* work: the implement and setup remove degrees of freedom so the athlete self-organizes toward the desired coordination (see §5). Driveline's guidance is to keep this in a *separate environment* from the mound so it doesn't leak overthinking into competition.

**Recovery / arm care.** Post-throw, plyos become decelerator and force-acceptance training. **Reverse throws** and **rebounder force-acceptance catches** (throw into a rebounder, catch straight-armed same hand — "focus on the catch, not the throw") load the eccentric decelerators to speed recovery. Recovery days run 50–60% intent; hybrid days ~70–90%; velocity days 100%, capped at 1–2×/week (3× is "too much high-intent throwing") (plausible).

**Return-to-throw / rehab.** Plyo balls are a formal rung in post-op protocols. Programs (e.g., ASMI/Andrews-lineage, Dr. Ahmad's plyo-ball rehab) use two-hand and one-hand plyo drills against a wall/trampoline as a bridge between isolated cuff/scap strengthening and an interval throwing program, progressing by ball weight and distance as symptoms allow (plausible). The reverse-throw/decel emphasis is the same tissue that fails in follow-through, which is why it anchors both recovery and rehab blocks.

**For Soto — Trevor:** as a post-2017 Tommy John pitcher who built a multi-year late-career run, Trevor's plyo use should skew toward the *warm-up and recovery/decel* ends — reverse throws, rebounder force-acceptance, sub-max patterning — with high-intent light-ball work rationed hard and only on demo/content days. The velocity-stress budget (light ball × max intent × flat-ground holds) is where the UCL risk concentrates, and it buys little for a coaching/credibility goal versus a comeback goal. For content, the heavy-plyo warm-up drills demo well *and* are the low-torque end of the menu.

## 5. Why constraint drills work: self-organization over cueing

The mechanism is motor-learning theory, not muscle. The constraints-led approach (CLA) holds that skilled movement *emerges* from the interaction of the performer, task, and environment; you shape it by manipulating constraints rather than dictating positions. Driveline's "different model for cueing" article is a clean statement of this: replace internal cues ("your arm needs to be higher") with **external constraints and cues** ("throw through the wall," green ball in the glove hand to force positive disconnection), because "weighted balls are their own pitching coaches" — the implement itself narrows movement options toward the target pattern (proven for the external-focus principle; plausible for specific implementations).

The external-focus literature (Wulf and successors) robustly supports directing attention to the movement *effect* rather than body parts for powerful, full-body ballistic skills (proven). CLA layers on **repetition-without-repetition**: vary the constraint so the athlete explores the perceptual-motor solution space rather than memorizing one groove. The related **differential learning (DL)** method pushes this further with deliberate movement variability.

A key nuance for a data-driven persona: CLA/DL are strong on *transfer* but can *underperform* blocked/prescriptive practice during *acquisition*. In basketball and general motor studies, blocked practice yields higher accuracy while you're drilling, but differential/variable groups win at retention and transfer to novel conditions. Translation: constraint drills can look worse in the cage and better on the mound. That is a feature, but it means you cannot judge a drill by in-session numbers alone.

## 6. Evidence for transfer: does the drill reach the mound?

This is the crux, and the evidence is real but partial.

- **Direct baseball transfer study (opposite-field hitting).** Comparing CLA, differential learning, and prescriptive instruction, both self-organizing methods (CLA and DL) produced significantly greater improvement than prescriptive instruction, and **the differential-learning group outperformed all others on the transfer test to novel conditions** (promising). This is hitting, not pitching, but it's the cleanest baseball-specific transfer signal available and it validates the *category*.
- **Meta-analytic support for variable methods.** A systematic review/meta-analysis of differential learning reports statistically significant, large effect sizes favoring DL over traditional training across post-, retention, and *transfer* tests (promising — with the usual caveats about small, heterogeneous studies).
- **Weighted-ball velocity transfer.** Seven of ten studies in the systematic review found velocity gains, but modern controlled work clusters at +2 to +3.3 mph over 6 weeks, and Driveline's own replication (Marsh 2018) found **no net gain** with wide individual scatter. So *velocity* transfer is real on average but small and unreliable at the individual level (promising).
- **Kinematic transfer.** Marsh 2018 did show adaptation that transfers to the delivery even without velocity gain: internal-rotation velocity +232°/s (4,527→4,759°/s), shoulder-adduction torque +35 N·m, and increased internal-rotation ROM (+7° dominant arm). The pattern changed; the radar gun didn't always reward it (proven for the specific cohort).

The synthesis: constraint and plyo drills are **credible tools for changing movement and, on average, nudging velocity**, but transfer is (a) highly individual, (b) better judged on the mound than in the drill, and (c) dependent on eventually blending back to full-intent, game-representative throwing — which the walking-windup → mound-blend progression is designed to do.

## 7. The safety envelope: numbers, risk concentration, and monitoring

The strength-and-conditioning reality is that plyo/weighted-ball work has a genuine, if imperfectly quantified, injury signal that a data-driven facility must manage rather than ignore.

- **Reinold et al. 2018 (RCT, n=38 HS pitchers, mean age 15.3, balls 2–32 oz, 3×/wk × 6 wk):** +3.3% velocity and +4.3° external-rotation ROM in the weighted group, but a **24% injury rate** — 4 of 19 suffered elbow injuries (2 during training, 2 the next season) vs. **0%** in controls (promising; small sample, adolescent cohort). This is the most-cited caution flag.
- **MiLB field study (n=88 pro pitchers, ~52% weighted-ball users):** users vs. non-users showed **arm-injury rates of 11.8 vs. 7.5 per 1,000 exposures**, trunk 5.9 vs. 2.1, injury prevalence 39.1% vs. 23.8%, and **time loss of 44.4 vs. 23.2 days** (nearly double). Authors flag it as underpowered (planned n=357) — directional, not definitive (promising).
- **Fleisig biomechanics (protective side):** peak elbow varus torque and arm velocity *decrease* as ball weight rises 5→32 oz; underweight (4 oz) throws are similar to standard. The risk is not the heavy plyo — it's **high-intent light-implement throwing and flat-ground holds**, which Fleisig frames as "hybrid exercises between throwing and resistance training," not biomechanics-enhancers, and which drive internal-rotation velocity and elbow varus torque *up* (proven).
- **Velocity–UCL link (indirect):** some analyses put ~+20% higher odds of UCL surgery per +1 mph (case-control), while others (Erickson 2016) found no velocity–UCL correlation — so "the thing plyos are for" (velocity) is itself an ambiguous risk factor (debated). GRADE quality across the weighted-ball literature is "very poor."

Risk-concentration model: stress ≈ *intent × (implement lightness) × (flat-ground-hold exposure) × (weekly high-intent frequency)*. Cap max-intent throwing at 1–2×/week, keep heavy plyos in the warm-up/recovery role, and treat 3–7 oz max-effort mound work as the accountable stressor.

If Neptune serves youth/HS bands, Reinold's adolescent 24% figure is the governing datum — youth weighted-ball programming should be conservative, ROM-monitored (watch external-rotation gain and GIRD), and paired with a workload monitor. ## 8. Practical programming defaults (synthesis)

A defensible default block, synthesized from Driveline/PRP/Tread published structures (plausible; individualize off assessment):

- **Every throwing day, warm-up:** reverse throws (heavy, e.g., 1,000–2,000 g, sub-max) → pivot picks → 2 individualized drills → walking windups, ~submax intent.
- **Day types:** recovery (50–60%), hybrid (70/90%), velocity (100%, ≤1–2×/wk). Off-season 5 days/wk with 2 velo-tracking days; in-season 3–4 days/wk with 0–1 velo days.
- **Mechanical block:** individualized constraint drills (roll-ins, janitors, step-backs, rockers) keyed to assessed faults, kept in a separate environment from mound competition work, then blended back via walking windups → mound.
- **Recovery block:** reverse throws + rebounder force-acceptance catches (decel focus), bands, light plyos.
- **Monitoring:** re-assess *on the mound*; grade on mound deltas; track ROM and high-intent volume for the safety envelope.

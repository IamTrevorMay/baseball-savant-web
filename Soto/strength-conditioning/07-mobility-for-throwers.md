# Mobility for Throwers — Thoracic Rotation, Hip ROM, Shoulder Management, and Screening-to-Programming Logic

## TL;DR

- **Thoracic rotation is the mobility bucket with the best risk/reward for throwers.** Pro pitchers land at front-foot strike with ~55° of upper-torso rotation; the SFMA dysfunction cutoff for lumbar-locked thoracic rotation is <50°, and normative isolated T-spine rotation is ~47°. In HS pitchers, lower throwing-side thoracic rotation correlates with *more* trunk lateral flexion (r = −0.51) and *higher* elbow valgus torque (r = −0.45). Buy thoracic ROM freely — it offloads the shoulder and elbow. (promising)
- **Hip internal rotation buys velocity but sells elbow health — it is a dose curve, not "more is better."** In 99 collegiate pitchers, +10° lead-hip IR at max shoulder ER = +0.6 m/s (~1.3 mph) but +5 N·m elbow varus moment (~6.7%). Hip position explained only 20–33% of variance. Target a *range* (~10–20° usable lead-hip IR), not a maximum. (promising)
- **Do NOT stretch a throwing shoulder into external rotation.** Pitchers already carry 8.3–15.6° more dominant-side ER, most of it bony (humeral retrotorsion), paired with anterior laxity. Cressey and Reinold both abandoned aggressive ER stretching and the sleeper stretch; a 2022 RCT (n=113 HS pitchers) found ER *strengthening* non-inferior to sleeper stretching, with injuries 9.8% (strength) vs 22.6% (stretch). (proven that ER stretch is unnecessary/risky; promising on strength alternative)
- **The predictive shoulder-ROM red flags are total-rotation deficit and flexion deficit, not GIRD in isolation.** In 296 pro pitchers (505 exams, 2005–2012): >5° total-rotation deficit = 2.6× injury risk; ≥5° flexion deficit = 2.8× elbow-injury risk; ER insufficiency (<5° more than non-throwing arm) = 2.2× shoulder-DL risk. A 3,314-athlete meta-analysis found GIRD ≥20° alone NOT associated with injury. (proven)
- **Ankle dorsiflexion is a shock-absorption qualifier, not a velocity driver.** Across 800+ Driveline force-plate sessions, ankle DF at foot-plant showed no notable correlation with velocity or block quality. Lead-leg vertical GRF (r ≈ 0.44 raw) and knee extension (34–36° in high performers vs 5–7° in low) are the real block levers. Knee-to-wall ~10 cm / ~5 in is the functional floor. (promising)
- **Screening beats guessing, but the goal is asymmetry and pattern, not chasing textbook numbers.** Bilateral differences >5–8° (hip) or a total-arc change (shoulder) flag adaptation vs pathology. Assess → classify (mobility restriction vs stability deficit vs benign adaptation) → program the smallest effective input. (plausible)
- **Joint-by-joint is the right mental model: ankle and hip and T-spine want mobility; knee, lumbar spine, and scapula want stability.** A stiff T-spine forces the lumbar spine and anterior shoulder to donate motion they should not — the classic thrower failure cascade. (plausible)
- **You are far more likely to get hurt loose than tight.** For hypermobile throwers, the intervention is stability/motor control, not stretching. Mobility work should be surgical, regional, and re-tested — never a blanket "everyone foam-rolls and stretches" warm-up. (promising)

## 1. Why Mobility for Throwers Is a Distribution Problem, Not a "Get Flexible" Problem

The single most important idea in this document: pitching is a sequenced transfer of energy up a kinetic chain, and mobility only helps when it lets the *right* joint contribute the *right* motion at the *right* time. Blanket flexibility is not the goal; a favorable **distribution** of motion across the chain is. The joint-by-joint framework (Gray Cook and Michael Boyle) captures this: the body is a stack of joints that alternate in primary need — ankle (mobility), knee (stability), hip (mobility), lumbar spine (stability), thoracic spine (mobility), glenohumeral joint (mobility with heavy stability demand), scapula (stability). (plausible) When a "mobility" joint underperforms, the adjacent "stability" joint is forced to donate motion it was not built to give, and that borrowed motion is where pain and tissue failure show up — Cook's line: "they already knew where it hurt, you just gave a Latin name."

The canonical thrower example: a T-spine that won't rotate forces the lumbar spine (which should stay stable) and the anterior shoulder capsule (which should stay taut) to make up the deficit. That is precisely the mechanism behind oblique strains, low-back pain, and anterior shoulder irritation in pitchers. (plausible) So the screening-to-programming job is not "make everything more flexible" — it is "find the joint that is refusing to do its job, and restore *its* motion so the neighbors stop compensating."

**For Soto:** This maps cleanly onto a Neptune assessment battery that should output a *per-joint mobility/stability verdict*, not a single "flexibility score." The intake screen should tag each segment as: adequate, restricted (mobility deficit), or unstable/lax (stability deficit) — because the intervention flips completely between the last two. Pair it with the TrackMan/biomech layer so a ROM restriction can be linked to the specific kinematic leak it is likely causing.

## 2. Thoracic Rotation — The Best Risk/Reward Mobility Bucket

Thoracic mobility is the mobility target with the cleanest upside for throwers because more of it tends to *reduce* downstream shoulder and elbow load while enabling the hip-shoulder separation that drives velocity.

**Benchmarks.** Isolated thoracic-spine rotation normative value is ~47°, and the SFMA calls the T-spine dysfunctional below 50° in the lumbar-locked (quadruped) rotation test. (promising) In thoracolumbar standing rotation (dual inclinometer, C7–S2), 56 collegiate/minor-league pitchers rotated 51.9° ± 6.6° to the non-throwing side vs 48.8° ± 6.4° to the throwing side — a significant 3.18° asymmetry toward the glove side (p = .006) that position players did not show, and pitchers out-rotated position players to the non-throwing side by 4.28° (p = .007). (promising) Lumbar-locked rotation (LLR) in HS pitchers runs ~53–55° per side. (promising)

**Velocity and injury links.** The velocity mechanism is timing, not just magnitude: pro pitchers arrive at front-foot strike with ~55° ± 6° of upper-torso rotation, and it is the *separation* between peak pelvis and peak thorax rotation (and its timing, ideally ~0.05 s or more of lag) that predicts velocity — raw peak rotation velocities alone do not. (promising) A 2024 HS study (n=15) found LLR positively correlated with hip-shoulder separation (r = 0.52) and with delayed trunk rotation, though LLR did not correlate with velocity *directly* — it is an enabler, not a direct cause. (promising) The injury case is stronger and more direct: in 21 HS pitchers (2025), lower throwing-side thoracic rotation correlated with greater trunk contralateral (lateral) flexion (r = −0.51, p = .02) and higher peak elbow valgus torque (r = −0.45, p = .04); the lateral flexion itself tracked elbow torque (r = 0.47). (promising) Translation: a stiff T-spine makes a pitcher "lean out" to find rotation, and that lean taxes the elbow.

**Programming — extension AND rotation, isolated before integrated.** Cressey's key caution: build thoracic *rotation independent of arm movement* first, because if you cue rotation through the arm on an athlete whose T-spine won't move, the arm "drags" and hunts external rotation in the wrong place, irritating the anterior shoulder. His progression: months 1–2 (and in-season) isolate — supine on a doubled tennis ball for segmental flexion, extension over a roller, quadruped rock-back extension-rotation; months 3–4 integrate arm motion — side-lying windmills, bent-over thoracic rotations. (plausible) Limited thoracic *extension* is its own problem: it drives lumbar hyperextension (low-back pain, oblique strain). (plausible)

**For Soto:** Thoracic rotation is a cheap "buy" in the Neptune mobility budget — few downside risks, direct offload of the shoulder/elbow. Screen it with the quadruped lumbar-locked rotation test (50° threshold, visual estimation is reliable), log left vs right, and flag glove-side/throwing-side asymmetry >5°. In Triton, a T-spine restriction flag pairs naturally with a biomech "trunk lateral flexion" or "hip-shoulder separation timing" metric as the downstream symptom to watch.

## 3. Hip ROM — Real Velocity, Real Cost, and Why It's a Dose Curve

Hips are where mobility most directly touches velocity — and where "more is better" is most dangerously wrong.

**Benchmarks.** General adult hip IR norm is ~35–45° (hip flexed 90°). Pro pitchers show a characteristic asymmetry: in 111 Astros pitchers (2008–2010), the stance/trail hip carried more IR (RHP: 32.2° IR / 30.8° ER) and the stride/lead hip more ER (30.8° IR / 36.3° ER); 32% of RHP had >10° more ER on the stride side — an adaptation associated with being older and more experienced, not pathology. (promising) In-delivery, usable lead-hip IR is small: ~16.9° passive in one D1 sample, ~12° at the instant of max shoulder ER. (promising)

**Velocity/kinematics links.** In 7 D1 pitchers, trail-hip total rotational arc correlated with trunk angular velocity onset (r = 0.712) and lead-hip total arc with max shoulder ER (r = 0.695); lead-hip IR correlated almost perfectly with elbow flexion at ball release (r = 0.952) — small sample, so treat the magnitude cautiously but the direction is consistent across studies. (plausible) The cleanest dose-response comes from 99 collegiate pitchers: **+10° of lead-hip IR at max shoulder ER bought +0.6 m/s (~1.3 mph) but added +5 N·m elbow varus moment (~6.7% more elbow load)**, and hip position explained only 20–33% of variance in velocity and stress. (promising) That is the whole story in one line: hip IR is a lever with a cost attached, and it is not the dominant lever.

**The prioritization takeaway.** Chase a *usable range* (~10–20° lead-hip IR at MER) with dynamic control, not a maximal passive number. Restricted hip rotation is genuinely bad — it forces the throwing arm out front and raises medial-elbow valgus load — but so is uncontrolled excess. The velou/Driveline consensus: prioritize dynamic stability and control of the hip over passive ROM. (promising) Note also that hip ROM *degrades over a season* — trail/lead ER, total rotational arc, and hip-abduction strength all drop preseason→postseason — so hip mobility is a maintenance target, not a one-time fix. (promising)

**For Soto:** Neptune's hip screen should capture prone/seated IR and ER *bilaterally* and compute total rotational arc plus L/R asymmetry (flag >5–8°). The programming rule: restriction → mobilize (trail-hip IR especially); adequate-but-uncontrolled → stability/motor-control (90/90 transitions, controlled rotational drills) rather than more stretching. Trevor's own trail-hip IR is worth measuring — a common late-career loss that quietly pushes the arm out front and adds elbow stress.

## 4. Shoulder Mobility Management — The "Do Not Stretch Into External Rotation" Chapter

This is the highest-stakes, most-misunderstood area, and the guidance is nearly unanimous among the credible sources: **do not blindly stretch a throwing shoulder, and specifically do not stretch it into external rotation.**

**Why ER is not a mobility target.** Pitchers naturally develop more dominant-side ER (range 8.3–15.6° more than the non-throwing arm), and much of that is *bony* — humeral retrotorsion, a twist of the humeral shaft laid down during skeletal development from throwing before growth plates close. (promising) It is a structural adaptation, not tight tissue to be stretched. Layered on top is anterior capsular laxity that loosens further across a season. Cressey's mechanism: if you stretch the anterior capsule looser, the biceps tendon has to pick up anterior-stabilizer duty during layback, and internal impingement/anterior instability get worse over time. (promising) His rule of thumb — you are far more likely to get hurt being *too loose* than *too tight*, and the more lax the athlete, the less aggressive any perturbation should be. Broomstick/dowel "dislocates" for throwers are explicitly a bad idea (elbow valgus + anterior stress). (plausible)

**The GIRD / sleeper-stretch reappraisal.** GIRD (≥20° IR loss vs the other arm) was treated for years as a mandatory fix via the sleeper stretch. The modern view: GIRD *in isolation* is not the villain. Reinold hasn't used the sleeper stretch in over a decade and reports no IR-maintenance problems, arguing post-throwing IR loss is *muscular/tendinous* (it appears immediately, faster than a capsule can thicken), not capsular — so aggressive capsular stretching mis-targets the tissue, and the sleeper's impingement-provoking position can do harm. (promising) When IR work is genuinely needed, cross-body horizontal adduction gives equal or better results with less risk, ideally preceded by soft-tissue work on the posterior cuff. (plausible)

**What actually predicts injury.** The prospective pro-baseball data (Wilk/ASMI, 296 pitchers, 505 exams, 2005–2012): >5° **total-rotation deficit** = 2.6× injury risk; ≥5° **flexion deficit** = 2.8× elbow-injury risk; **ER insufficiency** (throwing arm <5° more ER than the other side) = 2.2× shoulder-DL risk (23% of ER-insufficient pitchers hit the DL). (proven) Camp et al. (2017, 132 pitcher-seasons, 2010–2015) confirmed decreased ER and flexion are *stronger* predictors of future elbow injury than IR deficits. (proven) And a 3,314-athlete, 15-study meta-analysis found that absolute ROM and ROM asymmetries are inconsistent risk factors overall, with GIRD ≥20° NOT associated with injury and screening value concentrated specifically in pro baseball pitchers via the ER-insufficiency signal (pooled OR 1.90, 95% CI 1.24–2.92). (proven)

So the shoulder screen should watch **total motion and flexion**, not IR loss in a vacuum, and should never respond to an ER "deficit" by stretching into ER — the correct response to a genuine total-motion loss is targeted soft-tissue and *IR-side* work (cross-body), while the correct response to ER "excess" plus laxity is stability, not stretch.

**Stretch vs strength — a clean RCT.** A 2022 non-inferiority RCT (n=113 HS pitchers, ages 15–17) pitted daily sleeper stretching (5×60 s) against ER isometric strengthening (20×10 s). Injury incidence: **9.8% (strength) vs 22.6% (stretch)**; strengthening was non-inferior for preventing shoulder/elbow injury (10% margin). (promising) The practical read: for maintaining the shoulder, cuff/ER strength earns its place at least as much as stretching — and carries none of the over-mobilization risk.

**For Soto:** Encode the shoulder-ROM logic into Neptune's arm-care monitoring: measure IR, ER, and flexion supine at 90° abduction, compute *total arc* and side-to-side deltas, and alarm on (a) total-rotation loss >5° vs baseline or contralateral, and (b) flexion loss ≥5°. Never program ER stretching off an "ER deficit" alone without confirming a true total-motion loss. Default posterior-shoulder maintenance = cross-body + soft tissue + cuff strength, not sleeper stretch. For Trevor specifically (post-2017 TJ, career of layback), the operating principle is stability-first: assume adequate-to-excess ER and manage laxity, don't chase mobility.

## 5. Ankle Dorsiflexion — A Shock-Absorber Qualifier, Not a Velocity Lever

Ankle dorsiflexion (DF) gets over-hyped because the lead-leg block is a genuine velocity driver — but the data separate the two.

**What the block actually needs.** Across 800+ Driveline force-plate sessions, lead-leg **vertical GRF** was the best single-component predictor of velocity (r = 0.44 raw, 0.23 weight-controlled), anterior-posterior GRF next (r = 0.38 / 0.19), and **knee extension** from foot-plant to release correlated with velocity (r ≈ 0.29) — high performers extended the lead knee 34–36° while low performers managed only 5–7°. (promising) Ankle DF at foot-plant, by contrast, showed *no notable correlation* with velocity or with the composite block score. (promising) Independent work echoes it: stride-leg GRF predicts velocity (r = 0.45–0.61 in one sample), and lead-knee extension raises both velocity and elbow varus torque — the block is a knee/hip/GRF phenomenon.

**So why screen the ankle at all?** Because DF is a *permission* variable, not a *performance* variable. The ankle must absorb and redirect very high landing GRFs; inadequate DF forces compensation (early heel rise, knee valgus, a lost block, or upstream leaks), and fatigue-induced stride-length changes measurably alter ankle biomechanics. The joint-by-joint logic holds: the ankle wants mobility so the knee can stay stable. Screen with the knee-to-wall (weight-bearing lunge) test: ~1 cm ≈ 3.6° DF, restricted is <10 cm, and a common athletic target is ~5 in / ~12–13 cm of toe-to-wall clearance. (plausible) Below the floor, mobilize; above it, ankle *strength/stability* to control the block is the priority — you need both.

**For Soto:** Treat ankle DF as a pass/fail gate in the Neptune movement screen (knee-to-wall, both sides, flag asymmetry), not as a velocity KPI. If a pitcher fails the block on force plates, check knee extension and hip contribution *before* blaming the ankle — the data say the ankle is rarely the actual bottleneck. This is a good example of a metric to *not* over-index on in the Compete/facility dashboards.

## 6. Screening-to-Programming Logic — Turning Numbers Into Decisions

A screen is only useful if it changes a program. The decision tree:

1. **Measure bilaterally and store a baseline.** Every mobility metric is most informative as (a) a within-athlete change over time and (b) a left/right or dominant/non-dominant delta — because so much of a thrower's "abnormal" ROM is benign sport adaptation. Absolute numbers vs textbook norms are the *weakest* signal (the meta-analysis makes this explicit). (proven)
2. **Classify each finding into one of three buckets:**
   - **Benign adaptation** (e.g., dominant-side ER excess from retrotorsion, stride-hip ER asymmetry in an experienced pitcher) → leave alone, monitor.
   - **Mobility restriction** (e.g., T-spine rotation <50°, trail-hip IR loss, knee-to-wall <10 cm) → targeted regional mobility, re-test.
   - **Stability/laxity deficit** (hypermobile shoulder, uncontrolled hip IR) → motor control and strength, NOT stretching. (plausible)
3. **Flag the validated injury signals distinctly.** Total-shoulder-rotation deficit >5° and flexion deficit ≥5° are load-management/medical flags, not "stretch harder" cues. (proven)
4. **Program the smallest effective input and re-test.** Mobility is a targeted intervention with a dose, an owner joint, and a re-assessment — not a blanket team warm-up.

**Asymmetry thresholds worth encoding:** hip L/R rotation delta >5–8°; shoulder total-arc change >5°; thoracic rotation L/R >5°; humeral-retrotorsion limb difference — injured pitchers averaged ~7.2° more than uninjured (context, not a live screen). (promising)

**For Soto:** This is a productizable spine for Neptune: intake screen → per-joint classification (adapt / mobilize / stabilize) → prescribed drills with re-test dates → Triton logs the longitudinal deltas and raises flags when total shoulder motion or flexion drops past threshold. The differentiator vs a commodity cage is exactly this closed loop of assessment → individualized programming → monitoring, which supports the 3–10× price point in the facility research.

## 7. Mobility vs Stability Prioritization — The Governing Principle

When mobility and stability compete for the same joint, throwers should usually bias toward **stability/control**, for three reasons grounded in the evidence above:

- **Downside asymmetry.** Loose beats tight for velocity, but tight beats loose for durability. Cressey's clinical axiom — you're more likely to get hurt too loose than too tight — is consistent with the shoulder data where excess ER + anterior laxity, not restriction, drive the impingement/instability failure mode. (promising)
- **The velocity levers that matter most are control-dependent.** Hip-shoulder separation *timing*, lead-leg block/knee extension, and trunk sequencing are motor-control and force-application skills sitting on top of *adequate* (not maximal) ROM. Passive range explains a minority of velocity variance (hip ROM 20–33%). (promising) Past "adequate," the return on more passive ROM falls off while injury cost can rise (the hip IR dose curve).
- **Adaptations shouldn't be "corrected."** Much of a thrower's distinctive ROM profile is protective adaptation; treating it as dysfunction and stretching it away can remove a healthy buffer.

The exceptions — where mobility *is* the priority — are the low-risk, high-transfer buckets: **thoracic rotation/extension** (almost pure upside) and **restricted hip IR / ankle DF below the functional floor** (restore to adequate, then stop). Everywhere else, once a joint clears its adequacy threshold, the next dollar goes to stability and motor control, not more range. (plausible)

**For Soto:** Bake the bias into defaults. Neptune's mobility block should be short, regional, and gated by screen findings; the volume budget should tilt toward cuff/scap stability, trunk control, and lead-leg/hip control. In Triton metric design, resist building a "flexibility score" that rewards maximal ROM — reward *adequacy + symmetry + control* instead, and reserve alarms for the two proven injury signals (total-motion and flexion loss). For Trevor: stability-first is the correct personal default given laxity accrued over a long career plus a reconstructed elbow — mobility work stays surgical (trail hip, T-spine) and everything else is control and strength maintenance.

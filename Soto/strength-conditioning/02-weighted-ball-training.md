# Weighted Ball Training — Overload/Underload Theory, Evidence, and Honest Risk-Benefit

## TL;DR

- **Weighted balls reliably add velocity, and reliably add risk.** Across the two systematic reviews (Caldwell 2019, 10 studies; Melugin/Camp 2021, 10 studies), 7 of 10 studies showed gains of ~2–11 mph, but the only randomized controlled trial (Reinold 2018, n=38) posted a **24% injury rate in the trained group vs. 0% in controls** — including 2 olecranon stress fractures, a partial UCL tear, and one full UCL tear that needed surgery. (proven that it adds velo; promising-but-alarming on risk)
- **The velocity gain is real but modest and heterogeneous.** Reinold's 6-week RCT found ~2.2 mph (≈3.3%); DeRenne 1994 found ~5 mph over 10 weeks; Driveline's own overload/underload split found **underload ~3 mph vs. overload ~1.5 mph** — nearly double for the *lighter* implements. (proven)
- **Ball weight and elbow torque move in the opposite direction most people assume.** Fleisig's 2025 study (n=20 college/pro) showed elbow varus torque *decreased* 1.3–4.0% as ball weight/size went up; Okoroha 2019 (youth) found torque *rose* 0.92 N·m per +1 oz. The reconciliation: heavier balls slow the arm, so per-throw torque drops, but the youth arm is a different, more fragile system. (proven)
- **The stress is not in the heavy ball — it's in the max-intent throw.** Pulldowns ("run-n-guns") measured only ~1.5 N·m (elbow) and ~1.4 N·m (shoulder) *higher* torque than mound pitching (ASMI + Driveline, ~25 pitchers each), statistically nonsignificant, despite being ~5.5–7.8 mph faster. The danger is cumulative high-intent volume, not the implement per se. (proven)
- **Overload balls stretch the shoulder into more external rotation; underload balls don't.** Reinold 2018 measured a **+4.3° shoulder ER** shift after 6 weeks; his 2020 acute study showed **+3.3° ER from overload and +8.4° from extreme overload, ~0° from underload**. Gained ER is a known injury correlate — the mechanism connecting weighted balls to blowouts. (proven)
- **Professional field data confirms the risk signal.** A 2025 MiLB study (n=88) found weighted-ball users had **39.1% injury prevalence vs. 23.8%**, arm injuries 11.8 vs. 7.5 per 1000 game-exposures, and **44.4 vs. 23.2 days lost** — though underpowered and confounded (heavier throwers self-select into weighted balls). (promising)
- **Age is the hard gate: growth-plate maturity, roughly 15–16+.** Consensus practitioner guidance is no overhead throws with anything over ~9 oz for developing arms, and no aggressive overload programs until skeletal maturity. The 24% injury cohort in Reinold was *high schoolers*. (plausible/consensus)
- **The evidence base is genuinely weak.** Caldwell 2019 rated the overall body of evidence "very poor" by GRADE; protocols vary wildly (4–32 oz, 4–16 weeks), and there is essentially no long-term or well-powered safety data. Treat strong claims in either direction skeptically. (proven — this is a meta-observation)

## 1. The Theory: Why Anyone Throws a 2-lb Ball

Weighted ball training rests on a **speed-strength / power-training** logic borrowed from track and field (shot put, overweight/underweight javelins) and applied to the throwing arm. The arm is a kinetic-chain terminal link that must produce enormous force in a vanishingly short window — internal rotation velocities near release exceed **7,000 °/s**, among the fastest motions the human body produces. The premise: you can train the neuromuscular system to develop more force in less time by loading the movement on both ends of the force-velocity curve. (plausible — the F-V curve logic is sound; transfer to the mound is the contested part)

**Overload (heavier than 5 oz):** the arm moves slower but with greater muscular force output. Proponents use it for arm-path/timing work, strengthening, and eccentric deceleration capacity ("recovery"). Driveline's framing: overload conditions greater force production. (plausible)

**Underload (lighter than 5 oz):** the arm moves *faster* than it can with a regulation ball, teaching the nervous system to tolerate and organize higher arm speeds. Driveline's internal overload-vs-underload comparison found **underload produced ~2× the velocity gain of overload (≈3 mph vs. ≈1.5 mph)**, consistent with the idea that raw arm-speed exposure is the more potent velocity stimulus. (promising)

The honest mechanistic summary from the biomechanics literature: **the primary way weighted balls raise mound velocity is by raising arm speed (shoulder internal rotation and elbow extension velocity), and the primary way they raise injury risk is by increasing shoulder external rotation range of motion and the total dose of high-intent throwing.** Those two effects are hard to fully separate — the thing that makes you throw harder is metabolically the same thing that stresses the UCL. (proven, per Melugin/Camp 2021 synthesis)

**For Soto:** This is the load-bearing framing for Neptune's velo product and for any Triton "development" narrative. Weighted balls are not magic and not poison — they are a **dose-response power stimulus with a genuine injury tail.** Sell them as programmed medicine, not as a bucket of balls in the corner of the cage.

## 2. What the Velocity Studies Actually Show

Two systematic reviews anchor the efficacy question, and they reviewed overlapping literature:

- **Caldwell, Alexander & Ahmad (Columbia), 2019, *Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine*** — screened 4,119 titles, included 10 studies. Velocity improvements ranged ~**2 to 11+ mph**; 7 of 10 studies reported benefit. Ball weights across studies spanned **4–32 oz**. Crucially, they graded the overall evidence **"very poor" by GRADE**, flagging selection, performance, and reporting bias, and declined to recommend for or against. (proven that the review exists; its own verdict is "insufficient evidence")

- **Melugin, Smart, Verhoeven, Dines & Camp, 2021, *Current Reviews in Musculoskeletal Medicine*** — 10 studies; 7 of 10 significant velocity gains, range 2–11 mph. Notable individual results:
  - **Reinold 2018 (RCT):** +2.2 mph over 6 weeks
  - **Yang 2013:** +2.1 mph over 10 weeks
  - **DeRenne 1994:** +5 mph over 10 weeks
  - **Logan 1966:** +9.28 mph over 4 weeks (a pulley-based outlier, weak methodology)

The single best-designed study is **Reinold et al. 2018 (*Sports Health*)** — a randomized controlled trial, n=38 healthy pitchers, 6-week offseason program. It found a **~3.3% velocity increase (~2.2 mph)** — real, statistically significant, but modest. (proven)

Contrast **Marsh et al. 2018 (*PeerJ*)**, n=17 college/pro pitchers training at Driveline for 6 weeks: **no significant change in group mean velocity.** But it split by responders — 9 pitchers gained velocity and showed increased internal-rotation and elbow-extension velocity; 8 did not and showed no kinematic change. This is the key nuance: **weighted-ball response is highly individual.** Group means understate both the winners and the non-responders. (promising)

**For Soto → Triton/Compete:** This is exactly the case for facility-level tracking. Neptune should not report "our weighted-ball program adds X mph" as a population claim — it should track **per-athlete responder status** in the Compete pipeline (pre/post pulldown and mound velo, TrackMan-verified), because ~40–50% may be non-responders in any given block. A `compete_pitches`-backed responder dashboard is a real differentiator and an honest one.

## 3. The Biomechanics of Ball Weight — Torque Goes Down as Weight Goes Up (in adults)

This is the most counterintuitive and most important body of evidence.

**Fleisig et al. 2017 (*Sports Health*, "Biomechanical Analysis of Weighted-Ball Exercises")** — as ball mass increased from 5 to 32 oz, arm and trunk angular velocities *steadily decreased*, and most arm torques/forces *decreased*. The exception: **elbow flexion torque was significantly greater for 14- and 32-oz flat-ground holds/throws.** A slightly underweight ball (4 oz) produced essentially the same kinetics as a regulation ball. The caution: **flat-ground max-effort throwing increased shoulder internal rotation velocity and elbow varus torque** relative to the mound — so the *throw type* matters more than the *ball*. (proven)

**Fleisig et al. 2025 (*Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine*, "Increases in Ball Weight and Size Decrease Elbow Varus Torque")** — n=20 collegiate (D-I) and AA pro pitchers, 4 ball types crossing 2 weights (5 vs. 6 oz) × 2 sizes (standard vs. +5% circumference), Driveline Command Training Balls, indoor mound, 7 full-effort fastballs per condition. Results:
  - Elbow varus torque *decreased*: **−1.3%** (6 oz), **−2.2%** (+5% size), **−4.0%** (both), vs. regulation.
  - Maximal elbow varus torque: 99.5 N·m (5 oz) → 98.2 (6 oz) → 95.6 (6 oz + larger).
  - Shoulder internal rotation velocity, elbow extension velocity, and ball velocity all decreased with heavier/larger balls.
  - Practical read: the authors even float **larger/heavier regulation balls as an MLB-level elbow-injury-reduction lever.** (proven)

Now the pediatric counterpoint:

**Okoroha et al. 2019 (*Journal of Shoulder & Elbow Surgery*)** — youth pitchers, 4 weights (3, 4, 5, 6 oz), Motus sensor. **Medial elbow torque increased ~0.92 N·m, arm speed dropped ~8.52 rpm, and velocity fell ~2.0 mph per +1 oz.** In youth, heavier balls *raised* torque. (proven)

**Reconciling the two:** In mature, high-velocity arms, adding weight slows the whole kinetic chain enough that per-throw torque drops. In youth arms — lower baseline kinetics, open growth plates, less able to "gear down" gracefully — the added mass shows up as added elbow load. **The same intervention has opposite kinetic signatures depending on the athlete's maturity and force ceiling.** This is why age is a hard gate (§6), not a soft preference. (plausible synthesis — mechanistically consistent, not directly tested head-to-head)

**For Soto:** The "heavy ball = high torque" intuition is wrong for adults but right for kids. Any Neptune coach or Triton content that repeats the simple version will be wrong in one of the two populations. Bake the maturity split into programming logic.

## 4. Pulldowns vs. Mound vs. Constraint Throws

**Pulldowns / run-n-guns:** a crow-hop or shuffle-step throw into a net at maximum intent, typically with 3–7 oz balls. They are the closest thing to a true max-effort throw and are used to expose the athlete to peak arm speed and to develop explosive movement.

Two independent motion-capture studies converged:
- **ASMI:** 25 pitchers (18 HS, 7 college). Pulldowns measured **+1.5 N·m elbow torque, +1.4 N·m shoulder torque** vs. mound, statistically **nonsignificant**, despite pulldowns being **7.8 mph faster**.
- **Driveline (Motus sensor):** 25 pitchers (12 gap-year college, 13 pro). Pulldowns **+5.5 mph** vs. mound; **R² = 0.66** between pulldown and mound velocity; roughly split on whether peak stress was higher in pulldowns (16 pitchers) or on the mound (9). (both proven within their methods, small samples)

**Takeaway:** pulldowns are *not dramatically* more stressful per throw than the mound. The correlation to mound velo is strong-ish (R² ≈ 0.66–0.7) but imperfect — a big pulldown number does not guarantee a big mound number, and chasing the pulldown radar reading is a known failure mode. (proven within cited samples; generalizability limited)

**Constraint throws** (kneeling, rocker, pivot-pickoff, roll-in, walking windup — the classic Driveline plyo positions) isolate segments of the delivery, reduce lower-half contribution, and lower peak intent. They are the *lower-stress* end of the menu and the appropriate entry point, especially for younger or on-ramping athletes. Reinold's 2020 acute study used **kneeling, rocker, and run-and-gun positions** precisely because they span a stress gradient. (plausible — widely used, minimal direct kinetic comparison published)

**For Soto → Neptune facility design:** The stress gradient — kneeling/rocker (low) → hybrid flat-ground (medium) → shuffle/run-n-gun pulldowns (high) → mound at intent (competition) — *is* the periodization spine. Neptune's net setup, TrackMan placement, and session templates should physically encode this ramp. Log pulldown velo and mound velo separately in Compete so the R²-to-mound relationship is observable per athlete rather than assumed.

## 5. Protocols by Phase — On-Ramp, Intent, Blend to Mound

The modern (Driveline/PRP-style) structure, drawn from their published program docs:

1. **On-ramp (3–5 weeks):** progressive throwing build-up to prepare tissue for high intent. Volume and intent both low-to-moderate. Length scales with age, time of year, and training age. This phase is non-negotiable and is where most self-directed athletes cut corners and get hurt. (plausible/consensus)
2. **Assessment gate (weeks 4–5):** 80–90% shuffle pulldowns to gauge readiness; the goal is to **reach prior mound-velo PRs in shuffle pulldowns before clearing the on-ramp.** (plausible)
3. **Intent / velocity phase:** high-intent pulldown days (often 1×/week in offseason per Driveline) interspersed with lighter "hybrid" days. **Hybrid B days run ~60–70% intent** for arm-path and mechanics feel; heavy days push max intent.
4. **Blend to mound:** graduate flat-ground intent into mound intent ("Mound Blend" days). **Shuffle-step pulldowns are used as the bridge** because their velocity sits closer to mound velo than standard run-n-guns.
5. **In-season maintenance:** reduced volume/intent; overload often shifts toward recovery/arm-care use rather than velocity chasing.

Weight selection within a session commonly runs a descending or mixed ladder through the plyo set (see §7), pairing overload for strength/arm-path and underload for arm-speed. Reassessment of passive/active ROM **every ~4 weeks** to titrate dosage is standard best practice — because gained external rotation (§8) is the early warning sign. (plausible/consensus)

**Rep/volume anchors from the literature:** Reinold's protocol and acute study used **~27 throws per session** across positions (3 throws × each ball × 3 positions); DeRenne-style programs ran 10 weeks; commercial programs run **8-week on-ramps and 16-week in/off-season blocks.** There is no single validated volume — this is where coach judgment and monitoring, not a PDF, carry the load. (plausible)

**For Soto → Trevor:** For a 36-year-old post-TJ arm (2017 UCL reconstruction) training for sharpness/demos rather than a 100-mph comeback, the useful pieces are the **overload recovery/arm-care menu and low-intent constraint work**, not aggressive underload pulldown chasing. The on-ramp discipline matters *more*, not less, with age and surgical history. If Trevor wants to touch pulldowns for content, gate them behind a real 3–5 week on-ramp and cap frequency — the injury tail is volume-of-intent driven, and a reconstructed ligament plus older tissue is exactly the profile that lands in the 24%.

## 6. Contraindications and the Age Gate

**Skeletal maturity is the primary gate.** Practitioner consensus (ABCA, RPP, PBPTS reviews): athletes should be **~15–16+ and skeletally mature** before aggressive overload programs, and **no fully overhead throws with implements heavier than ~9 oz** for developing arms. There is "no magic age" — growth-plate closure varies — so assessment beats a birthday. (plausible/consensus)

The evidence for caution in the young is concrete: **Reinold's 24%-injury cohort was high schoolers**, and Okoroha showed youth elbow torque *rising* with ball weight. At ~12 years old, bones/tendons/ligaments are more pliable and the physis (growth plate) is the failure point — hence olecranon stress fractures and apophyseal injuries rather than clean UCL tears.

**Other contraindications / red flags:**
- Active or recent shoulder/elbow symptoms; recent surgery (Fleisig 2025 excluded surgery within 12 months).
- Poor foundational mechanics or inadequate strength base — "kinetic chain foundation before velocity" is the repeated review recommendation.
- Rapidly gaining shoulder external ROM without matching internal ROM (GIRD-type asymmetry) — a monitoring stop signal.
- In-season starters with high existing competitive throwing load (dose stacking).
- Inability or unwillingness to complete a proper on-ramp.

**For Soto → Neptune intake:** If Neptune serves youth/travel/HS (the assumed core clientele), the **default for pre-mature athletes should be low-intent constraint/plyo and command work, not overload velocity programs.** Build the intake assessment to record skeletal-maturity proxies (age, growth history, PROM ER/IR both arms) and hard-code an eligibility gate before any athlete is prescribed overload/pulldown blocks. This is both a safety and a liability posture.

## 8. The Mechanism of Harm — Gained External Rotation

The through-line connecting weighted balls to blowouts is **shoulder external rotation (ER) range of motion**, a known independent injury predictor.

- **Reinold 2018 (RCT):** the 6-week overload program produced a **+4.3° gain in passive shoulder ER** — alongside the 24% injury rate. (proven)
- **Reinold 2020 (acute study):** after a single session, **overload balls added +3.3° ER (P=0.05) and extreme overload +8.4° ER (P<0.001); underload balls produced ~0° change**, and internal rotation was unchanged across the board. (proven)
- **Marsh 2018:** found no significant ER change but significant **internal rotation and total ROM gains** and altered release-point kinematics (less shoulder ER at release, higher shoulder adduction torque). (promising — different program, different result, underscoring heterogeneity)

The clinical logic: acute and chronic ER gains reflect capsular/tissue laxity adaptation at the shoulder that has been associated with injury risk in throwers. **Overload is the ER-driver; underload largely is not.** This is the cleanest actionable finding in the whole literature — it gives you a *monitorable biomarker* (PROM ER, both arms, every ~4 weeks) that flags when a program is trending toward the danger zone before a structural injury occurs. (plausible/proven — the ER-injury link is established; the weighted-ball→ER link is measured; the full causal chain to UCL tears is inferred)

**Field-level confirmation — 2025 MiLB study (n=88):**
- Injury prevalence: **39.1% (WBT) vs. 23.8% (non-WBT).**
- Arm injuries: **11.8 vs. 7.5 per 1000 game-exposures**; core/trunk **5.9 vs. 2.1**.
- Days lost: **44.4 ± 41.8 vs. 23.2 ± 24.3.**
- Elbow injuries: 8 (WBT) vs. 3; the "most surprising" signal was **core/trunk injuries** (6 vs. 2).
- **Caveats that matter:** underpowered, overlapping confidence intervals, and — critically — **self-selection** (46 of 46 WBT pitchers were velocity-chasers using 4–9 oz overload in-season and offseason; harder-throwing, higher-effort pitchers gravitate to weighted balls, so some of the injury gap is the athlete, not the ball). (promising, not proven)

**For Soto → Triton monitoring product:** ER PROM asymmetry is the single most defensible arm-care biomarker to operationalize. A Neptune/Compete workflow that logs bilateral shoulder ER/IR at intake and every 4-week reassessment, flags a threshold ER gain, and ties it to the athlete's weighted-ball dose is directly grounded in Reinold 2018/2020. This is a buildable, evidence-graded feature — not bro-science — and it's the kind of thing that separates a "development lab" from a cage barn.

## 9. Honest Risk-Benefit Synthesis

**What we can say with confidence (proven):**
- Weighted balls add velocity for most (not all) trained athletes — modest in the best study (~2.2 mph / 3.3%), larger in weaker studies.
- In *mature* arms, heavier balls *reduce* per-throw elbow torque; in *youth* arms they *raise* it.
- Overload balls increase shoulder ER; underload balls largely don't.
- Pulldowns are only marginally more stressful per throw than mound pitching.

**What is suggestive but unproven (promising/plausible):**
- Weighted-ball programs raise injury *incidence* (RCT + pro field data both point this way, but the RCT is one small study of high schoolers and the pro data is underpowered and confounded).
- Underload is a safer, possibly more effective velocity lever than overload.
- Proper on-ramping, ER monitoring, and age-gating meaningfully blunt the risk.

**What is essentially unknown:**
- Long-term (multi-year) safety and durability.
- Optimal weight/volume/frequency — the whole field varies from 4 to 32 oz and 4 to 16 weeks with no consensus.
- Whether the velocity that weighted balls add carries injury risk *beyond* the velocity itself — i.e., is it the ball or just throwing harder? (Fleisig 2025's torque-reduction finding suggests much of the risk may simply be the intent/velocity, not the implement.)

**The defensible operating position:** Weighted balls are a legitimate, effective velocity tool with a real and under-characterized injury tail. Use them **programmed, monitored, age-gated, and underload-biased**, never as an unsupervised max-intent free-for-all. The 24% number should haunt every program design decision — and the honest counter is that it came from unmonitored high schoolers, which is precisely the population and the negligence a good facility exists to prevent. (This synthesis is the intended evidence-graded stance, not a cited claim.)

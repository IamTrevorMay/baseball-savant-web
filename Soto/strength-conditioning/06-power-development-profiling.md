# Power Development and Profiling for Baseball — Med Ball, Jumps, Force Plates, and VBT

## TL;DR

- **Rotational med ball throw velocity is the single best cheap proxy for baseball power.** In NCAA DIII players (n=35), rotational med-ball throw velocity correlated with bat-swing velocity (r=0.65, p=0.003, ~39% variance), pitching velocity (r=0.62, p=0.02, ~38%), and batted-ball velocity (r=0.53, p=0.02); competing "whole-body" tests (lateral-to-medial jump, broad jump) showed *no* significant relationships. (proven)
- **Add rotational med-ball work on top of a lifting program and rotational strength jumps ~2-3x.** Szymanski's 12-week HS study (n=49) showed the med-ball group beat the control on torso rotational strength (+17.1% vs +10.5% dominant; +18.3% vs +10.2% non-dominant) and the med-ball "hitter's throw" (+10.6% vs +3.0%). (proven)
- **Vertical jump *height* barely separates baseball levels — power output does.** MLB, AAA, AA, A, and rookie players jump nearly identically (~69-72 cm), but peak power tracks body mass: Rangers MLB ~11,542 W vs A-ball ~10,823 W. Jump height alone is a weak talent filter; power and force-time strategy matter. (proven)
- **Driveline's practical force-plate benchmarks: IMTP net peak force >3000 N and relative strength >3× bodyweight** for competitive athletes, with knee angle 125° / hip 150°, 2 familiarization + 3 max trials. But IMTP-to-velocity correlation is moderate at best. (promising)
- **Frontal-plane power is throwing-specific.** Lehman's lower-body field-test work found lateral-to-medial jump (the leg on the throwing-arm side) consistently predicted velocity; it explained ~32% of RH and ~69% of LH throwing-velocity variance in a stretch position, while vertical jump, broad jump, and drop jumps did not. (promising)
- **Proteus 3D rotational testing correlates with velocity and is trainable.** In 14 collegiate pitchers over 6 weeks, dominant-side acceleration correlated with pitch velocity (R=0.76, p=0.003); General Power Test scores rose ~25% and fastballs went 84→87 mph on average. (plausible — small n, no control)
- **Force-velocity "optimal profile" training is oversold.** Early Jiménez-Reyes work claimed 7.2-14.2% jump gains from FVimb-matched training, but 2023-2025 meta-analyses find optimized F-V training produces jump gains "comparable to non-optimized training" and small/trivial power effects; the profile is even task-dependent. (debunked as a magic bullet; individualization by force-vs-velocity deficit still plausible)
- **VBT gives you objective load-velocity zones.** Driveline's working ranges: starting strength >1.3 m/s (≤30% 1RM), speed-strength 1.0-1.3 m/s (30-40%), strength-speed 0.75-1.0 m/s (50-60%), power 0.3-0.6 m/s (75-95%), max strength 0.5-0.75 m/s (70-90%). (promising)
- **Weighted-implement / specific strength work raises throwing velocity modestly, ~1-4% typical (range 2.8-26.1% in reviews), with mixed transfer.** A 17-study systematic review found no single method superior; resistance-band work was the most reliable, especially for developing arms. (promising)

## 1. Why "power" is the right training target for baseball

Baseball is a series of maximal-intent, short-duration, rotational ballistic actions: a pitch delivery lasts <150 ms from front-foot contact to release, a swing is even shorter. Peak power (force × velocity), not maximal strength alone, is the quality that most directly underlies bat speed and pitch velocity. The core practical problem is that **strength is necessary but not sufficient** — an athlete can be very strong and slow, or twitchy but weak, and both leave velocity on the table. That is the entire rationale for *profiling* (finding where an athlete sits on the force-velocity continuum) before *developing* (training the deficient quality).

Two anchoring facts frame everything below. First, at the MLB level, **bat speed averages ~71.5 mph, a "fast swing" is ≥75 mph (only ~23% of swings), and average swing length is 7.3 ft** (Statcast bat tracking, 2024). Every 1 mph of bat speed adds roughly 6 ft of fly-ball distance, and swings ≥75 mph hit .307/.603 slug vs .246/.389 below it. (proven) Second, pitch velocity is heavily lower-body and trunk driven: stride-leg ground reaction forces explain up to ~61% of the variance in peak wrist velocity, and back-leg force toward the plate predicts wrist/ball velocity (MacWilliams 1998). (proven) Power development is therefore not accessory work — it is central to both offensive and pitching output.

**For Soto:** This is the conceptual bridge to Triton. Stuff+ rewards velocity and movement, both of which are downstream of the power qualities in this doc. A Neptune athlete's Proteus/med-ball/force-plate profile is the *upstream* input; the TrackMan/Statcast output (velocity, spin, movement → Stuff+) is the outcome. The facility's differentiation is closing that loop: profile → prescribe → re-test → show the velocity delta in the athlete's Compete dashboard.

## 2. Medicine ball training for rotational power

Rotational med-ball throws are the workhorse of baseball power development because they reproduce the proximal-to-distal (pelvis → torso → arm) sequencing of both the swing and the delivery under overload, in the transverse plane where the sport lives. (plausible mechanistically; proven as a correlate)

**The evidence for transfer is real but modest.** Szymanski's randomized 12-week study of 49 HS players (age 15.4 ± 1.2) is the cleanest. Both groups lifted on a periodized model and took 100 game-bat swings/day, 3x/week; group 2 added rotational and full-body med-ball throws 3x/week. Group 2 beat group 1 on dominant torso rotational strength (+17.1% vs +10.5%), non-dominant (+18.3% vs +10.2%), and the sequential hip-torso-arm "hitter's throw" (+10.6% vs +3.0%), all p≤0.05. (proven) A separate 12-week program reported bat exit velocity gains of 3.6-6.4%, hit velocity 2.6-3.6%, and hitter's-throw 3.0-10.6%. (promising)

**For throwing velocity specifically, the effect is smaller and noisier.** A systematic review of "specific strength training" (17 studies) found overweight/underweight ball work raised throwing velocity 2.8-24.2% (6 of 9 studies significant within-group, only 3 beating controls); Newton & McEvoy's 8-week med-ball throws produced +4.1%; resistance bands were actually the most reliable modality (3.7-26.1%, best for junior/less-experienced arms). The blunt conclusion: "no clear conclusion can be made on which method is best," and you need ≥6 weeks of adequate load. (proven that transfer is real; the magnitude and best modality are unsettled)

**The test doubles as the best low-cost profiling tool.** Rotational med-ball throw *velocity* (measured with a radar/Rapsodo or a Proteus/GymAware) out-predicts jump tests for baseball skill. In the DIII study (n=35), it was the *only* whole-body test to correlate with all three of bat-swing, batted-ball, and pitch velocity (r=0.53-0.65). (proven) Broader literature reports r=0.60-0.85 between rotational med-ball throw performance and bat speed / throwing velocity. (promising)

**Normative distances (protocol- and ball-weight-dependent — always standardize):**
- Standing rotational throw, 3 kg: adult males ~9-11 m ("good"), females ~7-8.5 m; DIII football ~7.78 m; young-adult male general ~7.71 m. Elite baseball players often exceed 12 m with a 3 kg ball. (promising)
- Seated med-ball throw (upper-body power only, removes legs): college males ~4.1 ± 0.5 m; adolescents 4.3 ± 0.7 m (12-13), 5.2 ± 0.8 m (14-15). In a youth pitching study, seated throw rose 2.51 m/s (11U) → 5.54 m/s (21U) and correlated with velocity at 13U (τ=0.483). (proven as age-graded norms)
- Ball weight convention: 2 kg youth/beginner, 3 kg standard adult testing, 4-5 kg trained/elite. Heavier balls test strength; lighter/velocity-measured throws test power.

**For Soto / Neptune:** Make the **standing rotational throw (3 kg, radar-measured velocity) a core intake test** — it is cheap, reliable, baseball-specific, and the best single power correlate to bat/pitch velocity. Log throw velocity per session into `compete_pitches`-style athlete records so you can trend it against measured velocity. Standardize weight, stance, and measurement method rigidly or the norms are useless.

## 3. Jump training and jump testing

Jumps split into two roles: **training stimulus** (plyometrics build reactive strength / RFD) and **assessment** (jumps profile power and neuromuscular readiness). Treat them separately.

**As training**, plyometrics reliably improve general power qualities. 2024-2025 meta-analyses show plyometric training yields medium effects on jump and sprint performance and large effects on change-of-direction, with youth also gaining reactive strength index and sport-specific skills (e.g., kicking velocity). (proven — general) But baseball-specific transfer to pitch velocity/bat speed is *not* well established by RCT; the direct-transfer literature is thin. (plausible)

**The plane matters more than the height.** Cressey's synthesis of the research is that **power is plane-specific**: traditional sagittal-plane tests (vertical jump, broad jump, 60-yd dash) show weak or no correlation to throwing velocity (Spaniol 1997 found none), while the **lateral-to-medial (frontal-plane) jump** — which mimics the pitching stride and back-leg push — consistently predicts velocity. Lehman/Drinkwater/Behm reported the lateral-to-medial jump on the throwing-arm-side leg was "4 for 4" across groups; in a stretch position it plus bodyweight explained ~32.2% of RH and ~68.8% of LH throwing-velocity variance. (promising) Practical translation: **add band-resisted lateral jumps, lateral bounds, and lateral sled drags; de-emphasize drop jumps and hang cleans for velocity transfer; train single-leg, eccentric front-leg and concentric back-leg strength.** (promising)

**As assessment**, jump *height* is a poor level-discriminator in pro baseball. Across MLB/AAA/AA/A/rookie, vertical jump height clusters at ~69-72 cm with no significant between-level difference; MLB=71.9±8.2, AAA=71.1±8.4, AA=69.1±7.1, A=70.1±7.1, rookie=70.1±7.6 cm. What separates levels is **peak power via body mass** (MLB ~101 kg → ~11,542 W vs A-ball ~92 kg → ~10,823 W). (proven) The MLB Draft Combine confirms jumps are collected but sparsely reported: 2023 average CMJ ~19.4 in and broad jump ~107.1 in; 2024 top marks included a 32-in CMJ (Cissell) and 123-in broad jump (Obermuller). (proven)

**For Soto:** Do not gatekeep or rank athletes on jump *height*. Use jumps for (a) frontal-plane power via lateral-to-medial jump distance, and (b) force-plate CMJ profiling/readiness (next section). If Neptune buys force plates, the CMJ becomes a daily fatigue check, not a talent test.

## 4. Force plate metrics and benchmarks (CMJ, IMTP)

Force plates turn a jump or an isometric pull into a force-time curve, from which you derive dozens of metrics. Organize them into three buckets (Premier/practitioner framework):
- **Outcome metrics:** jump height, peak power, net impulse — *what happened*.
- **Driver metrics:** concentric peak force, concentric peak velocity, eccentric peak force, modified reactive strength index (mRSI) — *what produced it*.
- **Strategy metrics:** countermovement depth, eccentric duration, total contraction time — *how it was organized*.

The key interpretive principle: **jump height is not dictated by one variable** — two athletes hit the same height with very different force-time strategies, so you program off the drivers/strategy, not the outcome. (proven)

**CMJ (countermovement jump):** the daily-monitoring gold standard. Reductions in eccentric braking force/impulse flag neuromuscular fatigue; rising concentric velocity/power reflect positive adaptation. Weekly (or per-outing) testing distinguishes acute fatigue from chronic change. In pro baseball, a Liberty University dissertation tracked peak power, concentric impulse, mRSI, and force-at-zero-velocity (FV0) across a season against hitting variability — confirming CMJ metrics move with neuromuscular status. (promising) A collegiate-pitcher CMJ study found braking force, peak force, braking RFD, braking/propulsive impulse, peak power, rate of power development, and leg stiffness all positively correlated with ball velocity, and absolute braking force + body height together explained 43.3% of velocity variance. (promising)

**IMTP (isometric mid-thigh pull):** the maximal-strength / RFD test. Driveline's protocol and benchmarks:
- Setup: knee 125°, hip 150°, bar locked at mid-thigh. 2 familiarization trials (75%, then 90% RPE), 3 max trials, +1 if a trial beats prior by ≥250 N.
- **Net peak force (NPF) = peak force − bodyweight; target >3000 N for competitive athletes.**
- **Relative strength = NPF ÷ bodyweight; target >3× bodyweight.** (Some frameworks cite ≥3× BW peak force as a strong-athlete line; soccer athletes tend to out-produce baseball on relative peak force.)
- Force at 100/150/200/250 ms captures RFD (early-force capability), which matters more than peak force for a fast sport.
- Caveat: **IMTP-to-pitch-velocity correlation is moderate at best** — use it to confirm a strength floor, not to predict velocity. (promising)

**Dynamic Strength Index (DSI) = CMJ peak force ÷ IMTP peak force** classifies whether to push *ballistic* work (low DSI = strong but can't express it fast → train power/velocity) or *max strength* (high DSI relative to poor absolute strength → build the force reservoir). Driveline's logic: NPF <3000 N → strength phase; >3000 N but below-average CMJ height → power phase. (promising) Note DSI reliability is debated — a 2024 study found jump performance and DSI uncorrelated, so treat DSI as one input among several, not gospel. (plausible)

**Hardware / cost reality:** Hawkin Dynamics and VALD ForceDecks are the two dominant baseball systems; both are quote-based. VALD leases (3-yr subscription, ~3%/yr escalator, bundled software); SimpliFaster lists VALD SKUs across a broad $360-$12,360 range. Hawkin markets itself as the most affordable professional dual plates and offers lease or purchase plus high-school pricing tiers. Budget **~$8,000-$15,000+ for a research-grade dual-plate system**, plus software subscription. (promising — figures are indicative, get quotes)

**For Soto / Neptune:** A dual force-plate system is the highest-leverage single hardware buy for the "development lab" positioning. Minimum viable battery: **CMJ (readiness + power profiling) + IMTP (strength floor + RFD) every 6-week block.** Store NPF, relative strength, CMJ peak power, mRSI, and DSI per athlete in the Compete data layer; benchmark each against Driveline's public thresholds (NPF 3000 N, 3× BW) and your own accumulating facility norms. Build normative percentile tables *by level* as data accumulates — that in-house norm set is a genuine moat and directly mirrors how Triton builds `league_averages`.

## 5. Force-velocity profiling — the promise and the reality check

The F-V profile plots force against velocity across loads to find an athlete's individual **optimal profile** and the gap to it (force-velocity imbalance, FVimb). The prescription is intuitive: velocity deficit → light/negative loads (<30% 1RM); force deficit → heavy loads (>80% 1RM).

**The original evidence was seductive.** Jiménez-Reyes' individualized-training studies reported that FVimb-matched training beat one-size-fits-all resistance training, with the optimized sub-groups improving jump height 7.2-14.2% and much more consistently than non-optimized groups. (This is why the concept swept S&C.) (promising, as originally reported)

**The 2023-2025 reappraisal is sobering.** Systematic reviews and meta-analyses now find FV-optimized training partially corrects a force deficit, fully corrects a velocity deficit, but has **small-to-trivial, often unclear effects on maximal power**, and produces jump-height gains **comparable to non-optimized training**. Worse, the F-V characteristics are **task-dependent** — the same athlete profiles differently on a squat jump vs a loaded CMJ vs a sprint — so the "optimal profile" isn't a stable individual property. A 2023 IJSPP piece flatly called the sprint version "a dead end." Free-weight CMJ F-V-power profiling also shows questionable between-session reliability. (debunked as a precise optimization tool)

**The salvage:** the *coarse* insight survives — identifying whether an athlete is force-deficient or velocity-deficient and biasing training accordingly is defensible and easy. The *fine* insight — computing a precise FVimb % and chasing a theoretical optimum — is not supported. (plausible for the coarse version)

**For Soto:** Do not build a Neptune product around a precise "F-V imbalance %" number — it will not survive scrutiny with Trevor, and the metric is unreliable session-to-session. Instead use the durable version: DSI + IMTP + CMJ to classify each athlete as *strength-limited*, *velocity-limited*, or *balanced*, and bias the block accordingly. Frame it honestly as a coarse profile, evidence-graded.

## 6. Velocity-based training (VBT)

VBT uses bar velocity (from a linear position transducer like GymAware/Tendo, or an accelerometer) to autoregulate load and quantify intent. Two uses: **load prescription** (pick load by target velocity, not %1RM) and **fatigue autoregulation** (stop a set at a velocity-loss threshold, e.g., 10-20%).

**Driveline's load-velocity zones (compound lifts):**
| Zone | Load (%1RM) | Velocity (m/s) | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Starting strength | ≤30% | >1.3 | Overcome inertia fast |
| Speed-strength | 30-40% | 1.0-1.3 | Light, high velocity |
| Strength-speed | 50-60% | 0.75-1.0 | Moderate load, max intent |
| Power | 75-95% | 0.3-0.6 | Peak power expression |
| Max strength | 70-90% | 0.5-0.75 | Build force reservoir |

Assessment anchors: ~30% 1RM should move >1.3 m/s, 40% at 1.0-1.15 m/s, 50% at 0.85-1.0 m/s. High-velocity power work runs low volume/high quality: e.g., 6×2 with 60 s rest, ~1:12 work-to-rest for max power. (promising) Driveline's own sequencing: **absolute strength first**, then transfer to a VBT program to develop rate of force development once a strength floor exists. (promising)

VBT's real value is **objectivity and daily readiness**: the same %1RM moves slower on a fatigued day, so velocity targets auto-regulate load down without a formal deload, and the velocity number is a motivating, gamified intent cue. (proven — the autoregulation logic; transfer to on-field velocity is inferred, not directly proven)

**For Soto / Neptune:** A single GymAware/Tendo-class unit (~$1-2k) is a cheap, high-yield add that produces exactly the kind of numeric athlete-facing feedback the Triton stack thrives on. Log bar velocity per set → athlete dashboard → trend against med-ball and CMJ. This is the weight-room analog of the TrackMan feedback loop.

## 7. Rotational power testing — Proteus and normative context

**Proteus Motion** is a 3D cable resistance trainer that measures power and acceleration in the transverse plane — uniquely suited to rotational athletes because it captures frontal/transverse-plane output that a barbell cannot. (plausible/promising)

**Evidence in pitchers (small but encouraging):** Cressey's collegiate cohort (n=14, 6 weeks, 3 test sessions) ran a 17-exercise General Power Test in <5 minutes. **Dominant-side acceleration correlated with pitch velocity at R=0.76, p=0.003** (session 3); General Power Test scores rose ~25% and fastballs averaged 84→87 mph (+3 mph). Counterintuitive asymmetry findings: 7 of 10 RH pitchers were ~5.6% *more* powerful rotating toward the dominant side; unilateral press/row out-produced bilateral by ~4% (transverse-plane contribution). (plausible — no control group, small n, confounded with concurrent throwing)

**Cost:** Proteus is quote-based (~$15k for a used unit as a reference point). Facility service pricing seen in the wild: ~$149 initial screen, $49 re-tests, or ~$50/General Power Test — a viable revenue line, not just a cost center. (promising — indicative figures)

**Where it fits vs alternatives:** Proteus is the premium rotational-power option; the **3 kg rotational med-ball throw with radar velocity** captures ~80% of the same insight for ~2% of the cost and has stronger published correlations to bat/pitch velocity. Buy Proteus for the athlete-experience / content / differentiation value and its granular per-plane data; rely on med-ball throw velocity as the everyday workhorse metric.

**For Soto:** Proteus is on-brand for the "tech-forward development lab" and generates compelling content (Trevor + Mayday Media), but on pure evidence-per-dollar the radar-measured rotational med-ball throw wins. Recommend med-ball throw velocity as the mandatory intake metric; Proteus as a premium tier. If Neptune buys Proteus, pipe dominant/non-dominant acceleration and power into the Compete layer and watch the dominant-side-acceleration-vs-velocity relationship in your own athletes before trusting the R=0.76 externally.

## 8. Putting it together — a Neptune power profiling battery

A defensible, evidence-graded intake + monitoring battery, ordered by leverage:

1. **Rotational med-ball throw velocity (3 kg, radar)** — best cheap power correlate to bat/pitch velocity (r≈0.6-0.65). Mandatory. (proven)
2. **Force-plate CMJ** — power profiling (peak power, mRSI) + daily/weekly readiness via eccentric braking metrics. (promising)
3. **Force-plate IMTP** — strength floor (NPF >3000 N, >3× BW) + early RFD (force at 150-250 ms). (promising)
4. **DSI (CMJ PF ÷ IMTP PF)** — coarse strength-vs-power bias, not a precise optimum. (plausible)
5. **Lateral-to-medial jump distance** — frontal-plane, throwing-specific power. (promising)
6. **VBT bar velocity** on primary lifts — autoregulation + intent + trendable numbers. (promising)
7. **Proteus** (optional premium tier) — granular rotational power + content/differentiation. (plausible)

Re-test every 6-week block. Store everything per athlete in the Compete data layer; build in-house percentile norms by level over time. Program from *deficits*: strength-limited → heavy/max-strength + IMTP-guided; velocity-limited → ballistic/VBT speed zones + light med-ball; balanced → mixed/contrast. Grade every claim to the athlete — Trevor will discount anything ungraded, and honestly, so should the athlete.

**A note on Trevor specifically:** as a post-TJ, late-career arm now in a staying-sharp/demo/coaching-credibility phase, the highest-value personal metrics are the *monitoring* ones (CMJ readiness, med-ball throw velocity trend) rather than max-strength chasing. CMJ eccentric-braking decline is a clean, non-throwing fatigue readout that respects the elbow — useful for calibrating demo/throwing days without overloading a reconstructed UCL. (plausible — apply conservatively)

# Periodization and the Annual Plan for Pitchers

## TL;DR

- **Periodized training beats non-periodized training, but the flavor barely matters**: meta-analytic advantage of periodized vs. non-periodized resistance programs on 1RM strength is ES ≈ 0.31, while linear vs. undulating vs. block comparisons show no reliable differences — sequencing and intent matter more than the model's name (proven).
- **The offseason arc is universal at every serious program**: shutdown → rebuild/on-ramp (3–5 wk) → build/velocity (3–6 wk) → mound blend (1–2 wk) → mound development/pitch design (3–4 wk), ending at the reporting date. Driveline budgets a pro offseason at ~20 weeks (Oct–Feb); a pitcher who doesn't pick up a ball until January gets on-ramp + prep only, no velocity block (promising).
- **The lifting calendar runs opposite the throwing calendar**: White Sox staff (Klein et al. 2021) prescribe hypertrophy/reconditioning at 60–70% 1RM ×8–10 reps in October when throwing is zero, accumulating to 80–85% ×6–8 by Nov–Dec, and absolute power at 85–90% ×3–5 in Jan–Feb as throwing intent peaks and lifting volume drops (promising).
- **Annual rest is the most evidence-backed lever for youth/HS arms**: ASMI mandates no overhead throwing 2–3 months/yr (4 preferred), no competitive pitching 4 months/yr, ≤100 innings/yr, and no pitcher-catcher doubling; PRP scales shutdown by age — 16 wk (8–12u) down to 6–10 wk (18–22) (proven).
- **Workload ratio math now anchors ramp design**: keep acute:chronic workload ratio 0.7–1.3; ACWR >1.27–1.3 associates with ~15% higher injury odds in throwers (and >1,500% relative risk spikes in some datasets). Reinold's 2024 IJSPT rebuild of the interval throwing program stretched it from 136 to 217 days specifically to hold ACWR in-range 91% of the time (promising).
- **Velocity blocks are a risk trade, not a free lunch**: Reinold's 2018 RCT — 6-wk weighted-ball program in HS pitchers — produced +3.3% velocity but a 24% injury rate (0% in controls) and +4.3° shoulder ER; Marsh 2018 found no velocity gain at all in 17 college/pro arms over 6 weeks. Program the velocity phase like a controlled overload, with monitoring (proven).
- **In-season is maintenance, not hibernation**: Cressey's pro template gets a 5-day starter ~12 lifts/month (hard lower-body lift the day after a start, upper on bullpen day, nothing heavy within 48 h of the next start); relievers pick from a menu (15–17-set "long" full-body vs. 8–12-set short options) based on recent pitch counts, minimum 2–3 gym sessions/week (promising).
- **Two-way players get half the recovery slots**: college coaches DH them the day before/after starts; the Dodgers run Ohtani in a six-man rotation, pitch him before off-days, and occasionally bench the bat on start days — the template is "protect the day-before and day-after," at every level (promising).
- **Summer ball is a fork in the road for college arms**: >100 spring innings → skip summer ball and train; low spring usage → summer innings are the development window. The fall/spring/summer college calendar makes this the single biggest annual-plan decision for that population (plausible).

## 1. What Periodization Actually Buys You

Periodization is the deliberate sequencing of training stress — volume, intensity, and exercise selection — across a year so that adaptations compound instead of competing. The empirical case for having *a* plan is solid: a systematic review and meta-analysis of periodized vs. non-periodized resistance training found a significant advantage for periodized programs on 1RM strength (ES = 0.31) (proven). The case for any *specific* model is much weaker: linear vs. daily-undulating comparisons show no consistent difference in strength or hypertrophy, and block vs. traditional comparisons in trained endurance athletes are mixed (some VO2max advantages for block, others null) (plausible). The honest read for baseball: the sequencing logic — general → specific, volume → intensity, gym-dominant → mound-dominant — is what matters, not fidelity to Matveyev or Issurin.

For pitchers, the organizing constraint is not the gym at all — it's the throwing calendar. The arm's stress budget (elbow valgus torque per throw, summed across the year) is the scarce resource; lifting, sprinting, and mobility are subordinated to it. Every credible annual plan, from Driveline to the White Sox performance staff to ASMI's rehab protocols, is a throwing plan with a lifting plan wrapped around it (promising).

**For Soto:** this is the framing for Neptune's programming product — the throwing plan is the spine, everything else attaches to it. A Neptune athlete's "annual plan" object should be a dated throwing-phase timeline with lifting/movement blocks as dependent layers, not two parallel calendars.

## 2. The Offseason Arc: Rebuild → Build → Ramp

Every elite program converges on the same offseason skeleton, differing only in labels and week counts. Driveline's canonical structure (for a pro reporting to spring training) (promising):

1. **Shutdown / active rest** — no overhead throwing. 2–4+ weeks minimum for pros, longer by workload and age (see §5).
2. **On-ramp ("rebuild")** — 3–4 weeks going from zero to 100%-intent throwing. Rule of thumb: the longer the time off, the longer the on-ramp. PRP's on-ramp template: ~18 throwing days over 4–5 weeks — Week 1: 4 light days at 60–90 ft; Week 2: 3 moderate days at 120–150 ft; Week 3: long-toss extension; Week 4: 4–5 days with compression (pulldown-style) throws added; always ≥1 rest day between throwing days.
3. **Velocity ("build")** — 3–6 weeks of scheduled 100%-effort throwing (pulldowns, weighted-ball work, constraint drills). This is where the offseason's physical gains get expressed as ball speed.
4. **Mound blend** — 1–2 weeks converting run-and-gun intent into mound mechanics.
5. **Mound development ("ramp")** — 3–4 weeks of pitch design, command work, and bullpens building toward game-ready pitch counts at the reporting date.

Driveline's planning heuristic is to work **backward from the date the athlete must be 100% mound-ready** and let available time dictate what fits (promising):
- Start throwing in **October** (~20 wk): full arc — extended on-ramp, 3+ wk velocity, both mound phases.
- Start in **November**: 4 wk on-ramp + 3–4 wk velocity + compressed mound work.
- Start in **December**: 4 wk on-ramp + 4 wk mound development; velocity window essentially gone.
- Start in **January**: on-ramp + 2 wk mound prep. Preparation only — no development.

This is the most actionable single idea in offseason planning: **late starters forfeit the velocity phase, not the readiness phase**. Readiness is non-negotiable; development is what time buys.

The velocity phase deserves an honest risk label. Reinold et al. 2018 (Sports Health, RCT with Fleisig and Andrews): 6-week weighted-ball program in high school pitchers produced +3.3% velocity and +4.3° shoulder external rotation — but a **24% injury rate in the training group vs. 0% in controls** (proven). Marsh et al. 2018: 17 collegiate/pro pitchers, 6 weeks of weighted implements, **no significant velocity increase** (promising). Meanwhile a 2023 cross-sectional survey found most US high school pitchers now use weighted-ball programs. Net read: velocity training works on average but with wide individual variance and a real injury tail; it belongs inside a monitored build phase with adequate chronic workload underneath it, never as a bolt-on for an under-prepared arm (promising).

**For Soto:** Neptune's offseason intake should capture two dates — last competitive pitch and required-ready date — and auto-generate the phase skeleton from the Driveline back-planning table. The Compete/TrackMan pipeline gives phase-over-phase velocity deltas for free; +3.3% (≈2.5–3 mph on an 80 mph HS arm) is the honest expectation to set with parents, alongside the injury-rate caveat.

## 3. The Lifting Calendar: Running Opposite the Throwing Calendar

The best-documented pro template is Klein, Cobian, Simmons & Reinold (Chicago White Sox staff), *Current Reviews in Musculoskeletal Medicine*, 2021 — a September–February plan (promising):

| Phase | Timing | Prescription |
|---|---|---|
| Active rest | Sept (2–4 wk) | Movement, ROM, bodyweight mobility; no barbell intensity |
| Reconditioning (hypertrophy) | Oct (4 wk) | 4×/wk upper/lower split, 2–3 sets × 8–10 @ 60–70% 1RM |
| Accumulative strength | Nov (4–6 wk) | 3–4 sets × 6–8 @ 80–85% |
| Strength-speed | Dec–early Jan (2–3 wk) | 3–4 × 6–8 @ 80–85% with velocity intent |
| Absolute power / precompetitive | Jan–Feb (4–6 wk) | 2–4 sets × 3–5 @ 85–90%, VBT with ~10% velocity-loss cutoffs |

The structural logic: **gym stress peaks when throwing stress is near zero, and tapers as throwing intent rises**. October's hypertrophy block coincides with shutdown/on-ramp; by the January–February velocity and mound phases, lifting is low-volume, high-intent power work that potentiates rather than competes with throwing (promising).

Other numbers worth carrying:
- **Trunk rotation power had the largest influence on throwing velocity** among physical qualities in the White Sox review — justifying heavy frontal/transverse-plane and med-ball work over purely sagittal barbell training (promising). Cressey programs med-ball throws 2–3×/week in early/mid-offseason, tapering to low volume late-offseason and in-season (promising).
- **Rotator cuff strength is realistically only rebuildable when throwing stops.** Cressey: pros get ~10–16 weeks off from throwing, and those weeks are "absolutely crucial" for cuff strength, because concurrent high-volume throwing blunts cuff adaptation (plausible — mechanistically sound, not RCT-proven).
- Nordic hamstring work roughly **halves hamstring injury rates** (van Dyk meta-analysis, 8,459 athletes) — cheap insurance in the rebuild phase (proven).
- The White Sox staff avoid full Olympic lifts (catch positions load the shoulder/elbow) in favor of plyometric and derivative alternatives — a defensible but debated convention (plausible).

Blending rule set for build-phase weeks (synthesis of Driveline/Cressey/RPP practice) (promising):
- High-intent throwing days pair with high-CNS lifting days ("high/high–low/low") so genuine recovery days exist.
- Heavy lower-body work sits ≥24 h before, not day-of, max-intent throwing; heavy upper pressing stays 48 h clear of velocity days.
- As throwing volume climbs ~week-over-week, lifting volume must fall — total systemic stress, not gym stress, is what's periodized.

**For Soto:** for Trevor specifically — post-TJ, post-career, training for sharpness and content rather than a comeback — the Klein phase ladder can run permanently truncated: live in reconditioning/strength blocks, visit strength-speed, and skip absolute-power peaking unless a specific throwing demo requires a mini on-ramp + blend.

## 4. In-Season: Maintenance Is a Real Program

Detraining is fast enough that "just pitch" costs strength and, by late season, velocity — Cressey catalogs in-season velocity decline as multifactorial (cuff weakness, mobility loss, cumulative fatigue) (plausible). The practitioner consensus is **2–3 quality lifts per week, minimum, all season** (promising).

Cressey's professional templates (promising):

**5-day starter** (~12 lifts/month):
- Day 0: start.
- Day 1: challenging lower-body lift + light push-up/row + cuff work (the arm is trashed anyway; sink the leg stress here).
- Day 2: movement day — sprints/agility.
- Day 3: bullpen + single-leg work + upper-body lift.
- Day 4: low-intensity dynamic flexibility only.
- Day 5: next start.

**7-day starter (college rotation)**: two full strength sessions (upper- and lower-emphasis), movement training 2×/wk (top-speed work to 50–60 yd), plus <30%-1RM circuit days.

**Relievers**: a decision menu, not a schedule — after outings >20 pitches, a "long" full-body session (15–17 sets); otherwise short full-body options (8–12 sets, lower- or upper-emphasis), chosen against recent workload and the odds of pitching tonight.

In-season principles: quality over quantity; sprint 2×/wk (3 max); strength + mobility outrank conditioning volume; distance running earns nothing a pitcher needs (promising — steady-state vs. interval conditioning showed no velocity difference in an 11-week collegiate study, and sprint-trained groups trend better on power measures) (promising).

**For Soto:** Neptune's in-season product for HS/college clients is scheduling software as much as programming — the reliever "menu" model (pick session by last-48h workload) maps cleanly to an app decision tree fed by logged pitch counts, and is a genuine differentiator vs. static PDF programs.

## 5. Calendars by Level: HS vs. College vs. Pro

**High school.** Season ~Feb/Mar–May(–July with summer ball). The dominant risks are overlap (school + travel teams), two-way workload, and no true offseason. Hard guardrails, all ASMI/Pitch Smart (proven):
- No overhead throwing ≥2–3 months/yr (4 preferred); no competitive pitching ≥4 months/yr.
- ≤100 competitive innings per calendar year.
- Never pitch while fatigued; never pitcher-and-catcher on the same team.
- Daily pitch caps by age: 50 (7–8), 75 (9–10), 85 (11–12), up to 105 (17–18), with mandated rest days.
Shutdown scaling (PRP) (promising): 8–12u → 16 wk low-stress/shutdown; 12–15 → 12–16 wk; 15–18 → 8–12 wk; 18–22 → 6–10 wk. A workload heuristic: youth ≈ 1 wk off per 10 IP; HS ≈ 1 wk per 15 IP; ≥60 IP → 3–4 wk full shutdown minimum before on-ramping (plausible). The HS annual plan is therefore: June/July summer ball → Aug–Sept shutdown + rebuild lifting → Oct–Dec build (this is the *only* real development window) → Jan on-ramp/blend → Feb tryouts. Velocity programs for this group must live in Oct–Dec, monitored, per §2's risk data.

**College.** The trickiest calendar in baseball: **fall ball (Sept–Nov, job competition at full intent)** → short winter gap (Dec–Jan, the only training block) → spring season (mid-Feb, per the NCAA's ~Feb 13 opening, through May/June) → **summer ball decision**. Freshmen commonly see fall velocity *drops* from the workload/lifestyle jump — expected, not alarming (plausible). The summer fork (Premier Pitching / Cressey) (plausible-to-promising):
- Threw >100 spring innings → skip summer ball; shutdown, rebuild, and train. Summer is the only true development window for a used arm.
- Low spring usage (bullpen guys, redshirts) → summer innings *are* the development plan; train around them with the 7-day-starter template.
The failure mode is the guy who throws Feb–May, plays summer ball June–Aug, and reports to fall ball in September having never shut down — a 12-month competitive year that violates every rest guideline above.

**Pro.** Longest offseason (Oct–mid-Feb, ~18–20 wk) and longest season (Feb–Sept/Oct, 140–160+ games). The offseason supports the full Driveline arc (§2) and the Klein lifting ladder (§3); in-season is Cressey's §4 templates. The pro-specific problems are travel-compressed recovery, September fatigue, and — for MiLB — small support staffs, which is why remote programs (Tread, Driveline) dominate that population (promising).

**For Soto:** Neptune's client intake must capture level *and* calendar (school season dates, travel/summer commitments) because the same 16-year-old gets a completely different plan depending on whether he plays fall travel ball. An automated "annual innings + rest audit" against ASMI limits is a high-trust, low-cost parent-facing report — and the data already flows through Compete.

## 6. Two-Way Players

Two-way athletes carry two stress streams (throwing + swing volume, plus fielding throws) into one recovery budget. Rules of thumb from college programs and the Ohtani precedent (promising):
- **Protect the day before and day after a start.** College coaches DH the two-way player those days (no fielding throws); NPB's Ohtani didn't play the two games before his Sunday starts.
- **Use schedule structure as the workload valve**: college's 2+ weekly off-days make two-way viable; the Dodgers use a six-man rotation, slot Ohtani's starts before team off-days, and sometimes make him a "one-way pitcher" on start days (no hitting). His Angels-era sustainable load: ~25 starts, ~143 IP, ~635 PA per year (2021–23).
- **In the HS version, the ASMI rule is blunter**: don't catch, and avoid SS/3B, if you pitch regularly — those positions' throw volumes silently double annual arm workload (proven for pitcher-catcher; plausible for SS/3B).
- Offseason for a two-way: sequence, don't stack — bias the early offseason to the arm (on-ramp/velocity while hitting volume is low), then shift to bat volume during mound-blend weeks when throwing intent is structured and submaximal (plausible — practitioner logic, no trials).

**For Soto:** most Neptune HS clients will be de facto two-way. Programming templates need a "position throws + swings" workload field, not just pitch counts; TrackMan pitch data alone undercounts a shortstop-pitcher's arm stress badly.

## 7. Workload Math: The Monitoring Layer Under the Plan

The annual plan's phases are hypotheses; workload monitoring is the check. Current state of the art:

- **ACWR 0.7–1.3 is the working corridor** (Science for Sport; Driveline PULSE). Below 0.7 = undertraining/detraining risk; above ~1.3 = elevated injury risk. In throwers, ACWR >1.27 associated with ~14.9% higher injury odds; one dataset reported >1,500% relative-risk inflation above 1.3 (≈ a 30% week-over-week workload jump) (promising — ACWR causality is contested in the broader sports-science literature; treat as a useful guardrail, not physics).
- **Driveline PULSE per-throw workload** = (peak elbow valgus torque [Nm] ÷ (height[m] × mass[kg]))^1.3 — the exponent means a 100 Nm throw ≈ 2.45× a 50 Nm throw, not 2×. Acute = 9-day weighted average (kernel 1.3 → 0.7); chronic = 28-day rolling average; "high-effort" = >70% of the mean of the 5 highest-torque throws in 14 days (promising).
- **Reinold et al. 2024 (IJSPT)** rebuilt the standard rehab/return interval throwing program from 111,196 sensor-tracked D1 throws: 217 days (vs. 136 in the legacy ITP), 120 throwing days (45 flat-ground, 75 mound), ACWR in-corridor 91% of days (vs. 82%), peak ACWR 1.33 (vs. 1.61); mound entry ~week 16 at 50% effort, changeups week 19, breaking balls week 23, deloads weeks 7/14/22/29. Key finding: **long-toss distance, not radar velocity, tracks elbow torque** — progress intensity by distance (promising). A 2025 IJSPT field study of 9 collegiate pitchers found preseason ACWR exceeded 1.27 in 4 of 6 weeks — i.e., even "normal" preseason ramps routinely violate the corridor, which is exactly the window where spring injuries cluster (promising).

**For Soto:** this is the Neptune/Triton integration thesis. Compete already ingests TrackMan sessions; adding a per-athlete rolling acute (7–9 d) and chronic (28 d) workload from logged throw counts × intent (or sensor torque if Neptune buys motus/PULSE-class sleeves) gives every athlete an ACWR dial on their dashboard. The 0.7–1.3 corridor + phase-skeleton from §2 is a fully computable annual plan — a genuine "development lab" product no cage barn can copy.

## 8. Case-Study Annual Plans

**Case A — HS junior, two-way (SS/pitcher), school season Mar–May, travel ball Jun–Jul.**
Aug: 4-wk full shutdown (threw ~70 IP + position throws → top of PRP's 8–12 wk low-stress band); lifting = Klein reconditioning block (60–70% ×8–10, 4×/wk). Sept: on-ramp weeks 1–4 (PRP template, 60–90 ft → 150 ft) + accumulative strength. Oct–Nov: 5-wk build — monitored velocity work 2–3 high-intent days/wk, plyo balls only if chronic workload is established, lifting drops to 3×/wk strength-speed; ACWR held ≤1.3 week-over-week. Dec: mound blend + pitch design (2 pitches max of focus). Jan–Feb: mound development to 45–60 pitch bullpens; absolute-power lifting 2×/wk. In-season: 2 lifts/wk (post-start lower, mid-week full), no catching, DH on day-before/after starts. Annual audit target: <100 IP, ≥3 months no-throw (plausible synthesis of proven guardrails).

**Case B — College starter, 95 IP in spring.**
Over the 100-IP-adjacent line with fall ball looming → **skip summer ball**. Jun: 3 wk shutdown + active rest. Jul: on-ramp + hypertrophy/strength (the year's only real gym block). Aug: 3–4 wk velocity build, peaking before campus return. Sept–Nov fall ball: treat as in-season (7-day-starter template, 2 lifts/wk); expect flat-to-down velo, don't chase it. Dec: 2-wk mini-shutdown + reconditioning. Jan: on-ramp/blend into mid-Feb opening day (promising synthesis).

**Case C — Late-career reliever archetype (the Trevor case).**
Pro reliever, ~60 appearances: Oct shutdown 4 wk (ASMI floor, plus cuff-rebuild window per Cressey); Nov–Dec Klein reconditioning → accumulative strength while on-ramping in Nov; Jan velocity block abbreviated (2–3 wk — relievers ride adrenaline, not offseason pulldown PRs, and post-TJ arms budget conservatively); late Jan–Feb mound development to game-ready 25-pitch bursts by report date. In-season: Cressey reliever menu (long 15–17-set session after 20+-pitch outings; 8–12-set short options otherwise; ≥2–3 lifts/wk). For the retired version of this athlete, delete the Jan velocity block entirely and cap intent at ~90% except for planned, on-ramped demo days (plausible).

## 9. Where the Evidence Is Thin (So Soto Doesn't Overclaim)

- No RCT has ever compared full annual-plan structures in pitchers; the phase skeleton is practitioner consensus + mechanistic reasoning, not trial evidence (plausible, and likely to stay that way).
- ACWR's causal status is genuinely contested in sports science (the metric partially predicts injury because workload spikes correlate with schedule chaos); use it as a guardrail with athlete-level context, not a verdict (promising-with-caveats).
- Weighted-ball and velocity-program effects are heterogeneous: +3.3% mean with a 24% injury tail (Reinold 2018) vs. null (Marsh 2018). Individual response screening — chronic workload, ER ROM change, torque monitoring — is the difference between programs (proven heterogeneity).
- In-season lifting's velocity-preservation effect is inferred from detraining physiology and practitioner observation, not pitcher-specific RCTs (plausible).
- Two-way programming is almost entirely case-study evidence (Ohtani, college anecdote); nothing controlled exists (plausible).

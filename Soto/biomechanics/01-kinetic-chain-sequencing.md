# The Pitching Kinetic Chain & Kinematic Sequence

## TL;DR

- **The efficient pitch is a proximal-to-distal whip: pelvis → trunk → arm (shoulder IR) → forearm (elbow extension) → hand, each segment peaking later and faster than the one before it.** Typical peak angular velocities cascade roughly pelvis ~650–700°/s → trunk ~1,000–1,200°/s → elbow extension ~2,300–4,600°/s → shoulder internal rotation ~6,200–9,000°/s, the last being the fastest human joint motion ever measured (proven).
- **Almost nobody throws the textbook sequence.** In mocap studies of real fastballs, 14–17 distinct sequence patterns show up and *none* was the pure proximal-to-distal ideal; the most common was pelvis → trunk → arm → hand → forearm (proven).
- **Sequence order dictates arm stress more than "delivery style."** Pitches where the arm peaks before the hand ("altered proximal") loaded the elbow at 65.3 ± 8.8 N·m vs 49.8 ± 13.8 N·m for the cleanest proximal-to-distal group — a ~31% jump in valgus load for the same job (proven).
- **Lower-body energy is not optional.** A ~20% drop in kinetic energy from the hips/trunk forces a compensatory ~34% increase in shoulder rotational velocity to hold ball speed — the arm literally pays the bill the legs skip (promising).
- **Timing beats magnitude on separation.** Hip–shoulder separation averages ~50 ± 12° at foot contact and 35–60° is the workable band, but *when* peak pelvis and peak trunk velocities occur (both after foot plant, pelvis first) matters more than the raw separation number (promising).
- **Trunk rotation is the single best mechanical predictor of ball speed.** Peak trunk velocity alone explains ~25% of ball-velocity variance; trunk rotation timing + trunk power explain ~53% of ball speed and ~68% of peak elbow valgus (proven).
- **Pros sequence *later and tighter* than kids.** Peak pelvis rotation lands at ~43% of the pitch cycle in pros vs ~28% in high-schoolers, and elite pitchers show far less trial-to-trial variability — mechanical repeatability rises with level (proven).
- **Injury shows up as a timing leak, not a reordering.** Symptomatic throwers keep the same segment order but decouple elbow extension and shoulder flexion (which fire simultaneously in healthy arms), leaning on shoulder internal rotation and trunk flexion to hold velocity — the classic compensation signature (promising).

## 1. What the Kinetic Chain Actually Is

The pitching "kinetic chain" is the coordinated linkage that converts ground reaction force at the back foot into hand speed at ball release. Energy is generated by the large, stable, slow proximal segments (legs, pelvis, trunk) and handed off — with amplification — to the small, fast distal segments (upper arm, forearm, hand, ball). This is the *summation of speed principle*: if each distal segment delays its own peak until the segment beneath it has finished accelerating, the velocities stack rather than cancel (proven).

The efficient order, in terms of when each segment reaches its **peak angular velocity**, is:

1. **Pelvis** rotation
2. **Trunk / upper torso** rotation
3. **Arm** (shoulder internal rotation)
4. **Forearm** (elbow extension)
5. **Hand**

Because it is *angular* velocity that transfers, the same energy applied to progressively shorter, lighter levers produces progressively higher tip speed — the whip effect. Rotational kinetic energy scales with the square of angular velocity (KE ∝ I·ω²), which is why distal segments can carry enormous velocity on a small fraction of the body's mass (proven). The endpoint of this cascade is shoulder internal rotation at 7,000–9,000°/s — described in the clinical literature as the single fastest joint motion in all of human movement (proven).

**For Soto:** This is the physical substrate under Stuff+. Velocity and extension (the `veloZ` and `extZ` terms in `100 + veloZ*4.5 + moveZ*3.5 + extZ*2.0`) are *downstream outputs* of chain efficiency. A Neptune intake that measures the chain directly (mocap/IMU) explains *why* a pitcher's Stuff+ inputs are where they are, and where the ceiling is. Extension in particular is a proxy for how far down the chain the athlete is projecting force before release.

## 2. The Peak-Velocity Cascade: Magnitudes and Ordering

The defining feature of an elite sequence is not just correct order but a rising staircase of peak magnitudes, each peak steeper and later than the last. Representative values from the mocap literature and practitioner datasets:

- **Pelvis:** ~400–700°/s; a practitioner-cited "average" of ~655°/s, with an elite example at ~875°/s (34% above average) and a poor example at ~552°/s that also peaked *before* foot plant (proven for range; the specific 655 figure is a practitioner benchmark, plausible).
- **Trunk / torso:** ~1,000–1,200°/s, built on top of pelvis rotation (proven).
- **Elbow extension:** ~2,300–2,700°/s in general adult samples; ~4,600°/s in professionals (proven).
- **Shoulder internal rotation:** ~6,200°/s in pros, up to 7,000–9,000°/s at the top end — the terminal, fastest link (proven).

Two curve properties beyond the peak value matter for coaches reading a mocap trace (proven/promising):

- **Timing (order + separation):** each peak should occur later than the previous, with clean spacing. Overlapping or reversed peaks signal energy leaks.
- **Curve shape (slopes):** steep acceleration *and* steep deceleration on a proximal segment is the signature of energy being *released* into the next segment. A proximal segment that decelerates slowly is still "holding" energy it should have already handed off, which forces the next segment to start early and cuts the runway for velocity accumulation.

**For Soto:** A per-segment peak-velocity table (pelvis / trunk / elbow-ext / shoulder-IR in °/s, plus peak *timing* as % of pitch cycle) is the cleanest facility-metric schema for a `neptune_biomech` table sitting alongside `compete_pitches`. Store both magnitude and % -cycle timing per segment; the *gap between* peaks (separation time) is the derived feature with the most predictive juice (see §4).

## 3. Nobody Throws the Textbook Sequence

The ideal is a teaching target, not a description of reality. When researchers actually classified real fastball sequences:

- One landmark analysis found **14 different kinematic sequence patterns** across the pitches studied, with **none** exhibiting a fully proximal-to-distal order; the single most prevalent pattern was pelvis → trunk → arm → **hand → forearm** (i.e., the hand peaked *before* the forearm) (proven).
- A follow-up classifying fastballs found **17 distinct patterns**, again with none matching the pure theoretical ideal, and grouped them into four categories (proximal-to-distal, altered proximal, altered distal, and mixed) (proven).

The practical read: the *proximal half* of the chain (pelvis-before-trunk) is where efficiency is won or lost; the distal-most ordering (hand vs forearm) scrambles even in healthy, high-velocity pitchers and is less coachable. Don't chase a perfect five-segment cascade on a mocap report — chase pelvis-leads-trunk, both peaking after foot plant, with rising magnitudes (proven).

**For Soto:** When Neptune reports a sequence to an athlete, classify into the four-category bucket rather than demanding the literal 1-2-3-4-5. Flag "altered proximal" (arm peaks early) as the high-priority correction because it carries the biggest arm-stress penalty (§5).

## 4. Hip–Shoulder Separation and the Timing Window

Hip–shoulder separation (the angular difference between pelvis and upper-trunk orientation, peaking near foot contact) is the loading of the trunk as a torsion spring. Benchmarks:

- Average separation at foot contact ≈ **50 ± 12°** in high-school/college samples; the workable band is roughly **35–60°**, with elite throwers ranging ~30° to ~55° (proven).
- Separation at foot contact correlates with trunk rotation velocity, which in turn correlates with ball velocity — an *indirect* pathway (proven).
- But among elite college pitchers the *direct* separation → velocity relationship is weak; the raw number is oversold (promising).

What actually matters is **timing**, not the peak separation angle (promising/proven):

- **Peak pelvis angular velocity should occur after foot plant.** If the pelvis peaks *before* the front foot lands, the stored separation never converts — there's no stable base to rotate against and ground reaction force is wasted.
- The **combination** of peak pelvis velocity, hip–shoulder separation at foot contact, and the *timing* of peak trunk velocity explains **55%** of the variance in trunk rotation velocity (proven).
- Stride length modulates the timing of peak pelvic–trunk separation and trunk angular velocity; overstriding or understriding shifts the window and can raise shoulder/elbow tensile stress (promising).
- Pelvic-trunk coordination modeling shows an **anti-phase** relationship from foot contact to max external rotation (mean 67.3 ± 23.8% of the phase; r = 0.74 with velocity) transitioning to **in-phase** during the acceleration phase (mean 51 ± 32.5%; r = 0.58) — i.e., the segments should move *against* each other while loading, then *together* while firing (promising).

Pelvic *control* is a hidden gate on all of this: single-leg-balance axial-plane pelvic control correlated with pitching velocity at r = −0.76 (stride leg) and r = −0.65 (drive leg), and axial-plane pelvic variation *during* the pitch correlated r = −0.78 with velocity (lower wobble → higher velocity) in a 34-pitcher IMU study (mean velocity 35.5 ± 1.7 m/s) (promising).

**For Soto:** Two facility-actionable derived metrics fall out here: (1) **peak-pelvis-timing relative to foot plant** (must be *after*, expressed as % of cycle), and (2) **separation time** = the gap between peak pelvis and peak trunk velocity. Both are cheap to compute from IMU or markerless data and both are more predictive than the static separation angle every showcase quotes. Pair the mocap capture with a single-leg-balance/pelvic-control screen in the intake battery — it's a low-tech proxy for the axial-plane control that gates the whole chain.

## 5. Sequence Order Drives Arm Stress (the injury lever)

This is the finding that turns sequencing from a velocity topic into an arm-health topic. In a 10-pitcher, 88-pitch, 360 Hz mocap study, sequence pattern predicted joint torque *more than delivery method did* (proven):

| Sequence pattern | Elbow valgus | Shoulder ext. rotation | Shoulder extension |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PDS** (cleanest proximal-to-distal, forearm+hand peak together) | 49.8 ± 13.8 N·m | 52.6 ± 14.4 N·m | 56.0 ± 15.0 N·m |
| **Altered distal** (forearm peaks before hand) | 54.9 ± 19.5 N·m | 59.1 ± 22.0 N·m | 68.1 ± 29.0 N·m |
| **Altered proximal** (arm peaks before hand) | 65.3 ± 8.8 N·m | 70.4 ± 11.0 N·m | 87.8 ± 18.5 N·m |

The altered-proximal pattern carried ~31% more elbow valgus and ~57% more shoulder extension torque than the clean sequence — for the same pitch (proven). Related work shows torques rise specifically when **the trunk reaches peak rotational velocity before the pelvis** (proven), and that early onset of pelvic and trunk rotation is associated with increased shoulder internal-rotation and elbow-valgus torques and greater risk of surgery (promising).

Context for those torque numbers: peak elbow varus/valgus load in adult pitching runs ~64–71 N·m (pros ~71.3 ± 20.0 N·m; high-schoolers ~50.7 ± 14.6 N·m, but *identical* when normalized to ~0.04 body-weight × height — the pros aren't per-kg safer, they just throw harder) (proven). Every 10° increase in contralateral (glove-side) trunk lean at release adds ~4 N·m of elbow varus moment, and ~100° shoulder abduction with ~10° contralateral tilt minimizes peak elbow varus (proven).

**For Soto:** This is the direct bridge to a *biomech-informed arm-health metric* for Triton/Neptune. A pitcher's sequence classification (PDS vs altered-proximal) plus contralateral trunk tilt gives a physically grounded valgus-load estimate that complements — and could feed — a workload/injury-risk layer. For Trevor specifically (TJ, 2017), the altered-proximal pattern and early-trunk timing are the two mechanical red flags most worth checking in any return-to-throwing capture, because they load the reconstructed UCL for zero velocity benefit.

## 6. Energy Transfer Efficiency and the Segmental Power Story

Efficiency isn't a metaphor — it's measurable as segmental power (energy generated vs absorbed at each joint). Comparing 16 pros to 15 high-schoolers via segmental power analysis (proven):

- **Pelvis power:** 20 ± 9 vs 21 ± 9 W/kg (n.s.) — kids generate hip power fine.
- **Trunk power:** 34 ± 14 vs 40 ± 11 W/kg (n.s., HS slightly higher).
- **Upper arm power:** −15 ± 9 vs −14 ± 5 W/kg — *negative* = the arm mostly **absorbs** energy, it doesn't generate much.
- **Forearm power:** −24 ± 10 vs −17 ± 7 W/kg (pros absorb more, P = .034).
- **Ball velocity:** 36.3 ± 2.9 vs 30.4 ± 3.5 m/s (P = .001).

Two big lessons: (1) the distal segments are net energy *absorbers/redirectors*, not primary generators — the engine is proximal; and (2) **timing separates the levels more than power magnitude does.** Pros peaked pelvis rotation at 42.9 ± 9.7% of the pitch cycle vs 27.9 ± 23.4% for HS, and trunk rotation at 33 ± 16% vs 2 ± 23% — the kids fire *early and erratically* (note the huge SDs) (proven). In modeling, trunk rotation time + trunk power + upper-arm power predicted peak elbow valgus at r = 0.823 (67.7% of variance), and trunk rotation time + trunk power predicted ball speed at r = 0.731 (53.4% of variance) (proven).

The compensation math when the chain leaks: a ~20% reduction in hip/trunk kinetic energy forces a ~34% increase in shoulder rotational velocity to preserve ball speed (promising). That is the exact trade that ages arms — the athlete keeps their radar number while shifting the load onto the least durable link.

**For Soto:** "Trunk rotation *timing*" (as % of pitch cycle, relative to foot plant) is arguably the highest-value single feature to capture at Neptune — it's the strongest common thread across ball-speed *and* valgus-load models. Variability of that timing across pitches is itself a signal: tightening it is a concrete, trackable development target that shows up before velocity does.

## 7. What Elite Sequences Look Like in Mocap (and how we capture them)

Elite mocap signatures (proven except where noted):

- **Order:** pelvis peaks first, trunk second, both **after** front-foot plant; arm and forearm follow late in the acceleration phase.
- **Rising staircase of magnitudes:** pelvis (hundreds °/s) → trunk (~1,000+°/s) → elbow extension (thousands °/s) → shoulder IR (6,000–9,000°/s).
- **Steep proximal deceleration:** the pelvis and trunk brake hard right after their peaks — the "block and transfer" that whips the distal chain.
- **Low variability:** SDs of key timing/velocity parameters shrink as competition level rises; repeatability *is* the elite trait, as much as any single peak value (proven). Six of eleven kinematic parameters — foot placement, knee flexion, pelvis angular velocity, elbow flexion, shoulder external rotation, trunk forward tilt — differ significantly across levels, with college > HS > youth on pelvis and trunk velocity (proven).

**Capture technology (2024–2026 landscape):**

- **Marker-based optical mocap** (e.g., ASMI-style labs) remains the gold standard, sampling ~240–360 Hz with ~60+ reflective markers. Angular velocities to ~5,000°/s+ genuinely require 3D — 2D video can't resolve them (proven).
- **The OpenBiomechanics Project (Driveline R&D)** publicly released marker-based mocap on ~100 pitchers and ~98 hitters across youth → MLB — the field's most useful open benchmark set for level-based norms (proven).
- **Markerless systems** are the practical facility play. **KinaTrax** (in-stadium, multi-camera) is used across MLB for in-game pitching/hitting biomechanics. **PitchAI** (smartphone/markerless) validated against marker-based optical on 38 elite pitchers reported pelvis/trunk agreement averaging r² ≈ 0.92 with ~6.0 ± 1.1° RMSE — good enough for the *proximal* segments that matter most, with more error at the fast distal joints (promising). Driveline has also partnered with **Uplift Labs** for markerless capture (proven).

**For Soto / Neptune:** The stack decision is markerless-first. A validated markerless system (PitchAI-class smartphone, or a fixed 2–4 camera markerless rig) captures the pelvis/trunk timing and separation features — exactly the proximal signals with the most predictive value and the best markerless accuracy — at a fraction of a marker lab's cost and setup time. Reserve any marker-based session for edge cases needing distal-joint torque precision. Norms should be built against OpenBiomechanics level buckets so a youth athlete is compared to youth, not to MLB.

## 8. Common Sequence Faults and How They Present

The recurring faults, most-to-least coachable (proven/promising):

1. **Early trunk rotation ("flying open").** Trunk peaks before or too close to the pelvis, so pelvis energy never transfers cleanly. Robs velocity *and* forces arm overuse; in kids with weak cores, premature trunk rotation leaks stored tension. This is cited as the single most common flaw in the pitching kinetic chain (promising).
2. **Pelvis peaks before foot plant.** No stable base to rotate against → ground reaction force wasted, separation never converts. Shows as a pelvis peak at ~28% (or earlier) of the cycle instead of ~40%+ (proven).
3. **Altered-proximal ordering (arm peaks before hand).** The highest arm-stress pattern (§5) — big valgus penalty for no velocity gain (proven).
4. **Trunk-before-pelvis reversal.** Directly raises shoulder and elbow torques (proven).
5. **Slow proximal deceleration.** Proximal segment holds energy instead of releasing it, forcing distal segments to start early and shortening the velocity runway (promising).
6. **Excessive contralateral trunk tilt at release.** +4 N·m elbow varus per +10° tilt — a velocity-neutral way to buy injury risk (proven).
7. **Post-injury timing decoupling.** In symptomatic throwers the segment *order* stays intact but elbow extension and shoulder flexion — simultaneous in healthy arms (~0.17% before release) — decouple (elbow extension ~0.06% before, shoulder flexion ~0.67% *after* release), with compensatory reliance on shoulder internal rotation and trunk (sagittal) flexion to hold ball speed. Ball velocity is *preserved* (21.6 vs 21.4 m/s, n.s.), which is exactly why the fault hides — the radar gun lies while the mechanics degrade (promising).

**For Soto:** Faults #2, #3, #4, and #7 are all detectable from proximal-segment timing (peak-pelvis-vs-foot-plant, separation time, sequence-bucket, elbow/shoulder-flexion coupling) — precisely the features a markerless rig captures well. Build the Neptune sequence report around a small set of green/yellow/red flags on those, not around a wall of °/s numbers no athlete will read. And bake in the lesson of #7 for return-to-throw (Trevor's use case): **don't clear a mechanical progression on velocity alone** — velocity can be normal while the chain has quietly shifted load onto the arm.

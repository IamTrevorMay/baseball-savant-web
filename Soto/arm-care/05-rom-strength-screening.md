# ROM & Strength Screening for Throwers — What to Measure, What It Predicts

## TL;DR

- **The single best-supported prospective ROM risk factor in pro pitchers is insufficient external rotation, not GIRD.** Wilk's cohort found pitchers with <5° more ER on the throwing side were **2.2x** more likely to hit the DL for a shoulder injury (95% CI 1.2–4.1, p=.014); for elbow injury, risk rose **7% per degree of ER deficit** (OR 1.07, p=.030) and **9% per degree of lost shoulder flexion** (OR 1.09, p=.017). (proven for pro cohorts)
- **GIRD in isolation is a weak-to-null predictor and is a normal throwing adaptation.** A meta-analysis of 819 overhead athletes found injured athletes averaged only 13.8°±5.6° GIRD vs 9.6°±3.0° uninjured — a small effect (SMD 0.46) that vanished in adolescents (SMD 0.20, p=.13). Multiple pro studies found GIRD unrelated to injury; one high-school study found >20° GIRD was actually *protective*. (debunked as a standalone screen)
- **Total rotational motion (TRM) matters more than either rotation alone: keep dominant within ~5° of non-dominant.** Normative dominant pitcher shoulder ≈ 132°±11° ER / 52°±12° IR / 184° TRM at 90° abduction; a side-to-side TRM loss >5° flags elevated risk. (promising)
- **Preseason ROM screening in youth/HS pitchers largely fails to predict injury.** An 832-player HS study found *no* ROM or humeral-retrotorsion variable predicted injury (p=.15–.89); the only signal was "being a pitcher" (RR 3.84). (proven — screening's predictive value is genuinely poor at amateur levels)
- **Rotator-cuff strength by handheld dynamometry is highly reliable (ICC 0.84–0.91) and the most defensible screen to trend over time.** Pitcher peak forces run ~137–233 N IR / 122–180 N ER with ER:IR ratios of 0.66–0.90; MDC is small enough to track fatigue and return-to-throw. (proven for reliability; promising for injury prediction)
- **Grip strength (whole-hand dynamometry) does NOT predict TJ surgery or throwing-arm injury.** It is a stride-length/mechanics indicator at best; three-finger FDS-specific tests are a more promising but unvalidated alternative. (debunked as an injury screen)
- **Hip ROM is a real kinetic-chain lever, especially the lead/plant leg.** Limited plant-side hip ER at 90° flexion was a prospective risk factor for in-season shoulder/elbow pain in HS pitchers; restricted stride-leg hip IR associates with elbow/shoulder pain. (promising)
- **The ArmCare sensor is strength-reliable but ROM-unreliable.** vs lab reference: strength ICC 0.73–0.79 and r=0.72–0.81 (moderate-good); ROM ICC 0.39–0.67 and r=0.23–0.47 (poor). Trust its strength/fatigue numbers, distrust its ROM. (proven for the device's split validity)
- **Screen to establish an individual baseline and detect *change*, not to compare against population norms.** Fresh-vs-fatigued strength retention (>90% target) and within-athlete trend lines beat one-time cross-sectional cutoffs for actual decision-making. (plausible — the operational consensus, not a settled trial result)

## 1. Why We Screen At All — And an Honest Framing of the Evidence

The pitch is that a battery of range-of-motion (ROM) and strength measures taken in the offseason and repeated through the year will flag the athlete about to break down, letting you intervene before an injury. The reality is more sober: **most individual screens are weak or null predictors of injury**, and the field's most-cited "risk factors" (GIRD, deficits) are also *normal adaptations* to throwing — the same asymmetry appears in healthy and injured arms alike. This is the central tension Soto has to hold: screening has real value, but not the value it's usually sold with.

Three legitimate reasons to screen survive the evidence:

1. **Individual baselining.** The single most useful number is not "is this athlete abnormal vs the population" but "is this athlete different from *himself* last month." Fatigue monitoring (fresh vs post-outing strength loss) and longitudinal trend detection are where screening earns its keep. (plausible — operational consensus)
2. **A small set of measures with genuine prospective support** at the professional level (ER deficit, shoulder flexion deficit, TRM deficit — Wilk/Camp). (proven for pro cohorts)
3. **Movement-quality context** that informs *programming* even if it doesn't predict injury — a stiff plant hip or a locked-down posterior shoulder changes how you'd write the training block regardless of its ROC curve.

What screening does *not* reliably do: identify, at a population-cutoff level, which amateur pitcher will get hurt this season. The 832-player Matsel/high-school study is the honest baseline — **no preseason ROM or humeral-retrotorsion variable predicted injury (p=.15–.89)**; the only significant predictor was being a pitcher at all (relative risk 3.84, 95% CI 1.72–8.56). Any Neptune marketing that implies a screen "prevents injuries" is overselling. (proven)

**For Soto:** Frame Neptune's intake battery as *baseline capture + programming input + fatigue-trend infrastructure*, explicitly NOT as an injury-prediction oracle. That honesty is a differentiator with a former-MLB founder who "will discount ungraded claims." Bake the evidence grade into the athlete-facing report itself.

## 2. Shoulder Rotational ROM — The Core Measures and Their Norms

The foundational shoulder ROM screen for throwers is **passive rotation measured supine at 90° of abduction in the scapular plane**, with the scapula stabilized (critical — an unstabilized scapula inflates ER and hides posterior tightness). The measures:

- **External rotation (ER) at 90° abduction** — normative dominant pitcher ≈ **132°±11°** vs non-dominant 127°±11° (p<.001). Throwers "gain" ER on the throwing side; this is adaptive.
- **Internal rotation (IR) at 90° abduction** — normative dominant ≈ **52°±12°** vs non-dominant 63°±12° (p<.001). Throwers "lose" IR — again adaptive, largely from humeral retroversion.
- **Total rotational motion (TRM)** = ER + IR — dominant ≈ **184°** vs non-dominant 190° (small side-to-side loss of ~5–7° is normal). Driveline's descriptive numbers run slightly higher: 136.9°±14.7° ER, 40.1°±9.6° IR, 176.3°±16.0° TRM — position/method differences, but the same picture.
- **ER at 45° abduction** — dominant ≈ 102°±12° (a secondary view).
- **Horizontal adduction (cross-body)** — dominant ≈ 42°±8°; a proxy for posterior capsule/cuff tightness.

**GIRD (glenohumeral internal rotation deficit)** = non-dominant IR minus dominant IR. The textbook "pathologic" threshold is ≥18–20° of IR loss, but definitions in the literature range from 11° to 40°. The key modern refinement: **GIRD only matters when paired with a TRM loss.** "Pathologic GIRD" is best defined as >18–20° IR loss *with* a corresponding TRM loss >5° side-to-side. Isolated GIRD with preserved TRM is a compensated, ER-gained thrower — usually fine. (promising)

**ER gain** (dominant ER minus non-dominant ER, typically ~5°) and **retroversion** context matter: ~10–12° of the IR disparity between shoulders is bony (humeral retrotorsion), not soft-tissue, and is not something you stretch away. Chasing "symmetric IR" in a thrower is a category error.

**For Soto:** For the Neptune intake schema, capture ER/IR at 90° AND horizontal adduction per side, then compute **GIRD, ER gain, and TRM-deficit** as derived fields (mirrors how Triton derives VAA/pfx_in from raw trajectory). The decision flag is TRM-deficit >5° AND GIRD >18°, NOT GIRD alone. Store humeral-side context so the report never tells a healthy thrower to "fix" bony IR loss.

## 3. Does Shoulder ROM Actually Predict Injury? Grade It Honestly

This is where you must be disciplined, because the answer differs sharply by population.

**Professional pitchers (Wilk et al., prospective, Andrews/ASMI cohorts):** Here the ROM signal is real but specific.
- **ER deficit (insufficient ER gain):** pitchers with <5° more ER on the throwing arm were **2.2x** more likely to be placed on the DL for a shoulder injury (95% CI 1.2–4.1, p=.014). (proven, this cohort)
- **Total rotation deficit:** dominant TRM deficit >−5° carried **~2.6x** greater risk of shoulder injury/surgery. (proven, this cohort)
- **Elbow injury (Wilk 2014):** each degree of ER deficit raised elbow-injury risk 7% (OR 1.07, p=.030); each degree of lost shoulder flexion raised it 9% (OR 1.09, p=.017).
- **Camp 2017 (132 pitcher-seasons):** *decreased ER and decreased flexion were greater predictors than IR deficits* — reinforcing that the field's GIRD obsession points at the wrong variable.

**Note carefully:** Wilk himself reported in two separate pro studies that **GIRD was unrelated to injury risk.** The predictive variables are ER, flexion, and TRM — not IR/GIRD.

**Adolescent/high-school pitchers:** the picture flips and weakens.
- **Shanley 2011 (115 HS pitchers, 33 injured):** side-to-side **horizontal adduction difference >15°** and **IR difference >13°** discriminated pitchers at ~4x and ~6x greater injury risk, respectively. This is one of the few positive amateur ROM findings — and note it's horizontal adduction and IR *difference*, a different profile than the adult ER story.
- **Matsel/HS 832-player study:** **no** ROM or retrotorsion variable predicted injury (p=.15–.89).
- **Tyler et al.:** >20° GIRD was *protective* in HS players.
- **GIRD meta-analysis (819 athletes):** adult SMD 0.60 (95% CI 0.18–1.02) but adolescent SMD 0.20 (95% CI −0.24 to 0.63, p=.13, NS); overall heterogeneity I²=69%.

**Verdict:** ER/flexion/TRM deficits are proven risk factors *in professional pitchers*. GIRD as a standalone screen is effectively debunked. In amateurs, shoulder ROM screening is mostly null, with Shanley's horizontal-adduction/IR asymmetry the lone reproducible-ish signal. (graded as written)

**For Soto:** The Neptune report should surface **ER gain, shoulder flexion, and TRM-deficit** as the ROM "risk" line (with a pro-vs-amateur caveat auto-inserted based on the athlete's level), and demote GIRD to a "monitor / context" tier. For Trevor personally (post-TJ, ex-MLB), the ER/flexion/TRM framing is exactly the pro-relevant one — and a lost ER gain trending over a training block is a legitimate yellow flag worth deloading around.

## 4. Shoulder Flexion, Scaption & Overhead Mobility

Shoulder **flexion** (overhead reach) is under-screened relative to rotation but carries independent prospective weight: per Wilk/Camp, **lost shoulder flexion predicted elbow injury (OR 1.09 per degree)** and was among the stronger predictors in the 132-pitcher-season analysis. Practically, the ability to get the arm cleanly overhead — pain-free flexion, adequate lat/pec-minor length, thoracic extension — is the "top priority" practitioners (Driveline, Tread) list first, ahead of rotational numbers.

Measure passive shoulder flexion supine (knees bent to kill lumbar compensation) with an inclinometer, and pair it with a **scaption / overhead readiness** check. ArmCare's velocity-readiness screen uses **scaption strength >15% body weight** as one gate. Restricted overhead motion pushes the thrower into lumbar hyperextension and altered scap positioning — a movement-quality flag that changes programming even where its injury-prediction ROC is modest. (promising for flexion→elbow; plausible for scaption gating)

**For Soto:** Add supine shoulder flexion to the intake battery as a first-class field, not an afterthought — it's cheap, reliable with an inclinometer, and one of the few ROM measures with real pro-level predictive support. It also directly informs whether an athlete is a candidate for overhead-loaded strength work vs needs a mobility phase first.

## 5. Rotator-Cuff Strength Dynamometry — The Most Defensible Screen

If ROM screening is oversold, **isometric rotational strength testing is undersold** — it's the measure with the best reliability and the cleanest fatigue-monitoring use case.

**Reliability (handheld dynamometry, HHD):** intrarater ICC **0.87 (ER)** and **0.90 (IR)**; interrater ICC **0.91 (ER)** and **0.84 (IR)** — good-to-excellent. SEM/MDC are small (e.g., SEM 0.3 / MDC 0.9 in one portable-system ER protocol), meaning real change is detectable. HHD is as reliable as fixed dynamometry and more field-practical. (proven)

**Normative values (pitchers, HS→pro):** peak force roughly **137–233 N IR** and **122–180 N ER**, with **ER:IR ratios of 0.66–0.90**. Context that matters:
- **Position players are 14–20% stronger** in both ER and IR than pitchers (a real population difference — don't benchmark a pitcher against position-player norms).
- **Regional/type effects:** in 242 MiLB players, Latin-American pitchers showed 11.8% greater ER and 16.7% greater IR strength than North-American pitchers.
- On the throwing side, **IR peak force > ER peak force** is normal; a depressed ER:IR ratio (weak posterior cuff relative to IR) is the classic "at-risk" pattern to watch, though its prospective injury link is suggestive rather than proven.

**Testing standardization** (essential for trendability): fixed position (most reliable and reproducible is **seated, arm at side, elbow 90°**, dynamometer wall-stabilized via jig — the MiLB-normative protocol), same tester or a fixed rig, same time-of-day relative to throwing, make-vs-break test consistency. Position drift is the number-one source of noise.

**Fresh vs fatigued (the highest-value application):** compare pre-outing to post-outing strength; **retain >90%** is the ArmCare heuristic. This quantifies how fatiguing a session was — something pitch counts alone can't. Trend the fresh baseline over a season to catch cumulative deconditioning.

**Injury prediction:** deficits in ER/IR strength and a depressed ER:IR ratio have been *linked* to upper-extremity injury and performance decrements, and rotator-cuff strength is a valid *status/monitoring* tool — but strong prospective causal evidence that a strength cutoff predicts injury is thinner than the reliability data. Grade: **proven for reliability and monitoring; promising, not proven, for injury prediction.**

**For Soto:** This is the anchor of Neptune's monitoring spine. Spec an HHD (or ForceFrame-style fixed rig for the facility) with a locked seated-at-side protocol; compute per-athlete **ER, IR, ER:IR ratio, side-to-side %, and fresh-vs-fatigued retention** as derived fields flowing into the same Compete pipeline as TrackMan. The fatigue-retention metric (post/pre %) is the killer app — it's the arm-health analog to a velocity-readiness score and pairs naturally with TrackMan velo trends. This is also directly actionable for Trevor: a fresh-baseline ER trend line is a real deload trigger.

## 6. Grip Strength — Popular, and a Poor Injury Screen

Whole-hand grip dynamometry is ubiquitous because it's easy — and it's a **poor throwing-injury screen.** Key points:

- **Handgrip dynamometry does not predict Tommy John surgery** or clearly distinguish injured from healthy pitching populations — there's a genuine lack of evidence for a grip-strength difference between the two. (debunked as a TJ/injury predictor)
- Grip's better-supported use is **mechanical/indirect**: grip-strength measurement has been proposed as a clinical indicator of **stride-length inefficiency** — a movement/mechanics signal, not an injury flag.
- The more physiologically targeted alternative is a **three-finger (index/middle/thumb) test isolating flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS)** — the primary dynamic stabilizer shielding the UCL from valgus tensile load. This is *conceptually* better (specificity to the baseball grip and the UCL-protective musculature) but is **not yet prospectively validated** as an injury predictor. (plausible)
- Emerging direction: **strength–velocity gap analysis** (medial-elbow strength dynamometry × 3D motion capture) to see whether larger deficits predict UCL injury / flexor-pronator symptoms — a promising research thread, not a deployable screen.

ArmCare still gates velocity-readiness on **grip >15% body weight** — reasonable as one of several readiness floors, but not as a standalone injury predictor.

**For Soto:** Include grip in the battery because it's cheap, trendable, and a mechanics/readiness input — but explicitly tier it as "readiness/mechanics context," NOT injury prediction, in the athlete report. If Neptune wants a forearm-specific measure with more theoretical UCL relevance, pilot a three-finger FDS protocol and *log it as experimental* until you have your own n to look at (this is exactly the kind of in-house dataset the Compete pipeline could accumulate).

## 7. Hip & Lower-Half ROM — The Kinetic-Chain Lever

The arm is the terminal link; the hips are where power is generated and where compensation begins. Hip ROM screening has more prospective support at the amateur level than shoulder ROM does.

**Prospective findings:**
- **Plant/lead-side hip ER at 90° flexion:** limited preseason plant-side hip ER was a **prospective risk factor** for in-season shoulder/elbow pain in HS pitchers (in a cohort where 9% became disabled). (promising)
- **Stride-leg hip IR restriction** is associated with elbow and shoulder pain in elite young players.
- **Decreased hip flexion and IR (both trail and plant leg)** were risk factors for elbow pain in adolescents.
- **Kinematic linkage:** lead-hip total-arc ROM correlates with max shoulder ER; lead-hip IR correlates with elbow-flexion angle at ball release. Reduced hip arc forces greater trunk-rotation dependence on the shoulder and reduces protective elbow flexion → higher medial-elbow valgus load. The mechanism is coherent: **stiff hips tax the arm.**

**What to measure:** supine passive hip IR and ER at 90° hip flexion, per leg, with an inclinometer; note bilateral asymmetry. Total hip rotational arc and side-to-side differences are the values of interest. Pros show measurable **asymmetric hip rotation** (lead vs trail) as an adaptation, analogous to shoulder asymmetry.

**Effect-size caveat:** practitioner claims like "sufficient hip mobility reduces shoulder/elbow injury up to 20%" are directional and not from a clean RCT — treat as promising, not proven.

**For Soto:** Hip IR/ER at 90° (both legs) belongs in the intake battery — it's one of the better amateur-relevant ROM signals and, unlike shoulder GIRD, points at something you can actually program (lower-half mobility + rotational power work) with plausible transfer to arm load. Derived fields: hip total arc per leg, lead-vs-trail asymmetry, plant-hip ER flag. This ties Neptune's mobility screen directly to the velocity/kinetic-chain story TrackMan already tells.

## 8. Screening Cadence, Tech Stack & Reliability Realities

**Cadence.** A defensible schedule:
- **Full battery (ROM + strength + hip):** offseason baseline, pre-spring, and mid-season — the "multiple measurements throughout the season" the null HS study explicitly recommended over one-time screening.
- **Strength/fatigue check (fast):** weekly in-season, plus **fresh-vs-fatigued around high-intensity outings** (bullpens, starts). ArmCare's 6-minute self-guided protocol is designed for exactly this frequency.
- **ROM spot-checks:** every 3–4 weeks if trending toward a TRM/ER-gain loss; otherwise with the full battery.

The point of frequency isn't more population comparisons — it's **within-athlete change detection.** MLB-season data show shoulder/elbow ROM can be *maintained* across a season regardless of workload in well-managed pitchers, so a *drift* from an individual's own baseline is the meaningful signal, not the absolute number.

**Tech stack & their honest reliability:**
- **Digital inclinometer** for ROM — cheap, reliable *if* scapula is stabilized and position is fixed; tester-dependent.
- **Handheld dynamometer (HHD)** for strength — ICC 0.84–0.91, small MDC; the reliability workhorse. A wall-jig or fixed frame removes tester push-strength as a confound.
- **ForceFrame / fixed dynamometer rig** — facility-grade, removes tester variability, good for a lab like Neptune; validated against isometric ER/IR and athletic-shoulder tests in baseball athletes.
- **ArmCare sensor (self-guided app):** **strength ICC 0.73–0.79, validity r=0.72–0.81 (moderate-good)** — trustworthy for strength/fatigue; but **ROM ICC 0.39–0.67, validity r=0.23–0.47 (poor)** — do NOT rely on its ROM numbers. n=51 HS/college validation. Great scalable strength/fatigue tool; pair with inclinometer ROM.
- **Isokinetic dynamometry** — research gold standard for strength, but expensive and low-throughput; overkill for routine facility screening.

**MCID / MDC framing.** There's no clean, throwing-specific "minimal clinically important change" consensus for these screens. The operational substitute is **MDC** (change beyond measurement error): small for HHD strength (real change detectable), and for ROM, treat **>5° TRM change** and Shanley's **>13° IR / >15° horizontal-adduction side-to-side differences** as the actionable thresholds. Anything inside the tool's MDC is noise, not a finding — a discipline worth encoding so you don't chase measurement error.

**For Soto:** Neptune's screening product = **inclinometer ROM + HHD/ForceFrame strength + ArmCare (scalable strength/fatigue) + hip screen**, all feeding derived metrics into the Compete pipeline. Encode each measure's MDC so the athlete report greys out sub-MDC "changes" rather than alarming on noise (same rigor as not over-reading a 1-unit Stuff+ wobble). Auto-insert the pro-vs-amateur evidence caveat per athlete level. The report's headline should be *individual trend lines*, not population percentiles.

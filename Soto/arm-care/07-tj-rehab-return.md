# Tommy John Rehab & Return — Reconstruction vs. Repair vs. Hybrid, Timelines, Benchmarks, and Facility Coordination

## TL;DR

- **Three surgeries, three timelines.** Traditional UCL reconstruction (UCLR / "Tommy John") returns pitchers to competition at a mean **~13.4 months** and **11.6–16.8 months** at the pro level; UCL **repair + internal brace** returns partial-tear athletes at **~9.2 months** (practice at **6.7 months**); the **hybrid** (graft **plus** internal brace, e.g., Ohtani 2023) targets long-toss at 5–6 months and full return at **~12 months amateur / ~14 months pro** (proven for repair vs. reconstruction timing; promising for hybrid).
- **Repair only works for the right tear.** In the largest head-to-head series (Dugas et al., AJSM 2025, n=461), the repair group was **76% partial tears** and mostly high-school athletes; reconstruction skewed toward **complete tears** and college/pro. Matched for that, PROs, RTS (**98% vs. 99%**), and revision (**9% vs. 8%**, p=.77) were statistically identical — repair just got there faster (proven).
- **Return-to-play is high but not universal, and re-injury is real.** Primary UCLR returns **~83%** of MLB pitchers; ASMI notes **10–20% never reach their prior level**. Revision TJ is far worse: **13.2%** of MLB TJ patients get revised, only **~65%** return to any MLB game and **~42%** to 10+ games, with careers cut roughly in half (proven).
- **"Same speed, less trust" — year 1 back is a mirage.** Velocity and spin recover immediately, but a matched study of **91 UCLR pitchers** found FIP up **~19%**, fastball usage down ~5 points (62.8%→57.9%), and fastball run value collapsing in year 1 — then **every metric normalizes by year 2 (~24 months)** (proven). Command, not stuff, is the lagging variable (Rondon 2024: velocity/spin retained, command significantly down at 1–2 yrs).
- **Rehab is criteria-based, not calendar-based.** The modern interval throwing program (Reinold/Wilk 2024) is built on **elbow varus torque per throw** modeled against throwing distance, targeting an acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR) inside **0.7–1.3** for ~91% of a **217-day** progression (vs. the legacy 136-day program that spiked ACWR to 1.61) (promising).
- **The injury environment is the backdrop.** The share of MLB pitchers with a TJ history rose from **27.4% (2016) to 38.8% (2024)**; MLB's Dec-2024 report (200+ experts) pinned the cause on **max-effort velocity/stuff chasing**, and youth UCL surgery has risen **~10x since 2000**, concentrated in ages 15–19 (proven).
- **Peak pitching torque exceeds the ligament's isolated failure load.** In-vivo peak elbow valgus torque is **~64 Nm (range 52–76)**; isolated cadaveric UCL fails at **~32–35 Nm**. The flexor-pronator mass is the difference-maker — which is why forearm/wrist strengthening is central to rehab and prevention (proven).
- **A facility's job is coordination, not clearance.** The rehabbing thrower needs a single throwing/strength/PT record, monthly retests, and explicit surgeon sign-off gates. The classic failure modes are **separating therapy from training** and **under-progressing workload** into the throwing bridge (plausible/practitioner-consensus).

## 1. The three surgical options and what actually differs

There are now three distinct operations under the "Tommy John" umbrella, and conflating them is the single most common error in return-to-play planning.

**UCL reconstruction (UCLR).** The original Jobe (1974) operation and its descendants (modified Jobe, docking, DANE-TJ) replace the ligament with a tendon **graft** — usually palmaris longus or gracilis — woven through bone tunnels in the ulna and medial epicondyle. This is the gold standard for a **complete tear**, a chronically attenuated ligament, or a ligament of poor tissue quality. A recent systematic review put return-to-play at **80.5% (modified Jobe)** and **82.3% (docking)**, with the docking technique showing the lowest minor complication rate (**2.35%**) (proven).

**UCL repair with internal brace.** Instead of replacing the ligament, the surgeon reattaches the native torn ligament to bone and augments it with a **collagen-coated suture tape ("InternalBrace," Arthrex)** anchored across the joint to offload the healing tissue. This only works when there is **healthy tissue to repair** — typically an acute, **proximal or distal avulsion** or a **partial tear** in a younger athlete. A systematic review reported RTP between **67% and 93%** with a postoperative complication rate up to **11.9%**; internal-brace repair posted the highest same-or-higher-level RTP (**93.2%**) of the three techniques but also a higher major-complication rate (**3.01%** vs. **1.16%** modified Jobe) (proven).

**Hybrid reconstruction (graft + internal brace).** The newest option combines a full tendon graft **with** internal-brace augmentation — the reconstruction's structural replacement plus the brace's early load-sharing. This is the procedure Shohei Ohtani had in September 2023, and the technique the ASMI/Andrews group (Dugas) has now used in **400+ cases**. The rationale is to protect the graft during the vulnerable early-remodeling window so rehab can advance without over-stressing the reconstruction (promising — outcome series still maturing).

> **For Soto:** These are three different injuries and three different timelines, not one. Any Neptune intake form or Triton "return-from-TJ" flag must capture **which** procedure and **which surgeon/protocol**, because a repair athlete at month 7 and a reconstruction athlete at month 7 are in completely different phases. Don't let a single "TJ date" field collapse them.

The candidacy split is the crux. In the Dugas 2025 series, the repair-with-brace group was **76.1% partial tears / 18.3% complete**, while the reconstruction group was **55.5% partial / 40.6% complete** (p<.01). Repair is a **selection** decision made on the operating table based on tissue quality — it is not a "faster Tommy John" you can request (proven).

## 2. Head-to-head outcomes: repair vs. reconstruction

The definitive comparison is Dugas, Wilk, Cain et al., *AJSM* 2025 (n=461 competitive athletes; mean age 19.1; 92% male; ~5-year mean follow-up), from the ASMI/Andrews repository. Key results:

- **Return to sport:** repair **241/247 attempted = 98%**; reconstruction **145/147 = 99%**. No statistical difference (p=.20) (proven).
- **Time to return to practice:** **6.7 ± 3.5 months (repair)** vs. **10.2 ± 11.7 months (reconstruction)**, p<.01.
- **Time to return to competition:** **9.2 ± 4.6 months (repair)** vs. **13.4 ± 13.3 months (reconstruction)**, p<.01.
- **Revision/reoperation:** subsequent elbow reoperations were **9% (repair)** vs. **8% (reconstruction)**; true UCL revisions were only **2%** of the whole cohort, with no significant between-group difference (p=.77).
- **Patient-reported outcomes** (KJOC, ASES-E, Andrews-Carson): statistically equivalent after controlling for follow-up time.

The blunt read: **for an appropriately selected partial tear, repair-with-brace gets an athlete back ~4 months sooner with equivalent durability and function.** But the "appropriately selected" clause is doing enormous work — the reconstruction group carried the complete tears the repair group would never have been offered (proven).

> **For Soto:** The 4-month delta matters enormously for Neptune's HS/college clientele — it can be the difference between missing one season and two. But Soto should never imply a facility can steer the surgical choice. The lever a facility owns is **earlier, cleaner imaging and referral** so a partial tear is caught while repair is still an option, rather than degrading to a complete tear that forces reconstruction.

## 3. Revision and the "second Tommy John" cliff

Re-injury outcomes are where optimism ends. In MLB pitchers (Marx/Wilk-lineage data, since 1999): of **235 MLB TJ patients, 31 (13.2%) underwent revision**, and **37% of those revisions happened within 3 years** of the index surgery. Of revisions with 2+ years follow-up, only **17/26 (65.4%) returned to pitch even one MLB game** and just **11/26 (42.3%) reached 10+ games**. Careers were roughly halved: matched controls averaged **4.9 vs. 2.6 seasons** post-index (proven).

There is a hopeful counter-signal for the newest technique: an ASMI series (Richard et al., in press 2026) of **11 pitchers** who had **revision UCL repair with internal brace after a failed primary reconstruction** reported **100% return to preinjury level or higher at a mean 9.0 ± 2.1 months**, with no further revisions at ~2.9-year mean follow-up. Small n, single center, but it suggests that even revision surgery is being reframed by the brace era (promising).

> **For Soto:** The revision cliff is the strongest data-backed argument for the whole Neptune arm-care thesis. A first TJ is survivable; a second is a career-ender ~60% of the time. Every dollar of monitoring that prevents the re-tear pays for itself. Frame Triton's workload/command dashboards around **protecting the second career, not optimizing the first return.**

## 4. Performance after return — velocity comes back, command and trust lag

Two independent, recent findings converge on the same story.

**Stuff recovers; the pitch stops working (year 1).** A matched study of **91 UCLR MLB pitchers vs. 91 controls (2015–2021)** found velocity and spin **unchanged** vs. controls after return, yet in **year 1**: fastball usage fell from **62.8% to 57.9%**, **FIP rose ~19%**, and weighted fastball run value collapsed (from about −0.2 to −0.7 per 100). By **year 2, every metric normalized to baseline** (~24 months post-op). The authors attribute the year-1 gap to proprioceptive drift, protective inhibition, and lost confidence — "structural recovery does not equal functional readiness" (proven for the pattern; the psychological mechanism is plausible).

**Command is the lagging variable (Rondon 2024, 119 MLB pitchers, 2018–2023).** Return-to-play was **4% at 1 yr, 79% at 2 yr, 99% at 3 yr**, mean **558 days** to return. Pitchers **retained velocity and spin** but showed **statistically significant decreases in command** at both 1- and 2-year follow-ups; fastballs and sinkers were most affected. At 2 years they reached **~96% of pre-injury performance**, but with reduced workload and WAR (proven).

Older literature (Makhni/Ahmad; the 148-pitcher UCLR series covering 1986–2012) showed **83% RTP**, **3.9-year mean post-op career**, **3.9% revision**, and even *improved* ERA/walks/hits vs. their own pre-surgery decline — but that "improvement" mostly reflects reverting from a pre-surgery injured baseline, not a surgical enhancement. **ASMI is emphatic that surgery does not make you better** — the widely believed "TJ adds velocity" myth is debunked; any velocity gains come from being young, resting, and rebuilding, not from the graft (debunked).

> **For Soto:** This is a direct Triton metric opportunity. Trevor's platform already computes **Stuff+, command metrics, and deception**. The literature says the *return-to-TJ signature* is **Stuff+ flat, Cmd+ depressed, usage/run-value depressed in year 1, recovering in year 2.** Soto can build a "post-TJ tracker" tile that plots a pitcher's Stuff+ vs. Cmd+ trajectory against this expected curve — flagging when command recovery stalls beyond the ~24-month norm. That is exactly the "advanced metrics for RTP" thesis Rondon argued for, and Triton is uniquely positioned to ship it.

## 5. The rehab phase structure

Across ASMI/Andrews (hybrid protocol), Driveline, and standard PT protocols, the phases rhyme. Using the published **hybrid reconstruction** protocol as the reference skeleton:

**Phase 1 — Protection (0–3 weeks).** Hinged elbow brace, initially locked ~90°, opened ~10°/week toward extension/flexion; ROM from **30–90° (wk 1)** to **0–120° (wk 5)**. Sutures out ~2 weeks. Grip work, submaximal isometrics, scapular and cardiovascular work. Repair-with-brace protocols run this **6–8 weeks *faster* to throwing** than reconstruction because the native tissue is protected by the brace.

**Phase 2 — Intermediate (3–12 weeks).** Progressive ROM to full; brace off ~week 6; progressive resistance exercise; compound lifting introduced ~week 10 with **valgus precautions** (no aggressive valgus-loading positions).

**Phase 3 — Advanced strengthening (12 weeks–6 months).** Full weight room with precautions. **Two-handed plyometrics** begin ~week 15 (amateur) / week 17 (pro); **one-handed plyos** follow 2–3 weeks later. This is the phase that builds the rotator cuff, scapular, and **flexor-pronator** capacity the ligament depends on.

**Phase 4 — Interval throwing (long toss).** Begins **~5 months (amateur) / ~6 months (pro)** for hybrid; the long-toss block runs ~3 months.

**Phase 5 — Mound progression.** Begins **~8 months (amateur) / ~9 months (pro)**; bullpens → live BP → sim games.

**Phase 6 — Return to competition.** **~12 months (amateur) / ~14 months (pro)** for hybrid; **12–18 months** is the standard UCLR range, pitchers at the long end.

A critical, easily-missed constraint: the whole plan is **criteria-based, not date-based.** ROM, strength symmetry (limb-symmetry index targets ~90%+), and **pain-free** completion of each throwing step gate progression — the calendar is a guide, not a permission slip.

> **For Soto:** The plyometric-ball phase (weighted-ball plyos in phase 3) is exactly where a Driveline-style facility adds value that a hospital PT clinic can't — reintroducing high-intent, high-velocity arm action under controlled load *before* throwing a baseball. Neptune's phase-3/phase-4 bridge (months 3–6) is the commercial sweet spot: too advanced for general PT, too medical for a commodity cage.

## 6. Return-to-throwing benchmarks and the workload-based interval program

The legacy interval throwing program (ITP) — Reinold & Wilk's classic — progresses through fixed distances (**45, 60, 90, 120, 150, 180 ft**), throwing **~75 reps at each distance**, completing each step **2–3 times pain-free** before advancing. It's simple and durable but distance-only.

The **2024 update (Reinold, Wilk, et al., IJSPT)** re-engineered it around **biomechanics**, and the numbers are worth memorizing:

- Elbow **varus torque per throw** was modeled from **238,611 anonymized flat-ground throws** via a 2nd-order polynomial of torque (Nm) vs. distance (ft). Torque rises with distance/intensity.
- **Daily workload** = sum of per-throw torque; **acute** = 7-day rolling average; **chronic** = ~28-day; **ACWR** = acute/chronic, target band **0.7–1.3**.
- The **updated ITP is 217 days** (105-day flat-ground block, 45 throwing days; 112-day mound block, 75 throwing days), building to a final chronic workload of **10.8**, staying inside the safe ACWR band **91% of the program** and peaking at just **1.33**.
- The **legacy 136-day program** by contrast finished at chronic workload **15.0** and spiked ACWR to **1.61 (24% over the safe ceiling)** — i.e., the old program ramped too fast for a modern high-velocity arm.
- Structural details: throwing **3x/week through week 22**, deload weeks built in at **weeks 7, 14, 22, 29**; long toss to **120 ft by week 11–12**, then a two-week "pull-down to flat-ground" before **mound at week 16**; **fastballs start at 50% effort**, **change-ups week 19**, **breaking balls week 23**.
- A key biomechanical caveat baked into the model: **perceived-effort reductions don't reduce torque proportionally** — at 60% *perceived* effort, pitchers still generate ~76% of forces and ~84% of ball speed (Driveline/Dowling data). "Take it easy" is not a reliable load control.

**Driveline's arm-speed benchmark.** Driveline monitors **arm speed (RPM via Pulse)** as the primary early metric until roughly **700 RPM**, then blends arm speed with **radar velocity** to titrate intensity. Their program critiques traditional ITPs for (a) starting at **80 throws day one** (soreness/setbacks), (b) tracking only distance, and (c) trusting RPE. Their four-phase build ramps arm speed from ~200→350 RPM in phase 1 (25→90 throws/session) before undulating intensity in later phases.

> **For Soto:** Neptune already has TrackMan (via Compete) and the workload thesis is directly monetizable. A **Pulse (elbow-torque) + TrackMan (velo/movement)** stack lets the facility compute per-athlete ACWR and plot it against the 0.7–1.3 band exactly as the 2024 ITP prescribes. Soto should spec a `compete_workload` table analogous to `compete_pitches` — per-throw torque, daily/acute/chronic load, ACWR — so the return-to-throwing athlete's dashboard mirrors the published program. The A:C ratio is the single most defensible number Neptune can put in front of a parent or a surgeon.

## 7. Objective return-to-sport testing (beyond "it doesn't hurt")

RTS clearance for overhead athletes is trending toward **multi-construct, criteria-based batteries** rather than a surgeon's calendar date. The components (from the shoulder-stabilization RTS continuum literature, which the elbow field borrows heavily):

- **Strength symmetry:** isometric/isokinetic shoulder ER/IR, grip, and **flexor-pronator** strength, targeting limb-symmetry ~90%+. Isometric ER/IR ratio pass-rates at 6 months run **75–90% at 0°** but only **54–62% at 90°** — the 90° (throwing) position is where deficits hide.
- **ROM:** full elbow extension/flexion; shoulder total rotation (watch GIRD).
- **Patient-reported readiness:** the **KJOC (Kerlan-Jobe Orthopaedic Clinic)** overhead-athlete score — captures pain, fatigue, weakness, instability; higher KJOC predicts symptom-free throwing.
- **Throwing-specific load tolerance:** completing the interval program's mound stages pain-free at target intensity (the ITP itself is the final functional test).
- **Movement quality / kinetic chain:** trunk control, single-leg stability, deceleration mechanics.

The field's own literature admits these are **not standardized** — passing thresholds vary widely study to study. That's an opportunity, not a defect: a facility that codifies its own consistent battery has a defensible product.

> **For Soto:** This is a Neptune **assessment-battery** spec, and it dovetails with the intake/monitoring spine the facility already needs. Soto should define a fixed RTS test set (grip dynamometry, ER/IR isometrics at 0° and 90°, KJOC, ROM, and a torque-gated throwing checklist) stored per athlete, so "cleared to progress" is a data event, not a vibe. The 90°-position strength deficit is the sneaky one to instrument.

## 8. What a facility must coordinate with medical staff

A performance facility supporting a rehabbing thrower is **not** the medical provider — it's the coordination and load-management layer around the surgeon and PT. The literature and practitioner consensus converge on a few non-negotiables:

**Explicit clearance gates.** Throwing begins only with **dual sign-off from PT and orthopedic surgeon**. The facility owns the throwing progression *after* clearance, not the medical decision to clear. Document each gate.

**One shared record.** The two biggest documented mistakes returning athletes make are **(1) separating therapy from training** and **(2) failing to progress workload correctly**. The fix is a single record spanning PT, strength, and throwing — with **monthly retests** (throwing, strength, PT) and **weekly staff meetings** so the skill coach, strength coach, and clinician are working from the same numbers.

**Scope-of-practice and legal structure.** In many states a training facility **cannot employ a medical provider directly**; clinicians operate as independent contractors or via referral partnerships. Three integration models exist: **referral** (partner clinic), **part-time embedded** (clinician on-site 1–2x/week), and **full-time on-staff**. Neptune should pick deliberately and get the corporate structure right early.

**Bridge the rehab-to-performance gap.** The named failure mode is skipping the **bridge phase** — jumping from clinical rehab straight to pitching without re-patterning mechanics, rebuilding sport-specific strength, and progressively loading throwing volume/intensity. The facility's core value-add is *owning that bridge*: plyo progressions, high-intent movement, workload-gated throwing, and mechanics work coordinated with the skill coach.

**Communicate in the surgeon's language.** Surgeons think in protocols and milestones; a facility that reports **ACWR, torque load, symmetry indices, and KJOC** in a clean monthly summary becomes a trusted extension of the medical team rather than a liability the surgeon warns the athlete against.

> **For Soto:** This maps cleanly onto Neptune's **assessment → programming → monitoring** spine and Triton's data layer. Concretely: (1) an intake that records surgery type/date/surgeon/protocol; (2) a clearance-gate object with PT + surgeon sign-off timestamps; (3) the `compete_workload` ACWR engine from §6; (4) a monthly auto-generated PDF/summary for the surgeon. This is a **product**, not a service — and it's exactly the kind of leverage/template play the standing constraints favor over headcount.

## 9. The environment Neptune operates in

Context that frames every rehab conversation: pitcher injuries are at an all-time high and the cause is understood. The share of MLB pitchers with a TJ history climbed **27.4% (2016) → 30.8% (2019) → 34.4% (2022) → 38.8% (2024)** (Roegele/MLBPlayerAnalysis tracking). MLB's **December 2024 report** (200+ experts — surgeons, biomechanists, coaches, ex-pitchers) concluded the primary driver is **max-effort velocity and stuff-chasing**: average MLB fastball rose from **91.9 mph (2008) to 94.2 mph (2023)**, and TJ counts paralleled it. Notably, in-season IL placements have **declined since 2021** — most injuries now cluster **spring training through Opening Day**, implicating offseason ramp-up and inconsistent spring workloads (proven).

The youth trickle-down is the sharper crisis: youth UCL surgery rates are **~10x higher than 2000**, **>50% of all TJ surgeries are now on ages 15–19**, growing **~9% per year**; New York State data showed a **193% increase (2002–2011)** with population rates tripling (0.15→0.45 per 100k). ASMI's risk factors are unambiguous: **pitching while fatigued, year-round pitching, multiple teams, showcase velocity chasing, catching when not pitching** (proven).

The biomechanical why: in-vivo **peak elbow valgus torque ~64 Nm (52–76 range)** during the late-cocking/early-acceleration phase, while the **isolated UCL fails at ~32–35 Nm** in cadavers. The ligament survives in a healthy arm only because the **flexor-pronator mass** shields it — which is why fatigue (muscle failure) and max effort (peak torque) are the two dominant re-injury levers, and why forearm/wrist strengthening and workload management are the two most defensible prevention tools (proven).

**Cost context:** UCLR runs **~$15,000–$50,000 cash (~$40–60k list)**, plus PT at **$100–200/session, 2–3x/week for 6+ months**. For MLB teams, TJ cost pitchers who couldn't play **~$395M over 2004–2014**. The economic case for prevention/monitoring is overwhelming at every level (proven).

> **For Soto & Trevor:** Trevor lived this arc — 2017 TJ, then a multi-year late-career run through 2023 (career-high 21 saves in his final season). That's the credibility asset. The data says his return profile — velocity intact, effectiveness lagging into year 2 — is the textbook pattern, and it's the story Neptune's positioning and Mayday's content can tell authentically. The facility's differentiator isn't doing surgery or PT; it's owning the **monitored bridge** (months 3–14) that the medical system hands off and the commodity cage can't staff. Triton's Stuff+/Cmd+/deception models plus a Pulse-driven ACWR engine are the moat.

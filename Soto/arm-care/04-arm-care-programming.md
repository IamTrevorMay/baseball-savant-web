# Arm Care Programming — An Evidence-Graded Field Manual

## TL;DR

- **The strongest injury-prevention evidence in all of arm care is a warm-up-integrated mobility + cuff program, not any product.** Sakata et al. (AJSM 2019), a block-randomized RCT of 237 youth pitchers aged 9–11, cut throwing-arm injury risk by **48.5%** with 5 stretches + 2 dynamic-mobility + 2 balance drills done during warm-up — and *increased* ball speed (6.4 vs 4.1 km/h gain) (proven).
- **Volume is the dominant injury lever, dwarfing pitch-type and mechanics debates.** Fleisig/Andrews' 10-year prospective study of 481 youth pitchers found >100 innings/year raised serious-injury risk **3.5×** (95% CI 1.16–10.44); pitching with fatigue elsewhere shows up to **36×** surgery risk. Curveballs before 13 did *not* raise risk in that cohort (proven).
- **The band-brand wars are noise; dosage is signal.** The head-to-head Crossover Symmetry vs Jaeger J-Bands study (Sims, OSU 2016) found *both* systems significantly improved IR/ER ROM and strength, with training duration the primary driver — not the product (promising).
- **Weighted-ball throwing buys velocity but rents injury risk.** Reinold et al. (Sports Health 2018), 6-week program: **+3.3%** velocity and **~+5°** shoulder external rotation, but a **24% injury rate** (4 elbow injuries, several UCL-adjacent) vs **0%** in controls — and gains came from more layback, not stronger/faster arms (proven, cautionary).
- **The flexor-pronator mass is a real dynamic UCL stabilizer, but "forearm curls fix your elbow" is oversold.** Cadaver work confirms FCU/FDS attenuate ~82 N·m average valgus torque (peaks ~120 N·m, above UCL failure strength), yet a collegiate forearm-strengthening program raised grip strength and girth while leaving static medial-elbow gapping statistically unchanged (plausible mechanism, unproven direct protection).
- **Ice-and-rest as post-throwing recovery is functionally debunked;** Dr. Mirkin retracted his own 1978 RICE protocol. Active recovery — light movement, soft tissue, blood flow, low-intent throws — is the modern standard of care (promising).
- **ER:IR strength ratio and total-rotation symmetry are usable screening benchmarks.** Target ER:IR ~66–75%; Byram's 5-year study of 207 pro pitchers linked preseason ER weakness to surgery; total-rotation deficit >5° carried ~2.5× shoulder-injury risk (Wilk) (promising).
- **Arm care should be periodized like everything else, not just "bands every day forever."** Driveline's intent-tiered model (hybrid-A 80–90%, hybrid-B 60–70%, recovery <60%) and on-ramping "as long as your time off, max 6 weeks" is the defensible modern structure (plausible).

## 1. What "arm care" actually is — and the one thing that works

"Arm care" is an umbrella covering everything a thrower does to keep the shoulder and elbow healthy and durable outside of the pitch itself: band routines, cuff strengthening, weighted-ball/plyo work, forearm and grip work, mobility, and pre/post-throwing routines. The category is drowning in products, but the literature is surprisingly clean about what moves the injury needle.

The single best-supported finding is that **a structured, warm-up-integrated program of mobility + cuff/scapular work reduces youth throwing injuries by roughly half (proven).** The anchor is Sakata et al. (*AJSM* 2019), a block-randomized controlled trial — the gold standard — of 16 teams / 237 players aged 9–11 (117 intervention, 120 control). The intervention was deliberately minimal: 5 stretches, 2 dynamic-mobility drills, 2 balance drills, done as warm-up. Result: **48.5% lower** throwing-arm injury risk, and the intervention group *threw harder* (velocity gain 6.4 vs 4.1 km/h, P=.010). This kills the excuse that prevention work costs performance.

A second RCT thread (Shitara et al.) found posterior-shoulder stretching lowered shoulder/elbow injury incidence by **~36%** in high school pitchers. A group-based prevention program dropped injury rate from **3.1 to 1.7 per 1,000 athlete-exposures** — nearly halving it. The consistent signal across designs: cheap, low-tech, warm-up-embedded programs work, and adherence/duration matters more than exercise selection exotica.

**For Soto:** This is Neptune's assessment-to-programming spine in one sentence — the highest-ROI product you can ship is not a gadget, it's a *prescribed, tracked, warm-up-integrated* mobility+cuff routine with adherence monitoring. Build the compliance-tracking layer (who did their routine, how often) into the Compete/Triton data pipeline; adherence is the variable the RCTs say predicts the outcome. Sell the *system and the tracking*, not the bands.

## 2. Band work: Jaeger J-Bands vs Crossover Symmetry vs the truth

Band routines are the most visible ritual in baseball, and the two dominant brands — Jaeger **J-Bands** (roughly $12–$25) and **Crossover Symmetry** (a door-anchored system, ~$150–$230) — inspire near-religious loyalty. The honest read: **the brand you pick barely matters; the dose does (promising).**

The direct head-to-head is Sims et al. (OSU/OK State thesis, ~2016), comparing Crossover Symmetry vs Jaeger J-Bands on shoulder IR/ER ROM and strength. Both systems produced **significant increases** in IR and ER ROM and strength in the experimental groups, and the authors concluded *length of training* was the primary factor — not the apparatus. The folk distinction ("J-Bands = warm-up, Crossover = scapular strengthening") is mostly marketing: either can serve either role; the difference is where the resistance vector is anchored (hand-held single-anchor vs dual-cable door mount).

Mechanistically, band exercises earn their place because they let you hit the rotator cuff and scapular stabilizers at controlled, low loads through throwing-relevant ranges. The EMG literature (Reinold et al., *J Athletic Training* 2007/2004) tells you *which* positions bias which muscles: side-lying ER at 0° abduction maximizes infraspinatus/teres minor while minimizing deltoid contamination; prone horizontal abduction at 100° abduction with ER strongly recruits supraspinatus and posterior deltoid. This is why the classic ER/scaption/rows menu exists — it's EMG-guided (proven for muscle recruitment, weaker for injury outcomes).

The caution: bands alone are a warm-up and a low-load strength stimulus, not a complete durability program. Tread Athletics' public position is that standard J-band routines are fine as a general upper-extremity warm-up but "fill gaps from a warm-up standpoint" — i.e., useful but not sufficient, and best paired with heavier progressive loading and patterning work.

**For Soto:** Don't let Neptune's brand choice become a debate. Buy J-Bands for cost/portability at scale, standardize a *dose* (e.g., daily warm-up set + 2–3× week loaded set), and log completion. If you want a facility-anchored station for cuff strength days, Crossover-style dual anchors are ergonomically nicer for group settings. The differentiator is the prescription and the tracking, not the logo.

## 3. Rotator cuff & scapular strengthening: the Thrower's Ten and its evidence

The **Thrower's Ten** (Wilk/Andrews, ASMI) is the canonical cuff-and-scapula program — ER/IR, scaption, prone rows/horizontal abduction, biceps, wrist work. A 2025 systematic review (8 studies, 2015–2025, 291 athletes; 2 RCTs at PEDro 7/10) found it **consistently improved scapular stability, shoulder ROM, rotator-cuff strength, endurance, and sport-specific performance** across cricket, handball, baseball, badminton, and shot put (promising — broad but heterogeneous, small samples, mostly performance not injury outcomes). Standard dose in the literature: **6–8 weeks, 3×/week**, minimal equipment.

The value proposition is *balance and endurance*: throwing hammers the anterior/internal-rotation chain and demands enormous eccentric ER/decelerator capacity. Cuff work rebuilds the posterior/ER side and the scapular stabilizers that position the glenoid. The screening logic follows: the **ER:IR strength ratio** should sit around **66–75%** (0.7–0.9); an ER:IR <1 is associated with upper-extremity injury risk. Byram et al.'s **5-year prospective study of 207 pro pitchers** found preseason ER weakness predicted subsequent surgery-requiring injury — making cuff ER strength a legitimate screening and training target (promising).

Normative anchors worth storing: reported isometric Fmax ~137–233 N (IR) and ~122–180 N (ER) across HS-to-pro; ER:IR ~0.7–0.9. These give Neptune real cut-points, not vibes.

**For Soto:** This is a direct Triton-metric opportunity. An intake **ER:IR ratio** and **total-rotation symmetry** field (handheld dynamometer + goniometer, cheap) can feed a per-athlete "shoulder balance" score in the Compete athlete dashboard, flagged red below ~0.66 or when ER Fmax sits low-for-level. Pair it with a prescribed Thrower's-Ten block and re-test at 6–8 weeks to show a measurable delta — that closed loop (assess → program → re-assess) is exactly the "development lab" story Neptune is selling.

## 4. Weighted-ball & plyo work: reverse throws, eccentrics, and the injury tax

Weighted implements (Driveline **PlyoCare** balls, ~$60–$100/set; overload/underload baseballs) do two distinct jobs, and conflating them is where people get hurt.

**Job 1 — reverse throws & rebounders (arm care / decelerator training).** Reverse throws, upward tosses against a wall, and rebounder catches load the *posterior* shoulder and the deceleration chain eccentrically — training the muscles that slow the arm down after ball release, which is where a lot of shoulder pathology lives. Wrist-weight circuits do similar low-load eccentric work for cuff/elbow musculature and are safe enough to run near-daily. This is legitimate, low-risk arm-care use (plausible-to-promising).

**Job 2 — high-intent weighted-ball throwing (velocity development).** This is a different animal with a real injury tax. Reinold et al. (*Sports Health* 2018, 6-week program) is the cautionary landmark: **+3.3%** pitch velocity and **~+5° (4.3°)** shoulder ER — but a **24% injury rate** in the weighted-ball group (4 elbow injuries, half occurring the *following* season) vs **0%** in controls. Crucially, biomechanics didn't improve: "arm speed and arm strength did not increase," velocity gains came from *increased layback (external rotation)* — the same increased ER that stresses the UCL — and control-group arm strength actually rose, suggesting weighted balls may *inhibit* strength gains. Reinold's takeaway: weighted balls are "another tool… for the right person, at the right time. But that is probably less often than you think" (proven for the tradeoff).

The nuance: increased layback is a double-edged mechanism — it's *how* you throw harder and *why* the elbow is at greater risk. Reverse/plyo *arm-care* throws (low intent, deceleration bias) and high-intent *overload* velo throws should never be lumped as "weighted balls."

**For Soto:** Two implications. (1) For Neptune programming, cleanly separate "plyo arm care" (low-intent, near-daily, posterior/eccentric) from "velo development" (high-intent, low-frequency, heavily monitored, screened populations only). (2) For Triton, layback/shoulder-ER from any biomech capture becomes a *risk* feature, not just a stuff feature — a pitcher gaining velocity purely via ER layback with flat arm-speed is the exact Reinold-2018 risk profile. That's a candidate biomech-informed flag in the Stuff+/command model stack.

## 5. Forearm & grip work: the flexor-pronator promise and its limits

The elbow's UCL is the primary static restraint to valgus stress; the **flexor-pronator mass (FPM)** — especially **flexor carpi ulnaris (FCU)** and **flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS)**, with pronator teres secondary — is the *dynamic* stabilizer, generating an opposing varus torque that offloads the ligament (proven mechanism, cadaver + ultrasound). The stakes are quantified: average valgus torque in pitching ~**82 N·m** for fastballs, peaks to ~**120 N·m**, exceeding UCL failure strength — meaning the ligament alone can't survive pitching, and muscular protection is not optional. Pitchers with UCL injury *plus* concomitant FPM injury have worse outcomes.

So forearm/grip training *should* protect the elbow — and the mechanism is real. But the direct-transfer evidence is humbling: a collegiate forearm-strengthening program **increased forearm girth and grip strength yet left static medial-elbow gapping statistically unchanged** (plausible mechanism, unproven that isolated forearm curls reduce in-vivo valgus loading). Grip and forearm strength correlate with valgus stability markers, and forearm posture matters — Tread's point that a *more supinated* forearm reduces the muscles' protective force capacity is mechanically sound. But "do wrist curls, save your UCL" overstates a real-but-incomplete story.

Practical read: forearm/grip work belongs in every arm-care program (low cost, no downside, genuine dynamic-stabilizer relevance), but sell it as *one contributor* to elbow durability alongside volume management and mechanics — not a standalone insurance policy.

**For Soto:** Grip strength is cheap to measure (dynamometer) and a plausible durability marker — worth an intake field. But grade the claim honestly in athlete-facing copy: "supports the dynamic stabilizers of the elbow" (true) not "prevents UCL tears" (unproven). Trevor will discount the overclaim instantly.

## 6. Daily vs periodized arm care, and pre/post-throwing routines

**Daily "minimum effective dose."** A light band warm-up, low-load cuff work, wrist-weight/forearm circuits, and mobility are safe and beneficial near-daily — the Sakata warm-up model proves a *daily-warm-up* delivery works. This is the floor.

**But arm care should be periodized, not static (plausible).** The modern practitioner model treats arm-care load as part of total throwing workload. Driveline's framework tiers *intent*: **hybrid-A days 80–90%**, **hybrid-B 60–70%**, **recovery <60%**, and monitors both throw count and intent week to week. The **PULSE** sensor measures actual biomechanical arm stress (arm speed, acceleration) rather than raw pitch counts — the argument being that a pitch count is a proxy and true tissue load is what you want to manage. **On-ramping** rule of thumb: ramp as long as your time off from throwing, **max 6 weeks**, from low volume/intent to game intensity, letting soft tissue adapt. In-season, heavier cuff strengthening backs off in favor of maintenance + recovery; off-season is when you build.

**Pre-throwing routine (warm-up):** general → specific. Raise core temp, dynamic mobility (thoracic, hips, posterior shoulder), band activation (the ER/scaption/row menu), then a graded throwing progression (catch play building distance/intent). The point is *readiness and blood flow*, not fatigue.

**Post-throwing routine:** here the evidence flipped. **Ice-and-rest is functionally debunked (debunked).** Dr. Gabe Mirkin, who coined RICE in 1978, publicly retracted it — inflammation is part of healing, and icing blunts it and can *delay* recovery. The modern standard is **active recovery**: light aerobic/movement flush, soft-tissue work, mobility, low-load band flow, and blood flow (pro staffs use BFR); load then *use* the tissue lightly rather than freeze it and wait for the next outing. Post-throw cuff/decelerator work and light plyo can aid the recovery of the muscles that just absorbed the deceleration load.

**For Soto:** This is the Neptune workload-monitoring product and a Triton data problem at once. You already have TrackMan/Compete throw data; the natural extension is an *intent-tiered workload ledger* per athlete (throws × intent → an acute:chronic-style load ratio), mirroring PULSE's logic without the hardware dependency. Flag athletes whose acute load spikes past their chronic baseline, and gate on-ramp progressions. This is high-leverage and shippable incrementally on the existing pipeline.

## 7. GIRD, total-rotation symmetry, and mobility screening

The most actionable mobility screen is **total rotational motion (TROM) symmetry**, not GIRD in isolation. GIRD (glenohumeral internal-rotation deficit) is common and often adaptive/benign; the risk signal is *asymmetry of total motion*. Wilk's work: a **GIRD of ~18° correlated with ~1.9× injury risk**, and pitchers with a **TROM deficit >5°** vs the non-throwing side had **~2.5× greater shoulder-injury risk**; in 362 healthy throwers, TROM stayed within 5° side-to-side. GIRD >25° is associated with SLAP lesions and internal impingement (promising — GIRD as suspect, TROM-asymmetry as the better predictor).

Intervention: the **sleeper stretch** and **cross-body stretch** (and modified versions, per JOSPT) increase glenohumeral IR — the sleeper stretch produced ~**9.8°** IR gains in study settings. The nuance from Manske/Ellenbecker ("friend or foe?") is that some IR loss is a normal throwing adaptation; you're chasing *total-motion symmetry within ~5°*, not zero GIRD.

**For Soto:** TROM symmetry is a clean, cheap intake field with a published cut-point (>5° flag). It slots directly into a Neptune shoulder-screen and a Triton athlete "mobility symmetry" indicator. Prescribe cross-body/sleeper stretching to deficits, re-measure, show the delta — same assess→program→re-assess loop.

## 8. Example program structures by age and season phase

Grades below reflect the *underlying evidence*, not the templates themselves (templates are practitioner synthesis).

**Youth (9–13):** Volume management first — it's the proven lever. Enforce **Pitch Smart** age limits and required rest; the data (Fleisig >100 innings/yr = 3.5× risk; fatigue = up to 36× surgery risk) says overuse, not pitch type, is the enemy — curveballs before ~13 were *not* shown to raise risk (proven). Arm care = the **Sakata warm-up battery** (mobility + light cuff + balance), done as pre-practice warm-up. No high-intent weighted-ball velo work. Master fastball/changeup before breaking balls (~14+). This age gets **daily light warm-up, zero periodized heavy loading.**

**High school (14–18):** Add a periodized cuff/scapular block (**Thrower's-Ten style, 6–8 weeks, 3×/week**) in off-season; maintain in-season. Introduce **plyo/reverse-throw arm care** (low-intent, posterior/eccentric) near-daily. Band warm-up before every throw. Screen ER:IR (~0.66–0.75) and TROM symmetry (<5°). High-intent weighted-ball velo work only for screened, later-teen athletes under monitoring — respect the Reinold 24% cautionary data.

**College/Pro & offseason returners:** Full periodization. Off-season = build phase (heavier cuff, forearm/grip, plyo velo work if indicated, on-ramp ≤6 weeks). In-season = maintenance + **active recovery** post-outing (no ice-and-rest), band warm-up, decelerator plyo, soft tissue, workload-managed via intent tiers (hybrid-A/B/recovery) and load monitoring. Screen preseason ER strength (Byram) and TROM.

**Pre-throw (all ages):** raise temp → dynamic mobility → band activation → graded catch play.
**Post-throw (all ages):** active flush → soft tissue → mobility → light band; *not* ice-and-sit.

**For Soto — Trevor-specific:** For a former MLB pitcher, post-TJ (2017), now in a staying-sharp/demo/coaching-credibility mode (assumption per context), the emphasis shifts to *durability with minimal volume risk*: periodized cuff + forearm/grip (dynamic FPM stabilizer story is directly TJ-relevant), TROM and ER:IR self-screening, decelerator-biased plyo arm care, and active recovery — while treating any high-intent velo throwing as the highest-risk activity requiring the most on-ramp discipline. His lived rehab arc makes him the ideal credibility voice for the "volume + active-recovery + screening" message Neptune should lead with, precisely because it's the part that's actually proven.

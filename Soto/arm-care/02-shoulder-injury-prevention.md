# Shoulder Injury Prevention for Throwers — Cuff, Labrum, Range-of-Motion, and Evidence-Graded Programming

## TL;DR

- **The throwing shoulder survives forces that would dislocate a normal one.** At late cocking the joint generates ~67 N·m of internal-rotation torque and ~380 N of anterior force, with a distraction force exceeding body weight at ball release (Fleisig 1995) — every prevention decision is really about managing a repeated near-failure load (proven).
- **External-rotation *insufficiency*, not classic GIRD, is the strongest passive-ROM red flag.** In Wilk's 8-season prospective study of 296 pro pitchers (505 exams), a throwing shoulder with <5° more ER than the non-throwing side carried 2.2x the shoulder-injury/IL risk; a total-arc deficit >5° carried ~2.5x risk. Isolated GIRD and IR deficit did *not* independently predict shoulder injury (proven).
- **GIRD matters, but the 18–20° "pathological" threshold is too coarse.** A 2018 meta-analysis (819 athletes) found injured throwers averaged only ~13.8° of GIRD and a medium effect size (SMD 0.46–0.60 in adults); the signal was weak/non-significant in adolescents (SMD 0.20) (promising).
- **Scapular dyskinesis is a marker, not a cause.** The best meta-analysis (Hogan 2021, 923 athletes, 212 injuries) found NO significant standalone injury link (RR 1.07, 95% CI 0.85–1.34); an earlier meta (Hickey 2018) found a 43% elevated *pain* risk. Treat it as one input in a risk profile, never a diagnosis (proven it's weak alone).
- **Strength training beats stretching for prevention.** A 158-pitcher RCT (ages 15–17) found 9.8% injury in the ER-strength group vs 22.6% in the sleeper-stretch group (HR 0.489) — strengthening was non-inferior and numerically far better (promising).
- **Program to EMG-validated exercises at higher intensity, lower volume.** The NISMAT/Thrower's-Ten-revisited work maps each exercise to specific muscle activation (e.g., push-up-plus 90–104% MVC serratus, prone ER 76–93% MVC posterior cuff/lower trap); Driveline explicitly shifted cuff/scap work to *high-intensity, low-volume* to match pitching demand (promising).
- **The ER:IR strength ratio is the single best-validated strength screen.** A functional ratio <0.76 is an accepted injury risk factor; throwing shoulders normally run lower ER:IR than the glove side because IR strengthens more than ER — the posterior cuff is the chronic weak link (proven as a marker).
- **The macro-trend is unambiguous: MLB shoulder IL days roughly doubled (2,634 in 2005 → 5,445 in 2024)**, and MLB's own 2024 report blames velocity, pitch-shaping and max-effort — meaning prevention must be paired with workload/velocity management, not run in isolation (proven trend).

## 1. The Load Problem: Why the Throwing Shoulder Is Always Near Failure

Prevention only makes sense once you internalize the magnitude of what the shoulder tolerates. Fleisig and Andrews' landmark 1995 kinetics study on highly skilled adult pitchers quantified two "critical instants": shortly before maximum external rotation (late cocking) the shoulder produces **~67 N·m of internal-rotation torque** while the elbow simultaneously produces **~64 N·m of varus torque**; at ball release and follow-through the joint experiences a **distraction/compressive force greater than body weight** trying to pull the humeral head out of the socket, plus roughly **380 N of anterior shear** during cocking that stresses the anterior labrum (proven). Maximum external rotation itself reaches ~170–180° of "layback" — an extreme the passive restraints (capsule, labrum, cuff) must decelerate.

This is why the throwing shoulder is a study in *adaptation vs pathology*. The same repetitive load that builds performance also remodels the joint: professional pitchers show roughly **17° more humeral head retroversion** on the throwing side than the non-throwing side (proven), which *shifts* the total rotational arc — gaining external rotation and losing internal rotation without any soft-tissue injury. Distinguishing this bony, benign adaptation from a tight posterior capsule (pathologic) is the central diagnostic skill of throwing-shoulder care. Miss it and you stretch a shoulder that doesn't need stretching; catch it and you leave a modifiable risk on the table.

The epidemiology confirms the stakes are rising. MLB shoulder-injury IL days climbed from **2,634 in 2005 to 5,445 in 2024** — more than doubling (proven). Average four-seam velocity rose from **91.3 mph (2008) to ~94.2 mph (2024)**, and MLB's yearlong 2024 internal report explicitly named **rising velocity, extreme pitch-shaping ("stuff"), and max-effort delivery** as the dominant injury drivers (proven). The lesson for any prevention program: you cannot out-cuff-exercise a workload problem. Tissue capacity work and load management are two legs of the same stool.

**For Soto:** Triton already computes velocity and movement per pitch. A shoulder-risk layer should trend *velocity relative to a pitcher's own baseline* and flag max-effort clustering, not just raw mph — the MLB report's finding is that intent and shaping, not velocity alone, drive the injury surge. This is a natural Compete/facility metric to build on the `compete_pitches` spine.

## 2. Rotator Cuff Injury Mechanisms in Throwers

The cuff fails in throwers primarily through **internal (posterosuperior) impingement** and **fatigue-driven eccentric overload**, not the subacromial "outlet" impingement seen in older non-athletes.

**Internal impingement** occurs in the ABER position (abduction + maximal external rotation) of late cocking: the undersurface of the supraspinatus and infraspinatus gets pinched between the posterosuperior glenoid rim and the greater tuberosity (proven mechanism). Repeated contact frays the *articular-side* cuff and the adjacent superior/posterior labrum — hence cuff and labral pathology so often coexist in the same thrower. Contributing adaptive changes include increased humeral and glenoid retroversion and anterior capsular laxity, which let the arm lay back further and deepen the contact.

**Fatigue and cuff imbalance** convert a tolerable position into an injurious one. A cadaveric study of rotator-cuff muscle imbalance (Mihata et al.) showed that **reduced subscapularis (and cuff) force increases both forceful internal impingement contact and superior-labral peel-back strain** — i.e., when the anterior/inferior cuff fatigues, the humeral head is no longer centered and the posterosuperior structures take more load (proven in cadaver model). Clinically this matches the "dead arm" that appears late in outings: as the subscapularis and posterior cuff tire, the athlete loses dynamic centering and both impingement and peel-back forces spike.

The posterior cuff (infraspinatus/teres minor) also does enormous **eccentric deceleration** work at release/follow-through, resisting the >body-weight distraction force. Chronic eccentric overload here produces the classic **infraspinatus/supraspinatus atrophy and articular-side partial tears** seen on imaging. Pitchers with GIRD specifically show **significantly lower throwing-vs-nonthrowing supraspinatus and infraspinatus muscle-thickness ratios** on ultrasound (proven) — an atrophy signature that ties ROM loss, strength loss, and cuff degeneration into one syndrome. (Ultrasound detects cuff tears with ~0.96 sensitivity / 0.93 specificity, making it a practical facility screen.)

**For Soto:** A Neptune intake ultrasound or a validated cuff-strength delta (throwing vs non-throwing infraspinatus) gives an objective "posterior cuff reserve" metric. Pair it with in-outing velocity decay from TrackMan — a late-outing velo/spin drop is the field proxy for the cuff fatigue that drives impingement and peel-back.

## 3. Labral Injury: The Peel-Back and SLAP Mechanism

The type II SLAP (Superior Labrum Anterior-to-Posterior) lesion is the signature labral injury of throwers. Two mechanisms dominate:

**Peel-back (Burkhart):** In the ABER cocking position, the biceps-labral complex vector rotates posteriorly; the biceps anchor twists and generates **torsional shear that literally peels the posterosuperior labrum off the glenoid** (proven mechanism). Cadaveric and dynamic imaging work confirms increased superior-labral strain during late cocking, supporting peel-back as the primary throwing SLAP driver.

**Internal impingement contact:** the same posterosuperior pinch that frays the cuff frays the labrum's articular edge.

Critically, the cuff and labrum are *coupled*: the Mihata cadaveric study showed that cuff imbalance **simultaneously** amplifies impingement contact and peel-back strain — so subscapularis/posterior-cuff fatigue is a shared upstream cause of both cuff and SLAP pathology (proven). This is why "the thrower with a partial cuff tear and a type II SLAP" is a recognizable clinical package rather than two coincidences.

GIRD and posterior-capsule tightness feed the peel-back loop: a tight posterior capsule shifts the humeral head's rotation point postero-superiorly, increasing contact and peel-back forces — the mechanistic bridge between the ROM findings (Section 4) and the labral tissue failure. This is also why a genuinely tight posterior capsule (as opposed to bony retroversion) is worth restoring: it's not just about degrees on a goniometer, it's about de-loading the labrum.

**For Soto:** Distinguish this cleanly in athlete education — a SLAP is a *deceleration/cocking-position* injury, addressed by (a) restoring true posterior-capsule mobility, (b) building posterior-cuff and subscapularis fatigue resistance, and (c) capping high-intent volume. It is largely NOT addressed by "throwing harder to strengthen the arm," a common bro-science trap Trevor will want debunked.

## 4. GIRD and Total Arc of Motion — What the Evidence Actually Says

This is the most misused topic in arm care. The nuanced, evidence-graded read:

**Classic GIRD alone is a weak, over-rated predictor of shoulder injury.** In Wilk's prospective work (below), isolated GIRD, IR deficit, and flexion deficit did **not** independently predict shoulder injury or surgery in pro pitchers (proven). The 2018 meta-analysis (Keller et al., 819 overhead athletes, 226 injured / 593 uninjured) found:
- Overall standardized mean difference (SMD) in GIRD between injured and uninjured groups = **0.46 (95% CI 0.15–0.77)** — a *medium* effect.
- Adults (458 subjects): SMD **0.60**; injured averaged **15.0° ± 13.1°** GIRD vs uninjured **9.9° ± 7.9°** — a difference of only ~5.1°.
- Adolescents (361 subjects): SMD **0.20 (95% CI −0.24 to 0.63), non-significant**; injured 11.4° vs uninjured 9.0°.
- Injured athletes averaged **~13.8°** of GIRD, well *below* the traditional 18–20° "pathological" threshold — meaning that threshold misses many at-risk athletes (promising).

So GIRD is real but *modest and age-dependent*: meaningful in adult pitchers, weak in youth, and best interpreted as one continuous risk input rather than a binary "pathological" flag.

**External-rotation insufficiency and total-arc deficit are the stronger flags.** Wilk's 8-season prospective study (296 pro pitchers, 505 exams, 2005–2012) found:
- A throwing shoulder with an **ER gain <5°** over the non-throwing side → **2.2x** the risk of shoulder injury / IL placement (proven).
- A **total-arc (ER+IR) deficit >5°** vs the non-throwing side → roughly **2.5–2.6x** the injury/surgery risk (proven).
- An earlier deficit-in-ER of <5° was also linked to ~4x surgery risk in the correlational data.

The mechanistic story: throwers *should* have MORE external rotation and a preserved (or larger) total arc on the throwing side because of humeral retroversion. When the throwing shoulder **loses** that ER advantage or the total arc collapses, it signals that the loss is pathologic (capsular/muscular restriction), not benign bony adaptation. **Total rotational motion should be roughly symmetric side-to-side**; asymmetry >5° is the actionable number.

**Practical screening rules (evidence-graded):**
- Measure passive IR and ER at 90° abduction, supine, scapula stabilized, both shoulders (proven method; bubble goniometer/inclinometer).
- Flag: throwing-side total arc >5° *less* than glove side (proven risk marker).
- Flag: throwing-side ER <5° *more* than glove side (proven risk marker).
- Treat isolated GIRD as a lower-priority, continuous input — especially discount it in <16-year-olds (promising).

**For Soto:** This maps cleanly to a Neptune intake screen: four numbers (throwing IR/ER, glove IR/ER), two derived flags (total-arc asymmetry, ER insufficiency), stored per athlete over time. In Triton terms it's a tiny structured table — exactly the "assessment → monitoring" spine the facility needs. The key design choice: store the *side-to-side deltas*, not raw degrees, because the deltas are what the literature validated.

## 5. Scapular Dyskinesis — A Marker, Not a Verdict

Scapular dyskinesis (altered scapular position/motion) is ubiquitous in overhead athletes and heavily marketed as an injury cause. The evidence says: pump the brakes.

- **Hogan 2021 meta-analysis (AJSM, 923 athletes, 212 shoulder injuries):** dyskinesis present in 46% of athletes; those athletes had a 25% injury rate, but the pooled **risk ratio was 1.07 (95% CI 0.85–1.34, P=.59) — not significant**. Conclusion: scapular dyskinesis is *not* an isolated risk factor for shoulder injury (proven that it's weak alone).
- **Hickey 2018 meta-analysis:** dyskinesis at baseline → **43% increased risk of a future shoulder-*pain* event** over 9–24 months (promising — note: pain, not structural injury).
- **Reliability is only moderate:** the Scapular Dyskinesis Test shows Kappa ~0.48–0.61 (moderate–substantial) between raters; classification systems ~0.41–0.44 (moderate). Visual assessment is usable but not precise (proven limitation).
- **"SICK scapula"** (Scapular malposition, Inferior border prominence, Coracoid pain, dysKinesis) is the fatigue-driven "dead arm" end of the spectrum, tied to overuse.
- **Correcting the scapula is hard.** Reviews note that manual therapy and exercise interventions **often change muscle activation but have little influence on the observable scapular motion itself** (plausible/mixed). Scapular kinetic-chain exercise does improve serratus/lower-trap activation in dyskinetic pitchers.

**Net:** dyskinesis belongs in a *composite* risk profile (alongside ROM asymmetry and strength ratios), not as a standalone diagnosis or scare tactic. Its real value is directing training toward serratus anterior and lower trapezius — muscles that also matter for cuff force-coupling and impingement prevention regardless of whether the scapula "looks" dyskinetic.

**For Soto:** If Neptune films a slow-motion scapular screen at intake, treat it as a categorical input (yes/no/type) fused with the ROM and strength numbers into one risk score — never report it as a lone red flag to an athlete or parent. This avoids the overtreatment the meta-analyses warn against.

## 6. Posterior Cuff Strength and the ER:IR Ratio

Strength ratios are the most actionable, best-validated *modifiable* screen — more so than ROM, because you can directly train them.

**Normative and threshold data:**
- Professional pitchers' throwing arm: IR strength **0.6–4.2 N/kg**, ER strength **0.5–2.5 N/kg**, ER:IR ratio **0.44–1.16** (wide, but the population baseline).
- A **functional ER:IR ratio <0.76 is an accepted injury risk factor** (proven as a marker).
- The throwing shoulder normally shows a **lower ER:IR ratio than the glove side** — because dominant-limb IR strengthens more than ER, leaving the posterior cuff (ER) as the relative weak link (proven). This is the single most important concept: throwers get IR-dominant, and the prevention job is to defend ER strength.
- **Position players have ~14–20% higher ER and IR isometric strength than pitchers.** Latin American pitchers showed ~11.8% greater ER and ~16.7% greater IR than North American pitchers — populations differ, so use *within-athlete* and side-to-side comparisons over universal cutoffs (proven variability).
- 2025 strain-gauge normative datasets now exist for HS and college pitchers, enabling handheld-dynamometer field screening with good reliability (promising — new tooling).

**Interpretation rules (evidence-graded):**
- Track ER:IR ratio and the **throwing-vs-glove ER strength delta** over time; a *falling* ER:IR or a widening ER deficit is a fatigue/overload flag (promising).
- Absolute cutoffs travel poorly across populations; **within-athlete trend and side-to-side symmetry are more trustworthy** than a single-number pass/fail (proven principle).

**For Soto:** This is the flagship Neptune metric. A ~$150–300 handheld dynamometer or a strain-gauge arm-care device (ArmCare-style, ~$400–1,000 with app) yields ER, IR, ER:IR, and side-to-side deltas in minutes. Store the series in Compete alongside TrackMan output; a within-athlete ER:IR decline that coincides with a velocity plateau or drop is a high-value composite injury-risk signal — and it's *directly trainable*, unlike bony retroversion.

## 7. Evidence-Graded Prevention Programming

**What works (highest confidence):**

1. **Posterior-cuff / ER strength training — strengthening beats stretching.** The 158-pitcher RCT (ages 15–17, 150-day follow-up) found **9.8% injury with ER-strength training vs 22.6% with sleeper stretching (HR 0.489, non-inferiority confirmed)** (promising — single RCT, wide CI but clear direction). Practical takeaway: strengthening is at minimum non-inferior and probably superior; a program built only on stretching is under-dosed.

2. **EMG-validated exercise selection at high intensity / low volume.** The NISMAT "Thrower's Ten revisited" work grades exercises by muscle activation, letting you pick the highest-yield movements (promising):
   - **Push-up-plus** → serratus anterior at **90–104% MVC** (the top serratus exercise).
   - **Prone/standing horizontal abduction (neutral)** → posterior deltoid **93% MVC**.
   - **Prone ER / bilateral ER with scapular retraction** → lower trapezius **76–78% MVC**.
   - **Standing row + 90/90 ER** → infraspinatus **50–88% MVC**, teres minor 39%.
   - **Side plank with ER** → infraspinatus 42–62%, teres minor 67%, supraspinatus 51%.
   - **Full-can scaption** → supraspinatus 32–64% MVC.
   - **Shoulder IR at 90/90** → subscapularis 65–71% MVC (don't neglect the front — subscapularis fatigue drives impingement/peel-back).
   - Forearm pronation and ulnar deviation (73% / 40% MVC) bridge to elbow/flexor-pronator health.
   
   Driveline's public shift is aligned: they moved traditional scap/cuff work to **higher intensity, lower volume to match the demands of pitching** rather than endless light-band reps (promising). Tread Athletics programs cuff/stability at ~**3×10 and scap at 3×10–12**, dropping to ~2–3×8–15 in-season (practice-based).

3. **Restore *true* posterior-capsule tightness (only when present).** Modified sleeper and modified cross-body stretches both increase IR ROM; recent RCTs show **both reduce pain and improve IR/GIRD over ~4 weeks with no significant difference between them** (promising). Use the *modified* versions (scapula stabilized) to avoid subacromial impingement (sleeper) or scapular substitution (cross-body). Crucially: **do NOT stretch a shoulder whose IR loss is from bony retroversion** — check that total arc is preserved before attributing IR loss to capsule.

**What's oversold (lower confidence):**
- **Scapular "correction" as injury prevention** — improves activation, rarely changes motion, weak standalone injury link (plausible at best).
- **Stretching alone as a prevention program** — under-doses the strength side; inferior to strengthening in the one head-to-head RCT (debunked as a standalone strategy).
- **Universal ER:IR / GIRD numeric cutoffs applied across populations** — population variance (position, region, age) makes fixed cutoffs unreliable; use within-athlete trends (plausible-but-caveated).

**What's non-negotiable regardless of exercise choice:**
- **Workload/velocity management.** Given MLB's velocity/max-effort findings and the doubling of shoulder IL days, no cuff program offsets chronic overload. Pitch-count limits, adequate recovery between high-intent outings, and monitoring in-outing velocity decay are foundational (proven at the population level).
- **Recovery-oriented arm care.** Driveline reframed post-throwing work around blood flow / recovery rather than "band burnout" — light, high-rep recovery work has a *recovery* rationale, not a strengthening one (plausible; keep the goals separate).

**Suggested Neptune template (synthesizing the above):**
- *Assessment (intake + monthly):* passive IR/ER both sides (total-arc + ER-delta flags), ER:IR dynamometry (ratio + side delta), scapular screen (categorical), optional cuff ultrasound.
- *Prevention block (2–3×/week):* push-up-plus (serratus), prone/standing ER & horizontal abduction (posterior cuff/lower trap), 90/90 IR (subscapularis), full-can scaption — high intensity, ~2–3×8–12, quality over volume.
- *Mobility (only if true capsule tightness):* modified sleeper OR modified cross-body, 3×30 s, re-test in 4 weeks.
- *Load layer:* velocity-vs-baseline and high-intent-outing spacing from TrackMan/Compete.

**For Soto & Trevor:** Trevor's own history — a career-long throwing shoulder plus a 2017 TJ and a successful late-career return (career-best 21 saves in 2023) — is a live case study for the "adaptation vs pathology" distinction and the strength-over-stretch message. His demo/staying-sharp training should lead with posterior-cuff ER strength and subscapularis work, treat any IR loss as retroversion-until-proven-capsular, and keep total-arc symmetry as the north-star ROM metric. It's also the credibility story Neptune sells: an assessment-driven, evidence-graded system, not a band-and-broscience barn.

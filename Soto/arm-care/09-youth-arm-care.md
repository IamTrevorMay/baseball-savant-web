# Youth Arm Care — Pitch Smart, Injury Epidemiology, and the Compliance Reality

## TL;DR

- **The single biggest, best-proven risk factor is chronic overuse, not any pitch type.** Youth pitchers who threw >100 innings/year were **3.5x** more likely to be seriously injured over 10 years (95% CI 1.16–10.44), and those pitching **>8 months/year had a 500% (5x) increased risk** of shoulder/elbow surgery. Pitching with arm fatigue was associated with a **36x** higher odds of surgery in adolescents. (proven)
- **The curveball myth is debunked.** Across 10 biomechanical studies, curveballs produced *less* elbow varus torque than fastballs (youth: **31.6 vs 34.8 N·m**; HS: **52.0 vs 56.8 N·m**). The Fleisig 10-year cohort could not show curveballs before age 13 raise injury risk. The fastball is the highest-torque pitch in the youth arsenal. (debunked myth / proven biomechanics)
- **Pitch Smart is widely ignored in the field.** In a 100-team, 1,046-pitcher Wisconsin study, **94% of teams** had ≥1 violation and **48.6% of pitchers** violated guidelines — inadequate rest (43.3%) was the most common breach. Only ~**40% of coaches** and **<20% of parents** know the volume recommendations. (proven)
- **Pitch counts systematically undercount workload.** Zaremski found **42.4%** of a high-school starter's game-day pitches (bullpen 27, warm-up 24) go uncounted; catchers throw a pitcher's-worth of high-intent balls per game and carry ~**2.9x** the injury risk when also pitching. (proven)
- **Velocity is the through-line from youth to the majors.** Youth throwing **>85 mph** carried a **2.6x** surgery risk (Olsen 2006); MLB's 2024 report named velocity, early-season workload, and maximal-effort throwing as the primary UCL drivers. 15–19-year-olds account for the largest share of Tommy John surgeries, with incidence climbing ~**9%/year**. (proven)
- **The showcase/travel economy structurally rewards the risky behaviors.** Families spend **$3,000–$10,000+/year**; recruiting at showcases pushes max-effort throwing on cold, fatigued arms — an environment that inverts every arm-health principle. (plausible driver, proven correlates)
- **Education works, but only if it reaches parents.** When parents know and follow Pitch Smart, kids get hurt less; the practical lever is a shared workload language (throws, not just pitches) plus a no-taboo culture for reporting pain — 46% of "healthy" youth were encouraged to keep playing through arm pain. (promising)

## 1. What Pitch Smart Actually Says (and How It Changed)

Pitch Smart is the MLB / USA Baseball joint program launched in 2014 and refined since. Its core is a two-part prescription: **daily pitch-count ceilings** and **mandatory rest tied to the count thrown** — plus age-gating of pitch types and an annual off-ramp from throwing.

**Daily maximums by age (in-game):**

| Age | Max pitches/game |
|-----|------------------|
| 7–8 | 50 |
| 9–10 | 75 |
| 11–12 | 85 |
| 13–16 | 95 |
| 17–18 | 105 |

**Required rest, ages 14 & under** (0 days: 1–20; 1 day: 21–35; 2 days: 36–50; 3 days: 51–65; 4 days: 66+). For **15–16** the bands shift up (1 day at 31–45, 4 days at 76+). A hard rule: no pitcher 14 or under throws on consecutive days after reaching 36+ pitches. (proven — these are consensus-panel guidelines built on the ASMI/Fleisig/Olsen evidence base below.)

**Pitch-type age-gating:** fastball only ~ages 8–10; add a change-up ~11–13; breaking balls (curve/slider) only ~14+ *and* with proper instruction. **Annual rest:** 2–4 months completely off overhead throwing every year (ASMI states ≥4 months, with ≥2–3 competitive-pitch-free). **Weekly ASMI ceilings:** 75 (9–10U), 100 (11–12U), 125 (13–14U). Note the pitch-type gating is precautionary/consensus, not biomechanically driven (see §3).

**2018 update** tightened the older bands: added "no pitcher appears as a pitcher on three consecutive days regardless of count," shifted 17–18 rest to 3 days at 61–80 / 4 days at 81+, and added a 19–22 tier. The radar-gun section was expanded to warn against max-velo chasing, and the TJ FAQ was refreshed. Later supplements (2021) addressed return-from-layoff ramp-ups after COVID stoppages. (proven)

**For Soto:** Pitch Smart is the *floor*, not the program. Neptune's intake should encode these tables as hard constraints in the athlete profile (age → daily max, count → rest days, months-off tracker) and treat them as the minimum a compliant athlete clears — the differentiated product is the layer *above* it (true workload, tissue readiness, velocity-context). Note the SP/RP-style logic already in Triton (games×pitch thresholds) is a natural home for a youth "compliance engine."

## 2. Youth Injury Epidemiology — the Numbers That Matter

The foundational prospective evidence:

- **Fleisig/Andrews 10-year cohort (AJSM 2011):** 481 pitchers aged 9–14 followed a decade. **5% cumulative risk** of serious throwing injury (surgery or retirement due to injury). The dominant modifiable factor: **>100 innings/year → 3.5x** risk (95% CI 1.16–10.44). Curveball-before-13 and catcher-comorbidity trends were *not* statistically significant at this sample size. (proven)
- **Olsen 2006 case-control (AJSM):** 95 adolescents who had shoulder/elbow surgery vs 45 uninjured. Injured pitchers pitched more months, more innings, more pitches/game, more warm-up pitches (34 vs 26), more showcases, at **higher velocity**, and — critically — **more often with arm fatigue**. Pitching with arm fatigue carried roughly a **36x** odds of surgery in this dataset; velocity **>85 mph** ≈ **2.6x** risk. (proven)
- **>8 months/year:** the widely-cited "**500% increased risk**" of shoulder/elbow surgery for 9–14-year-olds pitching >8 months/year. (proven)

Symptom prevalence in the "healthy" population is startling. **Makhni 2015 (AJSM, n=203 healthy youth):** only 26% said their arm *never* hurt while throwing, only 20% never hurt the day after; 30% said arm pain reduced their enjoyment; **46% were encouraged to keep playing through arm pain at least once**; 23% already had a prior overuse injury. Baseline pain and fatigue are the *norm*, not an alarm — which is exactly why "he'll tell me if it hurts" fails as a monitoring strategy. (proven)

Little League–specific pathology: symptomatic elbow pain in **17–26%** (some reports to 40%) of 9–12-year-olds in a given year. OCD (osteochondritis dissecans) of the capitellum — the **leading cause of permanent elbow disability** in adolescent throwers — appears on ultrasound in ~**2.1%** of asymptomatic 10–12-year-olds. Growth-plate vulnerability is the pediatric distinction: in a skeletally immature arm, valgus load concentrates on cartilage/apophysis (medial epicondyle, capitellum) rather than the UCL, producing "Little League elbow/shoulder" — often reversible with rest but capable of permanent damage if pushed through. (proven)

The macro trend: UCL reconstruction volume rose ~**200%** across 2002–2011, incidence per 100,000 tripled (0.15 → 0.45), and **15–19-year-olds are now the single largest demographic** undergoing Tommy John — a 2025 JSES analysis put the adolescent share highest of any age band, with incidence rising ~**9.12%/year**. The pros' torn UCLs "wear out over time" (ASMI) — i.e., the professional epidemic is substantially a *youth* overuse epidemic with a decade's delay. (proven)

**For Soto:** these effect sizes are the empirical anchors for any Neptune arm-health scoring. Fatigue (36x) and chronic volume (3.5–5x) dwarf everything else — so a monitoring product should weight *cumulative + fatigue-state* signals far above pitch-type or single-outing snapshots. This mirrors the MLB-side finding that early-season workload spikes drive UCL risk; the same acute:chronic logic applies to a 14U arm.

## 3. The Curveball Myth vs. the Evidence

Few beliefs are as entrenched — or as wrong — as "curveballs hurt kids' elbows."

**Biomechanics (proven):** A 2014 systematic review (10 biomech + 5 epi studies) found curveballs generate *less* elbow varus torque than fastballs at every level measured — youth **31.6 ± 15.3 vs 34.8 ± 15.4 N·m**, high school **52.0 vs 56.8 N·m**. Two studies found lower proximal elbow force during arm acceleration for the curveball; *no* study found increased elbow/shoulder force or torque for the curveball. Driveline's independent analysis reached the same conclusion: fastballs are the highest-stress pitch, offspeed pitches are lower. The curveball does show greater forearm supination and different wrist kinematics, but not greater joint load. (debunked myth; proven biomechanics)

**Epidemiology (mixed, confounded):** The historical scare traces to Lyman 2002 (n=476), which reported a **52% increase in shoulder pain** with curveballs and **86% increase in elbow pain** with sliders — but Lyman's own 2001 study (n=298) found *no* association, and the 2002 effect was confounded by pitch counts (kids who throw curves throw more, in higher-leverage roles). One uncontrolled surgical series found 67% of TJ patients threw curves early — with no comparison group, meaningless. The review's verdict: "biomechanical and most epidemiologic studies do not demonstrate an increased risk"; restrictions are "based on observational data and expert opinion." (debunked as a *primary* risk factor)

**The honest caveat:** ASMI and the review both note that *young pitchers may lack the neuromuscular maturity to execute proper breaking-ball mechanics*, and a poorly-thrown curve (yanking, forearm-heavy) plausibly loads tissue badly. So the defensible position isn't "curves are dangerous" — it's "curves are low-load when thrown correctly, and the real levers are volume, fatigue, and velocity." Pitch Smart's age-gating survives as sensible *sequencing* (master the fastball/change-up first), not as an injury firewall. (plausible for the mechanics caveat; debunked for the blanket ban)

**For Soto:** when Neptune/Triton messaging touches pitch design for young arms, lead with the load data — the fastball, especially at max velo, is the torque driver. Don't let a family burn worry-budget on the curveball while the kid throws 90 max-effort fastballs on 2 days rest. If Triton ever surfaces per-pitch torque proxies (Stuff+ inputs already carry velo/movement), a "load per pitch type" view would directly reframe this for parents.

## 4. Showcase Culture and the Velocity Chase

The mechanism connecting the youth game to the MLB injury crisis is **velocity, incentivized**. MLB's 2024 pitcher-injury report (the league's landmark study) named **velocity, early-season workload, and maximal-effort throwing** as the primary correlates of UCL injury, and explicitly flagged that "chasing velocity and stuff at a younger age" is feeding the youth epidemic. Youth velocities have risen sharply: developmental data show fastball velo climbing ~6 mph/year through the mid-teens (72→78→84→89 mph from age 13→16 in a sample of future MLB arms), and it's now common to see 14–15-year-olds throwing what college arms once struggled to reach. (proven trend; proven MLB correlates)

The problem is the *context* velocity is expressed in. Showcases (Perfect Game and peers) put recruitable teenagers on a mound to throw as hard as possible, often cold, often after a travel weekend, in front of radar guns that gate scholarships. Olsen's data already tied showcase participation and >85 mph to surgery. The travel/showcase economy is enormous — youth sports is a **~$40B** industry (double the NFL's revenue), families spend **$3,000–$5,000/year** on travel baseball alone (up to $10,000+ elite), and average per-child sport spending rose **46% in five years**. That money buys more games, more tournaments, more max-effort exposure — structurally the opposite of the 2–4 month annual rest the science demands. (proven economics; plausible causal chain)

Early **sport specialization** compounds it: MLB/MiLB players who specialized early had significantly *higher* rates of upper- and lower-extremity injury and played *fewer* career games — specialization buys neither health nor durability, and likely not even a development edge. (proven)

**For Soto:** Neptune's positioning as a "development lab" is the counter-narrative to showcase max-effort culture — the sell to parents is *durable* velocity built on assessment + programming + monitored ramp-ups, not one-day radar-gun peaks. Trevor's own arc (velocity guy, 2017 TJ, multi-year return) is the credibility asset here: he can say out loud that chasing velo without a health substrate is how arms end up on the operating table. This is also a content wedge — the "velo is fine, *unmonitored* velo is the problem" message is both true and differentiating.

## 5. The Compliance Reality — Guidelines Are Not Reality

Pitch Smart is excellent on paper and largely fictional in the field. The **Greiner/Trotter 2021 study** (100 Wisconsin teams, 1,046 pitchers, 2,439 games, 8U–14U): **94% of teams** had ≥1 violation, **48.6% of pitchers** violated, **46% of games** were noncompliant. Breakdown: inadequate rest **43.3%**, same-day pitching **26.7%**, max-count **9.5%**. Pitchers in ≥5 consecutive games without a rest day violated at **2.48x** the rate of single-game pitchers. High-volume pitchers violated more in every category. (proven)

Two structural blind spots make even "compliant" counts undercount the arm:

1. **Hidden pitches.** Zaremski (115 varsity HS starts, 13,769 pitches): bullpen **27.2**, warm-up **23.6**, game **68.9** — meaning **42.4% of all pitches thrown are never counted**. Pitch counts monitor roughly half the workload. (proven)
2. **The catcher.** A youth catcher makes throws-per-game comparable to a pitcher's outing, at high intent, from a squat. Youth pitchers who *also* catch carry ~**2.9x** the shoulder/elbow injury risk (HS prospective: 15% vs 5%, IPR 2.9, 95% CI 1.03–8.12). The Fleisig cohort saw the same non-significant trend and recommended pitchers "avoid playing catcher." Catch-and-pitch is the most common uncounted-workload trap in youth ball. (proven for combo risk)

Knowledge is the upstream failure. Only ~**40% of coaches** have proper knowledge of the official guidelines; **>50% of coaches** may be noncompliant with published recommendations; **>80% of parents** don't know the volume rules (84.5% unaware of hours/week, 82.2% of months/year, 89.9% of multi-league limits). But the payoff is real: kids whose parents know *and follow* Pitch Smart get injured less. (proven)

**For Soto:** the compliance gap is Neptune's product opportunity, not just a lament. A facility that *actually* tracks total throws (bullpen + warm-up + catch reps + game), enforces rest bands, and flags catch-and-pitch double duty delivers something 94% of teams demonstrably don't. In Triton terms this is a per-athlete workload ledger keyed off the same session data the Compete pipeline already ingests — count *throws*, not just game pitches.

## 6. Catchers and Two-Way Players — the Uncounted Load

Catching is arm-care's blind spot because the throwing volume is invisible to a pitch counter. A catcher's game workload — pop-time throws to second, returns to the mound, warm-up snap-downs — accumulates high-intent reps that stress the same tissues a pitcher rests between outings. The 2.9x combined-risk figure (§5) is the headline, but the mechanism is cumulative: a kid who pitches Saturday, then catches a doubleheader Sunday, gets *zero* real rest even if his Pitch Smart "rest days" are technically satisfied, because catching throws don't enter the ledger. (proven risk; plausible mechanism)

Two-way players (the Ohtani effect has amplified this ambition at every level) need a *combined* throwing budget, not two independent ones. Practical rules that follow from the evidence: don't let a pitcher catch the same day or the day after a start; cap combined weekly throw volume; treat a bullpen + a game of catching as the workload it actually is. Position variety is otherwise *protective* — Fleisig explicitly encouraged playing other positions; it's specifically the catcher-pitcher pairing that's dangerous, because both are max-throw-density roles. (proven for the catcher caveat; proven for position variety being protective)

**For Soto:** Neptune intake should have a "roles" field and a combined-throw budget per athlete/week, with catch-and-pitch flagged as a red state. This is a concrete metric Triton can own — no consumer pitch-count app models the catcher load, and it's a defensible facility differentiator plus a content hook ("your travel catcher who pitches is the highest-risk kid on the roster").

## 7. Monitoring Technology — From Counts to Torque

Pitch counting is a crude proxy; the field is moving toward measuring actual tissue load. **IMU sensors** (inertial measurement units) worn just below the UCL estimate arm speed, arm slot, and **peak elbow varus/valgus torque per throw**, converting the whole throwing day into a cumulative "workload number" scaled to the athlete's height/weight. **Driveline PULSE** (the productized descendant of the mocap-validated motusBASEBALL sleeve) retails ~**$320**, captures peak valgus torque per throw, and models acute:chronic workload — the same overtraining/undertraining logic used in elite sport. Handheld dynamometers + app-based shoulder ROM/strength screens add a cheap fatigue-readiness layer. (promising — validation is good for relative/within-athlete trends; absolute torque accuracy vs mocap is imperfect, so trend it, don't over-trust the absolute N·m.)

The value for youth isn't the vanity number — it's (a) catching the **hidden 42%** of workload, (b) quantifying the catch-and-pitch double duty, and (c) giving parents an *objective* signal to override "he says he's fine." This directly attacks the two proven killers: cumulative volume and pitching-through-fatigue. (promising)

**For Soto:** this is the Neptune tech spine. TrackMan (already in hand via Compete) covers velocity/movement/stuff context; an IMU layer (PULSE-class, evaluate for facility fleet) covers per-throw torque and cumulative workload; dynamometry covers readiness. All three feed the same Triton athlete record. The differentiated metric to build: a **youth arm-health score** blending cumulative workload (chronic), acute spike (acute:chronic), fatigue-state flags, velocity-relative-to-age, and role/catcher exposure — weighted by the effect sizes in §2 (fatigue and volume heaviest). That's a genuinely novel facility product, not a rebadged pitch counter.

## 8. Parent & Coach Communication Frameworks

Since knowledge deficits among parents/coaches are a *primary driver* of youth injury (§5), the intervention that matters most isn't a new stretch — it's communication. The Fury/Chang/Eliasberg 2025 sports-medicine framework organizes it into three pillars: **education, communication, and technology.**

Practical framework for a facility or coach:

- **Reframe the metric with parents: throws, not pitches.** Teach families that game pitches are ~half the real workload; give them a one-number weekly throw budget by age. This converts an abstract rule into a household routine.
- **Normalize pain reporting — kill the taboo.** Disclosing arm pain was "historically considered taboo"; 46% of healthy kids were pushed to play through it (Makhni). The cultural fix is making "my arm's tired" a *neutral, expected* data point, not a weakness. Coaches should ask, not wait to be told.
- **Set the annual calendar first.** Lock the 2–4 month throwing off-ramp into the schedule before the season/showcase calendar fills it. Frame specialization data honestly: early specialization → *more* injury and *fewer* career games, no development edge (§4).
- **Contextualize velocity.** Give parents the Olsen number (>85 mph ≈ 2.6x risk) and the MLB-report framing so a radar reading is understood as a risk signal, not just a bragging right. Durable velo > peak velo.
- **Debunk the curveball distraction directly** so worry-budget goes to volume/fatigue/velocity, where it belongs.
- **Use tech as a shared, objective language.** A workload number or readiness screen lets a parent, coach, and kid look at the *same* data instead of arguing about feelings — the practical unlock of the technology pillar.

(promising — education/communication interventions have evidence of reducing injury when they reach parents specifically, but adherence remains the bottleneck.)

**For Soto:** this section is effectively Neptune's parent-facing content and onboarding script. Trevor is the ideal messenger — a pro who threw hard, had TJ, and came back can say "I lived the end of this pipeline" with authority no cage-barn coach has. The through-line for every message: *the boring stuff (volume, rest, fatigue) is what actually protects arms; the scary stuff (curveballs) mostly doesn't matter; and we measure it so you don't have to guess.*

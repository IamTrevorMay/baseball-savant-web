# Recovery Modalities — An Honest Evidence Review

## TL;DR

- **Almost every recovery modality "works" for the same modest thing — perceived soreness — and almost none of them meaningfully accelerate the return of force.** Effect sizes for pain/DOMS cluster around g/SMD −0.4 to −0.7 across cold water, contrast, compression, and percussion, while strength recovery effects are consistently non-significant (e.g., CWI on strength SMD 0.12–0.74, all p>0.3). Soreness is real, but it is the cheapest outcome to move and the least predictive of readiness. (proven)
- **Cold-water immersion after lifting blunts hypertrophy — this is now meta-analytically established.** Pooled comparative effect cSMD ≈ −0.22 (95% CrI −0.47 to 0.04) across 8 studies; RT-alone gains (SMD 0.36) shrink to near-zero with CWI+RT (SMD 0.14). Mechanism: suppressed satellite cells, ribosomal biogenesis, and muscle protein synthesis (MPS down for ~5h) after 10°C/10–20 min immersion. (proven)
- **CWI genuinely helps acute recovery from eccentric/high-intensity work — power comes back faster.** Muscular power 24–72h post-eccentric: SMD 0.34 → 0.47 → 0.62 (all p≤0.02, GRADE high). DOMS reductions g ≈ −0.40, CK g ≈ −0.24. Best dose ≈ 10–15 min at 5–15°C. Use it in-season, avoid it in a build block. (proven)
- **Heat/sauna is the most underrated tool and the one with real long-term upside** — plasma-volume expansion ~5.6% (Bayesian meta-regression, 211 studies), cardiovascular fitness gains, and in Laukkanen's 2,315-man cohort a dose-dependent drop in cardiac and all-cause mortality (sessions ≥19 min > 11–18 min). Unlike cold, heat does not obviously blunt strength adaptation. (proven for cardio-health; promising for muscle adaptation)
- **Pneumatic compression (Normatec) and percussion guns are soreness/ROM tools, not recovery accelerators.** IPC: pain ES ≈ −0.45, CK ES ≈ 0.36, everything else trivial; ~80 mmHg, 20–30 min. Massage guns: large acute ROM gains but no reliable strength/power benefit and a small risk of blunting explosive output if used immediately pre-performance. (promising for soreness/ROM; debunked for performance recovery)
- **Cupping and most "myofascial decompression" claims rest on low-quality, unblinded trials** — reviews repeatedly conclude cupping is ineffective for musculoskeletal pain in athletes once controls and blinding are demanded. (plausible at best; largely debunked)
- **The RICE/ice-after-pitching model is dead on the adaptation front** — Mirkin retracted it in 2015; icing blunts the IGF-1/inflammatory signaling that drives repair. Driveline replaced passive ice with active recovery + non-fatiguing e-stim (Marc Pro), used daily; all 30 MLB teams own Marc Pro units. (proven that inflammation is required for adaptation; e-stim benefit itself is promising, not proven)
- **The single most important framing: recovery vs. adaptation is a tradeoff, not a synergy.** Anything that suppresses inflammation and blood flow (ice, NSAIDs) speeds how you *feel* tomorrow at the cost of the *stimulus* you trained for. The decision rule is entirely about calendar position — game week vs. build block. (proven)

## The Core Tradeoff: Recovery Is Not Free

Every modality in this document sits on one axis: **how much do you want to dampen the post-exercise inflammatory and blood-flow response?** Post-exercise inflammation is not damage to be suppressed — it is the courier system for repair. Macrophages release IGF-1; the exercise-induced rise in inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) triggers satellite-cell activation, ribosomal biogenesis, and elevated muscle protein synthesis (proven). Anything that reliably blunts that cascade — ice, cold-water immersion, NSAIDs — will make you feel better tomorrow while partially erasing the adaptive signal you paid for in the session (proven).

That means the *same* intervention is correct or incorrect depending only on where you are on the calendar. During a competition week, you want the athlete to feel and perform their best in 24–48h, and you accept a small adaptive tax — cold and compression are appropriate. During an off-season build block, the adaptation *is* the point, and blunting it is self-sabotage — heat, movement, sleep, and food are appropriate, and cold should be pushed several hours away from the lifting/throwing stimulus or skipped (proven). Sportsmith's in-season vs. adaptation-phase periodization captures this: prioritize heat weeks 1–3 of pre-season, introduce contrast weeks 4–6, and shift to cold-focused protocols only near competition (promising).

**For Soto:** This is the organizing principle for Neptune's recovery product and for anything Soto recommends to Trevor. The recovery prescription should be a *function of phase and goal*, generated from the athlete's programming block, not a fixed "menu of modalities." A cold plunge offered to a 15-year-old in a velocity-build block is actively counterproductive; the same plunge the night before a showcase is smart. Build the phase logic into the programming templates, not into a signage list on the wall.

## Cold-Water Immersion (CWI)

CWI is the most-studied modality and the clearest illustration of the tradeoff.

**Acute recovery (the upside).** After eccentric or high-intensity exercise, CWI genuinely speeds the return of muscular power. In the Springer meta-analysis vs. passive recovery: power at 24h post-eccentric SMD 0.34 (95% CI 0.06–0.62, p=0.018), 48h SMD 0.47 (0.22–0.73, p<0.001), 72h SMD 0.62 (0.27–0.98, p=0.001), all GRADE high (proven). Broader pooling shows DOMS reduced g ≈ −0.40 (95% CI −0.64 to −0.16) and creatine kinase g ≈ −0.24 (−0.37 to −0.10) (proven). A network meta-analysis on dose suggests moderate-duration, lower-temperature protocols — roughly 10–15 min at 5–10°C — are most effective for restoring jump performance (promising). Practical consensus lands near **10–15 min at 10–15°C within ~2 hours** of the session.

**Strength recovery (the non-effect).** The same meta-analysis found *no* significant effect on strength recovery: post-eccentric 24h SMD 0.74 but 95% CI −0.78 to 2.26 (p=0.34); post-high-intensity SMD 0.12 (−0.38 to 0.62, p=0.64) (proven null). CWI restores *power/output* and *feel*, not maximal force.

**The hypertrophy interference (the cost).** The 2024 meta-analysis ("Throwing cold water on muscle growth," 8 studies) is now the canonical citation: RT-alone hypertrophy SMD 0.36 (95% CrI 0.10–0.61) collapses to SMD 0.14 (−0.08 to 0.36) when CWI is added, comparative cSMD −0.22 (−0.47 to 0.04), with p(<0)=0.957 (proven, though study quality was fair-to-poor: mean SMART-LD 9.8/20, zero "good" studies). Mechanism is well-characterized: 10°C/10–20 min immersion 3–15 min post-lift reduces MPS for up to 5h and suppresses satellite-cell activity for up to 48h (Roberts et al., J Physiol 2015) (proven). Note the paradox — for *endurance* adaptation, cold may *augment* mitochondrial-biogenesis and angiogenesis signaling, though downstream aerobic performance effects are equivocal (plausible).

**For Soto:** For a pitcher, the throwing session *is* the strength/power stimulus you're trying to bank. A hard bullpen or a max-velo pulldown day should not be chased with an ice bath if the goal is to build. Reserve CWI for in-season back-to-backs, tournament clusters, and the night before a start where feeling fresh outweighs the adaptive tax. In Triton terms, phase this against workload monitoring — if an athlete is in an acquisition block (rising Stuff+/velo targets), flag cold immersion within ~4–6h of throwing as contraindicated in the programming layer.

## Heat and Sauna

Heat is the mirror image of cold and, for a developing athlete, arguably the better default.

**Cardiovascular and endurance adaptation.** Passive heat (sauna, hot-water immersion) drives plasma-volume expansion — a 2025 Bayesian meta-regression across 211 heat-acclimation studies pooled PV expansion at ~5.6% (proven), most of it within the first 5–10 sessions with a smaller second-stage gain to ~day 14. Adaptations include lower resting/exercise HR, better stroke-volume maintenance, improved skin blood flow and sweat response, and higher thermal tolerance. Regular sauna added to exercise increased cardiorespiratory fitness and lowered systolic blood pressure in an RCT (proven). Adaptations decay fast — roughly 1 day of acclimation lost per 2 days without exposure.

**Long-term health.** Laukkanen's KIHD cohort (2,315 Finnish men, 20.7y follow-up) found dose-dependent reductions in sudden cardiac death, fatal CHD, CVD, and all-cause mortality, independent of standard confounders; sessions ≥19 min conferred more protection than 11–18 min, and 4–7×/week associated with a 77% lower risk of psychotic disorders (proven association; observational, so causation is promising not proven). Finnish protocol norm: 1–3 bouts of 5–20 min with cooling between.

**Muscle and hormones.** Passive heat raises growth hormone, cortisol, and renin acutely (proven), and — critically — does *not* carry cold's documented hypertrophy-blunting signature; some evidence suggests heat may *protect* muscle during disuse and support adaptation (promising). Direct tendon/collagen benefits in humans are under-evidenced (plausible).

**For Soto:** Sauna/heat is the recovery modality most aligned with a *development* mission because it adds an adaptive stimulus rather than subtracting one — and it's a strong Neptune facility differentiator. If Neptune builds one thermal asset, a sauna has a better evidence-to-cost story for a youth/HS/college-offseason clientele than a plunge, and it doubles as heat-acclimation for summer-season durability. Program it away from immediately pre-throwing (acute heat + fluid shifts aren't ideal right before high-output work).

## Contrast Water Therapy (CWT)

Alternating hot and cold. A 2024 meta-analysis (13 studies) found CWT beat passive recovery for soreness (g ≈ 0.72) and perceived fatigue (g ≈ 0.74), and reduced strength loss at every timepoint from <6h to 96h (proven vs. passive). The catch, established across reviews: **CWT is no better than any other active modality** — CWI, warm immersion, compression, active recovery, and stretching all land in the same band, with "little evidence for a superior treatment" (proven). A common protocol is ~7 rounds of 1–2 min alternating, finishing on cold.

Because contrast includes a heat component and shorter cold exposures, it likely blunts adaptation *less* than a full cold plunge while still delivering the soreness/feel benefit — which is why periodization frameworks slot contrast into the transition weeks between a heat-dominant build phase and a cold-dominant competition phase (plausible).

**For Soto:** Contrast is a reasonable "middle setting" — the modality to recommend when an athlete wants the psychological and soreness benefit of cold without fully paying the adaptation tax. But don't oversell it as superior; it's a preference/compliance play, not a physiological edge over cheaper active recovery.

## Pneumatic Compression (Normatec)

The boots. Evidence is honest and unglamorous: a systematic review/meta-analysis found statistically significant but *small* effects for soreness/pain (ES ≈ −0.45, 95% CI −0.65 to −0.25) and CK (ES ≈ 0.36), with everything else — power, performance, other damage markers — trivial and non-significant (ES −0.17 to trivial) (proven). Reviewers repeatedly conclude IPC gives "short-term relief of DOMS" but "does not provide continued relief from exercise-induced muscle damage" and that evidence is "insufficient for definitive clinical recommendation." Common protocol: ~20–30 min at ~80 mmHg.

The realistic value proposition is: a passive, pleasant, zero-effort way to reduce next-day soreness and give athletes a recovery ritual they'll actually comply with — plus 20–30 min of forced stillness that itself aids rest. It is not moving the needle on tomorrow's force output.

**For Soto:** Normatec is a *compliance and experience* product, not a performance product. For Neptune, its value is athlete experience, recovery-ritual buy-in, and a visible "we invest in your body" signal — worth it for that, not for a measurable performance delta. Don't let marketing claims drive a metric on the athlete dashboard; there's no defensible readiness signal here.

## Percussive Therapy (Massage Guns)

A critical review of 39 studies concluded percussion guns effectively increase flexibility and reduce DOMS but "do not significantly impact muscle activation or force production" (proven). A 2023 review of 11 studies: acute short-term ROM/flexibility gains are real, but there is *no clear benefit — and sometimes a small decline —* for strength, balance, acceleration, agility, and explosive output (proven). Percussion modestly reduces soreness when applied post-exercise or 24–72h later; it does not restore strength/power faster (proven for soreness/ROM; null for performance).

The one genuine caution: because it can transiently reduce explosive output, don't hammer a muscle with a gun immediately before a performance that needs peak power (plausible). Post-session and between-session use for soreness/ROM is fine.

**For Soto:** Treat massage guns like foam rolling with a motor — a cheap, athlete-owned, warm-up/mobility and soreness tool. Fine to recommend broadly; just steer athletes away from percussing the throwing arm right before max-intent throwing.

## Cupping / Myofascial Decompression

The weakest evidence base in this document. Multiple systematic reviews conclude cupping is **ineffective** for musculoskeletal pain in athletic and general populations once methodological quality is accounted for, and that most positive findings come from unblinded trials comparing cupping to *no treatment* (proven weakness of the evidence). A few single-session studies report acute DOMS/ROM improvements vs. self-myofascial release (plausible), and chronic-back-pain reviews show moderate pain reduction — but that's a different population than a healthy athlete recovering from throwing. Documented risks: hematoma, site pain, occasionally *increased* pain, tingling.

**For Soto:** Do not build any Neptune protocol or Triton readiness signal around cupping. If an athlete likes it and it's applied safely, it's a low-cost placebo-plus-mild-ROM tool — but it should never displace evidence-backed modalities or appear in a data-driven recommendation engine. Grade: plausible for subjective ROM/soreness, debunked as a performance-recovery intervention.

## E-Stim (Marc Pro) and the Death of Post-Pitching Ice

**The icing reversal.** Gabe Mirkin coined RICE in 1978 and publicly retracted the "Ice" and full "Rest" components in 2015: ice constricts vessels, shuts off the inflammatory delivery of healing cells, and blunts the IGF-1 signal that drives repair (proven as mechanism). For pitchers specifically, post-throwing soreness *is* the adaptive inflammatory process; suppressing it with passive ice trades away the training effect. This is why the most data-driven programs abandoned reflexive post-throwing ice.

**Marc Pro's active-recovery model.** Driveline replaced passive ice with active recovery + non-fatiguing electrical muscle stimulation, using Marc Pro *daily*; the device reserves cold-compression (PowerPlay) only for genuine post-surgical/traumatic swelling (documented practice). Marc Pro's mechanism is a low-frequency, non-fatiguing waveform that drives a muscle-pump without adding fatigue — flushing metabolites, pushing oxygenated blood in, raising nitric oxide, and (claimed) promoting angiogenesis with repeated use. Reported effect: conditioned limbs showed ~247% increase in limb blood flow vs. ~200% in control (single-application) (promising). Kyle Boddy's internal Driveline test split pitchers into Marc Pro vs. no-Marc-Pro after Monday off-season sessions and reported faster restoration of grip strength and internal-rotation power in the Marc Pro group — both markers tied to command and injury risk (promising, internal/unpublished). Over 100 pro teams, including all 30 MLB clubs, own Marc Pro units.

**Evidence honesty.** The direct RCT base for non-fatiguing EMS is thin. The most-cited supporting pilot (2011) reported reduced soreness and better next-day performance, but **four authors were paid Marc Pro consultants** — a real conflict. Dupuy et al.'s 2018 meta-analysis found electrostimulation helped perceived fatigue and damage markers modestly (promising). The *mechanistic rationale* (circulation without fatigue) is sound; the *outcome evidence* is suggestive, not proven.

**For Soto:** The defensible position for Neptune and for Trevor: (1) drop reflexive post-throwing ice as a default — it's the clearest "stop doing this" in arm care (proven); (2) use active recovery + Marc Pro as the post-throwing default, understanding the evidence is mechanism-strong / outcome-thin; (3) reserve cold-compression for true acute swelling. Boddy's grip-strength / IR-power readout is a *great* Triton-native idea — those are cheap, objective, daily-trackable recovery markers Soto could operationalize into a facility readiness metric far more honestly than any modality's marketing claim.

## Foam Rolling and Self-Myofascial Release

The best evidence-to-cost ratio of any modality here, precisely because expectations are calibrated low. Meta-analysis: foam rolling produces a *large* acute ROM effect (d = 0.76, 95% CI 0.55–0.98), effective on hamstrings and quads (less so ankle/triceps surae) (proven). For recovery, it slightly attenuates exercise-induced drops in sprint and strength performance and reduces muscle-pain perception (proven, small). Crucially, it has **no detrimental effect** on performance measures — unlike static stretching or aggressive percussion, you can foam roll pre-activity without a power penalty (proven). It does *not* meaningfully build long-term flexibility or drive recovery beyond the pain/ROM window (the effects are "minor and partly negligible" outside those).

**For Soto:** Foam rolling is the zero-risk, zero-cost, athlete-owned baseline every Neptune athlete should have in their warm-up and cooldown. It won't show up as a performance metric, and that's fine — its job is ROM prep and soreness management, and it does that without stealing adaptation. Recommend universally; expect little; risk nothing.

## Grading Summary and Practical Hierarchy

Ranked by honest evidence and by fit for a *development* (not merely in-season) mission:

1. **Sleep, nutrition, hydration** (not a "modality," but the reminder every source repeats): these dominate everything below and are the precondition, not the supplement (proven).
2. **Heat / sauna** — adds adaptation, strong cardiovascular-health evidence, no strength-blunting; best development-aligned tool (proven for cardio-health, promising for muscle).
3. **Foam rolling** — cheap, safe, real acute ROM, no downside (proven, modest).
4. **Active recovery + Marc Pro (post-throwing)** — replaces ice, mechanism-sound, outcome-thin (promising).
5. **Contrast therapy** — soreness/feel benefit, less adaptation tax than pure cold, no superiority over other active modes (proven vs. passive; not superior).
6. **CWI** — real acute power/soreness recovery, real hypertrophy cost; a scalpel for in-season use only (proven both directions).
7. **Pneumatic compression** — small soreness/CK benefit, otherwise a compliance/experience product (proven-small).
8. **Percussion guns** — ROM/soreness yes, performance no, mild pre-performance caution (promising for ROM/soreness).
9. **Cupping** — weak, low-quality evidence; do not systematize (plausible at best; largely debunked).

The through-line: **the modalities that make you feel best fastest (ice, compression) are the ones most likely to cost you adaptation, and the ones that add adaptation (heat, movement) ask you to tolerate feeling a bit worse.** For a facility whose entire value proposition is *development*, the recovery philosophy should bias toward heat, movement, sleep, and food, and treat cold/compression as in-season, phase-gated tools — the opposite of the ice-everything default most youth players arrive with.

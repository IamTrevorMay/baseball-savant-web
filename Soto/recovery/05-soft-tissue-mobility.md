# Soft Tissue & Mobility Work for Recovery — What Moves the Needle for Throwers

## TL;DR

- **Massage is the single best-supported soft-tissue recovery modality for soreness.** A meta-analysis of 11 RCTs / 504 subjects found massage cut DOMS with a standardized mean difference (SMD) of **−1.16 overall**, peaking at **−1.51 at 48h** and **−1.46 at 72h**, and reduced creatine kinase (SMD −0.64) while nudging force back up (max isometric force SMD +0.56, peak torque +0.38) (proven).
- **Stretching is the weakest of the mainstream recovery tools.** The 2025 umbrella review of 29 systematic reviews / 863 RCTs / 24 therapies flatly states evidence for stretching, exercise, and e-stim is "weak"; post-exercise stretching shows **no significant effect on soreness at 24/48/72h** vs passive rest (GRADE certainty: low) (debunked as a recovery modality).
- **Foam rolling reliably buys range of motion and blunts pain, but not much else.** Meta-analysis shows a **large ROM effect (d = 0.76)** and reduced pain perception, with negligible effect on jump or strength; self-myofascial release cut DOMS ~50% vs ~20% control and added ~4.2% hip ROM (promising for ROM/pain, plausible for recovery).
- **Massage has a real mechanism, not just placebo.** Crane 2012 (Science Translational Medicine) biopsied quads and showed 10 minutes of massage attenuated inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6), reduced NF-κB nuclear accumulation, and up-regulated mitochondrial biogenesis (PGC-1α) via FAK/ERK1/2 mechanotransduction (proven mechanism, modest magnitude).
- **Percussion guns roughly match or beat massage/stretch for DOMS, and dose matters.** A 2025 RCT found **40-min** percussion sessions produced lower 48h pain, better knee ROM, and better jump than short sessions or static stretching (promising).
- **Throwing itself creates measurable, transient motion loss that recovery work targets.** Minor-league starters lost IR (**49.4°→46.0°** immediately, resolved by 24h) and gained ER (**150.7°→154.0°**, persisting past 24h); elbow injury risk rises ~7% per degree of lost shoulder ER, and GIRD >18–20° or total-rotation deficit >5° flags elevated injury risk (proven association).
- **The elite-facility trend is away from high-volume post-throw "arm care" toward higher-intensity/lower-volume work plus dynamic mobility.** Driveline moved band/tube arm care into the lift and replaced post-throw endurance circuits with a dynamic cool-down after concluding the old protocol didn't meaningfully reduce soreness or next-day performance loss (promising, practice-led).
- **Self-administered ≈ practitioner for most recovery endpoints — the gap is targeting depth, real-time adjustment, and adherence, not the core effect.** Foam rolling matches manual massage on ROM, pain, and perceived recovery; therapist hands win on deep tissue, multi-region sessions, and load individualization (plausible).

## 1. The Hierarchy of Evidence: What Actually Works

The cleanest map of the field is the 2025 umbrella and mapping review (Dupuy-style meta-meta-analysis) covering **29 systematic reviews, 863 unique RCTs, and 24 distinct physiotherapeutic treatments** for delayed-onset muscle soreness. Two facts frame everything below. First, the evidence base is shaky: **17 of 29 reviews were rated critically low quality on AMSTAR-2, and only 2 achieved high quality.** Second, despite that noise, a rough ranking emerges. Pooled effect sizes (Hedges' g) ranged from **0.36 (cooling) to 1.82 (heat)**, and the therapies with the strongest evidence class (II–III) were **massage, kinesiotaping, compression, contrast therapy, cryostimulation, and phototherapy.** The explicit loser: "Evidence for stretching, exercises and electrical stimulation is weak" (proven — this is the highest-tier synthesis available).

The timeline matters as much as the ranking. The umbrella review found the effective window is modality-specific: contrast and cooling help immediately post-exercise; **massage and cooling dominate at 24h**; compression, contrast, and kinesiotaping at 48h; kinesiotaping at 72h; compression and phototherapy out at 96h. For a thrower whose next meaningful output is 24–72h away, that puts massage-family and compression tools squarely in the useful window (proven).

**For Soto:** This hierarchy is the backbone of any Neptune recovery menu. Sell massage/percussion and compression as the evidence-forward core; position stretching honestly as mobility maintenance, not a soreness cure. When athletes ask "does X work," anchor to the 24-and-48h windows, because that's where their next bullpen or start actually lands.

## 2. Massage: The Best-Supported Modality (and Why)

The anchor trial-set is the Frontiers in Physiology 2017 meta-analysis: **11 RCTs, 23 data points, 504 participants**, individual studies of 15–30 subjects. Massage after strenuous exercise reduced soreness with SMD **−0.61 at 24h**, **−1.51 at 48h**, **−1.46 at 72h**, and **−1.16 overall** — the 48h effect is genuinely large by Cohen's conventions. Beyond pain, massage improved **maximal isometric force (SMD +0.56)** and **peak torque (SMD +0.38)**, and reduced **creatine kinase (SMD −0.64 overall, −1.69 at 72h)**, suggesting it's not purely an analgesic. Effective session durations in the pooled trials ran **6–30 minutes** depending on the muscle area treated. Caveats are real: low methodological quality, only ~18% of trials used masked outcome assessors, and high heterogeneity (proven for soreness/CK; promising for performance restoration).

A separate synthesis on massage technique found **deep-tissue massage produced greater pain reduction than Swedish, sports-specific, or myofascial approaches**, with bi-weekly 40–60 min sessions the most favorable protocol (plausible — smaller evidence base).

The mechanism question was largely settled by Crane et al. 2012 in *Science Translational Medicine*. Eleven young men did exercise-induced muscle damage; one quad got 10 minutes of massage, the other served as control, with **muscle biopsies at baseline, immediately post-massage, and after 2.5h recovery.** Massage activated mechanotransduction (FAK, ERK1/2), potentiated mitochondrial biogenesis signaling (PGC-1α), and **attenuated the exercise-driven rise in NF-κB and the inflammatory cytokines TNF-α and IL-6** — while, notably, having **no effect on lactate clearance or muscle metabolites**. So the old "massage flushes lactate/toxins" story is wrong; the real story is that mechanical load dampens the inflammatory signaling cascade of muscle damage (proven mechanism; debunked lactate-flush myth).

**For Soto:** Massage is the flagship. For a Neptune arm-care/recovery service, deep-tissue focused sessions of ~15–30 min, deployed within 24–48h of high-intensity throwing, are the defensible protocol. Frame the value honestly: it reduces soreness and marginally speeds strength return, not "removes toxins." The Crane finding also justifies percussion and foam rolling as lower-cost mechanical substitutes that plausibly trigger a similar mechanotransduction pathway.

## 3. Stretching for Recovery: Mostly Weak

Static stretching's reputation as a recovery tool does not survive the data. The Frontiers 2021 meta-analysis of RCTs on post-exercise stretching found **no meaningful effect on DOMS, strength recovery, or ROM recovery** vs passive recovery, with **GRADE certainty rated low across all outcomes** (risk of bias + imprecision). A more recent 2025 lower-limb meta-analysis reached the same non-significant conclusion, and the umbrella review put stretching in the explicitly "weak" bucket. The honest bottom line from the 2021 authors: evidence-based recommendations to stretch *for recovery* "should be avoided" given the scarce, heterogeneous, low-certainty data (debunked as a recovery modality).

Stretching does two other things worth separating from recovery: (a) it acutely and chronically increases ROM, and (b) done statically before performance, it *impairs* output. Static stretching decreases maximal strength ~**5.4%**, power ~**1.9%**, and explosive performance ~**2.0%**; short-duration static stretch (<90s total, sub-discomfort) minimizes that penalty. Dynamic stretching, by contrast, is neutral-to-positive on power and favorable on agility, largely via raised muscle temperature and fiber conduction velocity (proven).

**For Soto:** Do not sell static stretching as recovery — it's a mobility/ROM tool, and pre-throwing it's actively counterproductive. Neptune's warm-ups should be dynamic; static/PNF work belongs in dedicated mobility blocks away from high-intent throwing, targeted at specific ROM deficits (see §6), not as a generic "cool-down stretch to recover."

## 4. Foam Rolling & Percussion: Cheap, Self-Administered, Real (for ROM & Pain)

Foam rolling is the best-studied self-myofascial release (SMR) tool. The 2020 *Journal of Bodywork & Movement Therapies* meta-analysis (13 ROM studies, 18 datasets) found a **large ROM effect, d = 0.76 (95% CI 0.55–0.98)**, with increased ROM in every study analyzed. A separate meta reported a moderate training effect (ES ≈ 0.82) across 290 participants. For recovery, the 2019 meta-analysis concluded pre-rolling gives a **small boost to sprint and flexibility** with negligible jump/strength effect, while post-rolling **slightly attenuates sprint/strength decrements and reduces pain perception** — but outside ROM, foam rolling is not directly performance-enhancing. A focused SMR-after-HIIT study found foam rolling cut DOMS by **~50% (vs ~20% control)** and added **~4.2% hip ROM** (proven for ROM/pain; plausible for recovery-of-performance).

Percussion (massage guns) is the newer entrant and holds up. A 2025 RCT in active young men found percussion beat static stretching for DOMS recovery, and crucially **dose-dependent**: **40-minute** ("long-duration") percussion produced significantly lower 48h pain, greater knee ROM, and better jump performance than short sessions or stretching. Percussion reliably increases passive ROM acutely without the strength penalty of static stretching (promising). A frequently cited applied study showed hamstring ROM increased in a Theragun group vs baseline and control.

The catch on all self-tools is standardization: pressure, dwell time, and coverage are user-dependent, and inexperienced users under-dose or avoid discomfort, hurting adherence and effect.

**For Soto:** Foam rollers and percussion guns are the highest-ROI recovery hardware for Neptune — cheap, athlete-operated, and evidence-backed for the two things throwers care about between outings (ROM restoration and pain). Program them with actual doses (e.g., 30–120s per muscle group for rolling; longer percussion blocks track better), not "roll around till it feels good." Consider selling branded units + a coached protocol as a retail/recurring line.

## 5. Manual Therapy & IASTM: Modest, Heterogeneous Evidence

Beyond generic massage, throwers encounter instrument-assisted soft tissue mobilization (IASTM/Graston), cupping, and joint mobilization. The IASTM systematic review + effect-size analysis found **8 studies showed statistically significant within-group ROM gains**, but the evidence is limited by small study counts and heterogeneity, and between-group superiority over other treatments is not established. Manual therapy broadly shows benefit for **pain modulation and ROM restoration** across musculoskeletal rehab, and for specific conditions like patellofemoral pain it has JOSPT-level support — but the UK evidence-report update classifies much of the manual-therapy literature as "inconclusive (favourable)" rather than definitively proven (promising for ROM/pain; plausible overall).

For the throwing shoulder specifically, instrument-assisted work plus a stretching program improves shoulder motion in baseball players, and muscle energy techniques (MET) can acutely restore internal-rotation motion in overhead throwers (promising, low-N case-level evidence).

**For Soto:** Treat IASTM/cupping/MET as legitimate but interchangeable "restore-ROM" tools with modest, ROM-and-pain-limited evidence — worth having a practitioner deliver, not worth over-claiming. Their real edge over self-tools is a trained set of hands finding and loading the right tissue, which is exactly the practitioner-vs-self distinction in §7.

## 6. Mobility Programming for Throwers: The Real Targets

Generic "mobility work" is noise; thrower-specific ROM has a hard evidence base tied to velocity and injury.

**Shoulder rotation.** Throwing shifts the shoulder's arc: minor-league starters lost internal rotation (**49.4°→46.0°** immediately, p=0.037) and gained external rotation (**150.7°→153.6°** immediately; **→154.0° at 24h**, p=0.028) across 9 pitchers / 22 starts. IR deficit resolved by 24h; ER gain persisted. This is the physiological basis of GIRD (glenohumeral internal rotation deficit). Pitchers with GIRD have up to **~4x higher incidence of shoulder/elbow injury** and miss more games; a **total-rotation deficit >5°** flags elevated risk; and elbow injury risk rises **~7% per degree of lost shoulder ER**. Post-pitching IR strength drops ~10–15% and ER strength ~5–10%. Restoring motion lowers injury rates (proven associations).

**The sleeper/cross-body debate.** Sleeper and cross-body stretches are the standard GIRD interventions and reliably increase IR ROM (moderate evidence in asymptomatic GIRD). Notably, a RCT found **sacroiliac (contralateral SI joint) stretching improved GIRD more than the sleeper stretch** — a reminder that the kinetic chain, not just the glenohumeral joint, drives shoulder motion (promising; challenges the sleeper-stretch monopoly).

**Thoracic spine.** Pro pitchers land with **55 ± 6° of upper-torso rotation at front-foot strike**; adequate thoracic rotation (~70–90° active on rotation tests) enables hip-shoulder separation, which correlates with trunk-rotation velocity and thus pitch velocity, while offloading the rotator cuff and elbow. Athletes with low thoracic rotation compensate with early trunk rotation — a velocity leak and an arm-stress driver (proven biomechanical link).

**Hips.** Hip passive-ROM deficits correlate with impaired pelvis/trunk rotation, degrading the down-chain shoulder/elbow mechanics, since pelvis rotation initiates the kinetic-chain energy transfer (promising).

**For Soto:** This is Neptune's assessment battery, not a stretching class. Screen and track: bilateral GH IR/ER + total rotation (flag GIRD and >5° TRM deficit), lumbar-locked thoracic rotation (target ~70–90°), and hip IR/ER. Feed these into Triton as athlete-level fields alongside TrackMan output — a per-athlete "motion loss vs baseline after outing" chart is a differentiated facility metric and a direct arm-care monitoring signal. Program mobility to *specific* deficits (sleeper/cross-body + SI/hip mobility for GIRD; thoracic-extension/rotation drills for early trunk rotation), and re-measure to prove the intervention closed the gap.

## 7. Practitioner vs Self-Administered: What You're Paying For

For most *recovery* endpoints, self-administered tools match practitioner hands. Head-to-head work shows **foam rolling is roughly as effective as manual massage** for flexibility, pain reduction (comparable analgesic effect via nociceptor/mechanoreceptor pathways), and perceived recovery; in one comparison self-massage even edged manual therapy for dynamic-balance recovery. The mechanistic review frames the difference as control mode: massage is passive (retroactive control, the practitioner integrates proprioceptive feedback), foam rolling is active (proactive control, the athlete drives it) — different routes to overlapping outcomes.

Where practitioners genuinely win:
- **Depth and target specificity** — reaching deep/hard-to-self-treat tissue (posterior cuff, subscap, deep hip rotators) that a roller can't isolate.
- **Real-time adjustment** — palpation-guided load, dwell, and direction the athlete can't self-diagnose.
- **Multi-region, holistic sessions** — 15–30 min covering the full chain vs 30–120s per muscle self-work.
- **Individualization to fatigue/load** — a skilled clinician modulates by how the athlete presents that day.

Where self-tools win: **cost, frequency, autonomy, and adherence** — an athlete can foam roll or percuss daily for free; a manual therapist is episodic and expensive. The practical model most elite programs land on is **practitioner for assessment + episodic deep work, self-tools for daily volume** (plausible/promising).

**For Soto:** Neptune's economics should mirror this. Bundle a periodic hands-on manual/IASTM session (higher margin, assessment-anchored) with a self-administered daily protocol (rollers, percussion, banded mobility) the athlete owns. The daily self-work carries the recovery load between hands-on touches; the practitioner session is where you find and attack the deep, individual restrictions and re-screen the §6 motion metrics. This also scales — one clinician can't massage every athlete daily, but every athlete can self-treat daily off a coached template.

## 8. The Elite-Practice Shift: Less Volume, More Intent

The strongest signal from named practitioners is a deliberate move away from high-volume post-throw "arm care." Driveline publicly changed its recovery protocol: it retired the traditional post-throw circuit (rebounders, waiter walks, band, shoulder-tube work) on moderate-to-high-intensity days and replaced it with a **full-body dynamic-mobility cool-down**, and it **moved arm-care strength work into the lifting program** so it can be programmed higher-intensity / lower-volume. Their stated reasoning: research suggested the old high-volume arm care **didn't meaningfully reduce soreness, ROM loss, or next-day performance**, and since "throwing itself is a fatiguing exercise," piling low-intensity endurance work on a fatigued arm was counterproductive (promising — practice-led, evidence-informed).

Tread Athletics reflects the same individualization ethos through tech: it integrated the **ArmCare app + hand-held strength sensor** (Crossover Symmetry) to screen shoulder strength/mobility imbalances predictive of injury and generate **individualized daily arm-care routines** driven by each athlete's fatigue/recovery/strength profile, rather than a one-size circuit (promising).

**For Soto:** This is the philosophical spine for Neptune's arm-care product. Don't sell generic high-volume band circuits as "recovery." Sell (1) individualized, sensor-screened arm care programmed by intent and volume like any other lift, (2) dynamic mobility cool-downs, and (3) targeted self soft-tissue/mobility to measured deficits. This is exactly the assessment→programming→monitoring spine the facility needs, and it's directly buildable on the Compete/TrackMan + Triton data layer: log ArmCare-style strength/ROM screens next to pitch data, flag athletes whose post-outing motion loss or strength drop exceeds baseline, and auto-surface the individualized recovery prescription.

# Recovery Nutrition & Hydration for Baseball Athletes

## TL;DR

- **Protein: hit the per-meal dose, not just the daily total.** ~0.3 g/kg (≈0.25–0.40 g/kg, ~20–40 g) per feeding, containing ~2.5–3 g leucine, every 3–4 h maximally stimulates muscle protein synthesis (MPS); daily target 1.6–2.0 g/kg/day, with meta-analysis showing lean-mass gains plateau near 1.6 g/kg/day (proven). Even distribution across 4 meals beats a dinner-skewed pattern.
- **Carbohydrate need is role- and day-specific.** Multi-inning pitchers and catchers burn more (game-day estimate ~3,500 kcal for a starting pitcher vs ~3,000 kcal for position players) and should make carbs ~2/3 of the recovery plate; other position players ~1/4 (promising). Glycogen refill peaks at 1.0–1.2 g/kg/h of carbohydrate in the first ~4 h post-exertion (proven).
- **Even mild dehydration degrades pitching.** A 1–2% body-mass fluid deficit measurably reduces strength, reaction time, coordination, and focus; college players lost ~3.5% body mass over 2 h in 36.7 °C heat without fluids, and baseball athletes need on the order of ~1 L (≈32 oz) per hour in heat despite having lower sweat losses than football/endurance athletes (proven for hypohydration → performance; plausible for exact velocity delta).
- **Creatine monohydrate is the highest-evidence supplement — 3–5 g/day, or a 0.3 g/kg/day × 5–7 day load.** Safe up to 30 g/day for 5 years in healthy people; raises intramuscular phosphocreatine and improves repeat high-power output; ~8.7% brain-creatine rise supports possible cognitive/sleep-deprivation benefit (proven for power; promising for cognition).
- **Omega-3 (EPA+DHA ~2–3 g/day) lowers post-exercise soreness and inflammatory markers.** ~4.2 g EPA+DHA delayed perceived soreness best; effects are real but modest, and it does not replace training adaptation (promising).
- **Collagen/gelatin timing matters more than dose: 15 g gelatin + ~50 mg vitamin C, 30–60 min before loading, roughly doubled collagen synthesis (PINP +153% at 4 h).** Works by feeding the tendon's response to load, not by building tendon alone (promising for connective-tissue support).
- **Tart cherry (≈260–480 ml/day or concentrate, 4–5 days pre and 2–3 days post) modestly aids functional recovery and lowers IL-6/IL-8.** Evidence is heterogeneous, low-to-moderate certainty; no reliable effect on CRP, TNF-α, or CK (promising-to-plausible).
- **Vitamin D deficiency is common in athletes and tracks with injury.** NBA combine data: ~79% insufficient/deficient, ~32% deficient (<20 ng/mL); each 5 ng/mL drop ≈ +13% injury odds. Correcting low status reduces stress-fracture risk (promising).
- **Whole food first; supplements are the last 5%.** Priority order: total calories > macros/fiber > micros/water > timing (proven as a framework). Timing is real but only after the daily totals are right.

## 1. Protein: dose, distribution, and the overnight window

The single most robust finding in recovery nutrition is that MPS responds to protein in a **dose-dependent, saturable** way, and the meal-level dose is what athletes chronically underexploit. Multiple isotope-tracer studies converge on **~0.31 g/kg of high-quality protein per meal** to maximally stimulate MPS, with a practical operating band of **0.25–0.40 g/kg** (proven). For an 90 kg (≈200 lb) pitcher that is roughly **28–36 g per feeding** — meaning a "protein shake with one scoop" (~24 g) is at the low edge, and a larger athlete eating a single chicken breast at lunch is under-dosing that meal.

The mechanism runs through the **leucine threshold**: each feeding needs ~2.5–3 g leucine to trigger the mTOR-mediated MPS signal (proven for the signaling step; plausible as a hard "threshold" in practice). This is why protein *quality* matters — whey (~2.5–3 g leucine per 25 g) crosses easily, while lower-leucine plant sources may need a larger dose or blending.

**Distribution beats skew.** Feeding ~0.3 g/kg across 3–4 evenly spaced meals produces greater 24-h MPS and better long-term lean-mass/strength gains than skewing most protein to dinner (proven). The daily target for athletes is **1.6–2.0 g/kg/day**; Morton et al.'s meta-analysis found lean-mass gains largely plateau near **1.6 g/kg/day**, and ACSM/ISSN guidance spans **1.2–2.0 g/kg/day**, upper end for heavy resistance phases (proven). Practitioner sources agree: Driveline recommends 1.6–1.8 g/kg for athletes; Tread Athletics periodizes it (1.6–1.8 g/kg off-season for mass, 1.3–1.5 g/kg in-season for maintenance) (promising — practitioner consensus aligned with literature).

**The overnight feeding** is the one timing manipulation with strong tracer support. **40 g of casein pre-sleep** raised overnight MPS to 0.044 %/h vs 0.033 %/h placebo (P = 0.02), a ~33% increase, giving a slow 7–8 h amino-acid release. Notably, **20 g did not** significantly beat placebo overnight, and adding free leucine to 30 g casein did not augment it — for the overnight window, **total dose (~40 g) matters more than hitting the leucine minimum** (proven).

**For Soto:** For Neptune's programming spine, encode protein as a **per-meal target keyed to bodyweight** (0.3 g/kg × 4 + a 40 g pre-sleep casein), not a daily blob — this is the single highest-leverage nutrition rule for developing pitchers adding mass. For Trevor specifically (masters athlete, post-TJ, staying-sharp not bulking), the master-athlete literature argues for the **upper end (~1.8–2.0 g/kg/day)** because of anabolic resistance with age, with special attention to the pre-sleep dose on heavy throwing days.

## 2. Carbohydrate: pitchers vs position players

Baseball is intermittent, not endurance — but "low intensity" hides real glycogen and CNS demands, especially for **multi-inning pitchers and catchers**, who repeat maximal-effort actions (a starter throws 90–110 pitches; a catcher squats and throws every pitch). Game-day energy-expenditure estimates put a **starting pitcher near ~3,500 kcal** and a **position player near ~3,000 kcal** (promising — modeled, not doubly-labeled-water measured).

The role split shows up in recovery-plate guidance from pro baseball sports-science sources: **carbs should be ~2/3 of the recovery plate for multi-inning pitchers/catchers, and ~1/4 for other position players** (promising). Pre-game, a carb-focused meal ~3 h out at **1–1.5 g/kg** is a common target (promising).

Where the evidence is hardest is **glycogen resynthesis**: after glycogen-depleting work, **1.0–1.2 g/kg/h of moderate-to-high-GI carbohydrate for the first ~4 h** maximizes refill (rate ~10 mmol/kg/h), and **adding ~0.3 g/kg protein** both drives MPS and helps glycogen when carb intake is suboptimal (≤0.8 g/kg) (proven). For a starting pitcher the day after an outing, this matters less for same-day glycogen (they're not pitching tomorrow) and more for **replenishing between-start training loads**; for position players in a daily lineup, the 4-h refill window is genuinely relevant because they play again in ~18–24 h.

**Practical translation:** a starter's post-outing meal should still be carb-forward (they emptied tanks and the CNS is taxed), but the *urgency* of the 4-h window is highest for everyday players and for anyone playing a doubleheader or back-to-back in heat.

**For Soto:** This is a clean place to make Neptune programming *role-aware*. Tie carbohydrate prescriptions to the athlete's game role and next-workload timing, and — where TrackMan/Compete data exist — flag high-pitch-count outings that warrant an aggressive refill. It also connects to Triton: a "workload → fueling" note on the pitcher dashboard (pitches thrown, days to next outing) is a low-effort, high-credibility facility differentiator.

## 3. Hydration and pitching performance

Dehydration is the most *actionable* in-game variable because it's large, common, and cheap to fix. The consensus threshold: **a fluid deficit of ~1–2% of body mass measurably impairs strength, power, coordination, reaction time, mood, and focus** — every attribute a pitcher needs in the 6th inning (proven). Most athletes arrive **chronically under-hydrated at rest**, compounding in-game losses (plausible).

Baseball's sweat losses are, sport-for-sport, on the **lower** end (below football and endurance athletes) — but "lower" is not "low" in heat. In college-age players at **36.7 °C**, body-mass loss reached **3.47% over 2 h with no fluid intake**, and total sweat loss across a 5-h practice/game session was **56.53 ml/kg** (proven — measured). At 3.47%, a pitcher is well past the 2% performance-decrement line. Field guidance lands around **~1 L (≈32 oz) per hour** in heat, adjusted to individual sweat rate (promising).

**Individualize by sweat rate.** The correct protocol is to weigh in/out and replace **~1.25–1.5 L per kg of body mass lost**, adding **sodium** for salty sweaters (sweat sodium varies widely by athlete). Prescribing individualized hydration protocols improved hydration status in adolescents exercising in warm, humid conditions (promising). For heat specifically, **ice-slurry ingestion** lowered core temperature and preserved pitching performance in a hot-environment softball-pitching RCT — a usable pre-game/between-innings cooling tactic (promising).

The **velocity-specific** claim ("dehydration costs X mph") is intuitive and mechanistically supported (reduced strength/power, earlier fatigue → later-inning velo drop) but I did not find a clean peer-reviewed mph delta; treat the *directional* claim as plausible and the *underlying performance decrement* as proven.

**For Soto:** Neptune should run a **sweat-rate assessment** in the intake battery (pre/post-bodyweight on a hot bullpen day, plus a sweat-sodium field test for salty sweaters) and hand each athlete a personalized fluid + sodium target — this is exactly the kind of individualized, Driveline-style deliverable that justifies a "development lab" price point. On the platform side, later-inning velocity decay is already computable from Statcast/TrackMan; pairing a pitcher's within-outing velo-decay curve with a hydration protocol is a differentiated, data-backed intervention.

## 4. Creatine — the highest-evidence supplement

Creatine monohydrate is the most-studied, best-supported ergogenic aid relevant to baseball's repeat-power demands (proven for strength/power). Dosing: **3–5 g/day** maintenance, or an optional load of **~0.3 g/kg/day for 5–7 days** (≈20 g/day split into 4 doses) followed by **0.03 g/kg/day (~3–5 g/day)** maintenance. Loading raises intramuscular phosphocreatine faster; the endpoint concentration is the same either way, so many athletes skip loading to avoid GI upset and water-weight questions.

**Efficacy:** creatine reliably improves repeated high-intensity/phosphagen-system efforts (strength, jumps, short sprints). Sport-specific meta-analyses are more mixed — a soccer meta-analysis found limited effects on some field tests and none on aerobic performance — reflecting that creatine helps the *power/strength substrate*, which then must transfer to skill (proven for the substrate; plausible for direct on-field transfer).

**Safety:** the ISSN position stand concludes creatine is safe and well-tolerated **up to 30 g/day for up to 5 years** in healthy individuals across ages; kidney/liver-harm claims in healthy people are not supported (proven). A women's-football real-world in-season study found no clinically meaningful derangements in biochemical safety markers with long-term use.

**Emerging angle — brain/cognition/sleep:** ~95% of body creatine is in muscle, but the ~5% in brain is responsive: **20 g/day × 4 weeks raised brain creatine ~8.7%**, and creatine shows promise for **cognitive performance under stress/sleep deprivation** (promising) — directly relevant to a travel-heavy pro/college schedule and to catchers/pitchers managing sleep debt.

**For Soto:** Creatine is the one supplement Neptune can recommend broadly with a straight face for developing athletes (with parental consent for minors). It also pairs with the mass-gain/velocity program: more training volume tolerated + more lean mass. Flag it in programming as **3–5 g/day, any time of day, indefinitely**, and drop the loading phase unless the athlete needs a fast ramp.

## 5. Omega-3, collagen, tart cherry, vitamin D

**Omega-3 (EPA + DHA).** Anti-inflammatory, membrane-incorporating fatty acids that modestly reduce **DOMS and inflammatory/muscle-damage markers**. Effective doses cluster at **~2–3 g/day combined EPA+DHA** (systematic reviews show benefit around ~2.4 g/day over ~4.5 weeks); one study found **~4.2 g EPA+DHA** best at delaying perceived soreness, with less DOMS at 72–96 h (promising). Emphasize EPA slightly for anti-inflammatory effect. In collision-sport athletes, **2 g DHA/day over a season** showed a small-to-moderate neuroprotective signal — of secondary interest for concussion-adjacent contexts, not core to baseball. Verdict: a reasonable **recovery + arm-tissue-inflammation** adjunct, not a needle-mover on its own; incorporation into tissue takes **weeks**, so it's a standing supplement, not an acute one.

**Collagen / gelatin for connective tissue.** The Keith Baar / Greg Shaw work is the anchor: **15 g gelatin + ~48–50 mg vitamin C, taken 30–60 min before loading**, roughly **doubled collagen-synthesis markers**, with **PINP +153% at 4 h** for the 15 g dose vs 5 g/placebo (promising). Hydrolyzed collagen peptides **10–15 g/day** with vitamin C, pre-load, over **3–6 months** appears to increase tendon stiffness/function. Critical caveat: **collagen supports the tendon's response to loading — it does not build tendon by itself**; it must be paired with progressive tendon loading (heavy-slow resistance). For a pitcher, the relevance is UCL/flexor-tendon and shoulder connective tissue resilience — supportive, not curative (promising, and explicitly load-dependent).

**Tart cherry.** Anthocyanin-rich, antioxidant/anti-inflammatory. Meta-analyses show **beneficial effects on functional recovery and IL-6/IL-8**, with an optimal functional dose around **~260 ml/day** (or 8–12 oz twice daily / 1 oz concentrate twice daily), **loaded 4–5 days before and 2–3 days after** an event. But evidence is **heterogeneous, low-to-moderate certainty**, with **no reliable effect on CK, CRP, TNF-α, IL-1β, or perceived soreness** in pooled data (promising for function/select markers; plausible-to-weak for soreness). A real consideration: chronic high-dose antioxidants **may blunt training adaptation**, so reserve tart cherry for **congested schedules / recovery-priority windows** (doubleheaders, tournaments, playoff stretches), not as an everyday off-season supplement.

**Vitamin D.** Deficiency is common in athletes and tracks with injury and impaired recovery. **NBA-combine data: ~79% insufficient/deficient, ~32% deficient (<20 ng/mL), ~47% insufficient (20–32 ng/mL).** Across athlete studies, **each 5 ng/mL decrease ≈ +13% injury odds**, and low vitamin D is over-represented in ligament/cartilage (76.5%), patellofemoral (71.0%), and muscle/tendon (54.6%) injuries. **Correcting low status reduces stress-fracture risk** (promising). Indoor-heavy, higher-latitude, and darker-skinned athletes are at elevated risk. Practical: **test 25(OH)D**, target sufficiency (commonly ≥32–40 ng/mL), and supplement deficient athletes (typically ~2,000–5,000 IU/day, retest) rather than blanket-dosing.

**For Soto:** Build a **tiered supplement stack for Neptune** graded by evidence: Tier 1 (recommend broadly) = **creatine 3–5 g/day + vitamin D correction when tested-low**; Tier 2 (situational, promising) = **omega-3 2–3 g/day standing, collagen 15 g + vit C pre-throwing/lifting for connective-tissue-history athletes, tart cherry for congested schedules**; Tier 3 = everything else, off the menu until asked. This maps cleanly to the "evidence-graded advice only" standing constraint and gives the facility a defensible, non-bro-science supplement policy. For Trevor (post-TJ, arm-health-first), the **collagen-pre-throwing + omega-3 standing + vitamin-D-tested** combination is the most defensible personal stack, always secondary to load management.

## 6. In-game and post-game fueling

**Pre-game (~3 h out):** a carbohydrate-forward meal at **1–1.5 g/kg** with moderate protein and low fat/fiber to ease digestion (promising). For a starting pitcher, this is the tank-fill before a ~2.5–3 h outing.

**In-game / between innings:** the goals are **maintain blood glucose, replace fluid + sodium, avoid GI load**. Practical tools: **~32 oz fluid/hour in heat** individualized to sweat rate, an **electrolyte/carbohydrate drink** for long outings or hot days, easy carbs (fruit, sports drink, chews) for multi-hour games, and **ice slurry / cooling** pre-inning in heat to blunt core-temperature rise (promising). Catchers and starters are the priority targets. Caffeine (~3–6 mg/kg) is a separate, well-supported ergogenic lever but sits outside this doc's scope.

**Post-game recovery ("the plate"):** the highest-leverage window combines **protein 0.3 g/kg** (drive MPS) **+ carbohydrate** (refill glycogen, urgency scaled to next-game timing) **+ fluid/sodium** (replace 1.25–1.5 L per kg lost). The role-specific plate split — **~2/3 carbs for multi-inning pitchers/catchers, ~1/4 for other position players** — is the field-usable heuristic (promising). The **4-h, 1.0–1.2 g/kg/h carbohydrate** aggressive-refill protocol is worth invoking specifically for everyday players and back-to-backs; for a starter with 4–5 days to the next outing, a normal carb-forward meal plus the **40 g pre-sleep casein** covers overnight tissue repair (proven for the MPS piece).

**Program-level truth:** timing effects are real but **fourth in priority** — total calories, then macro/fiber quality, then micronutrients/water, then meal frequency & timing. An athlete who nails the daily protein total and hydration but ignores the exact post-game window will out-recover one who obsesses over windows while missing daily targets (proven as a hierarchy).

**For Soto:** Encode a **post-game "recovery plate" template** in Neptune's programming that auto-selects the carb:protein ratio by role and by hours-to-next-game, and defaults every athlete to **fluid replacement by measured weight change + 40 g pre-sleep casein**. On Triton, a lightweight "recovery flag" on the pitcher/outing view (high pitch count + hot game + short rest → aggressive refill + hydration reminder) turns nutrition science into a shippable, incremental platform feature consistent with the build-fast, high-value-per-change constraint.

# Recovery Technology Buyers Guide 2026 — What a Training Facility Should Actually Buy

## TL;DR

- **The evidence-to-dollar ranking is nearly the inverse of the marketing-hype ranking.** The three cheapest interventions — sleep programming, hydration/nutrition education, and daily movement — move readiness more than any device on this page (proven), yet they cost ~$0 in capital. Every hardware purchase below should be justified only after those three are systematized, because Tread Athletics and Driveline both explicitly rank gadgets *last* behind sleep/food/hydration (proven).
- **Almost every modality reliably moves one cheap outcome — perceived soreness (ES/SMD ≈ −0.4 to −0.65) — and almost none reliably restores maximal force.** Intermittent pneumatic compression: pain ES 0.49 at 0–2h, 0.64 at 48h, but muscular-function ES 0.09–0.13 (trivial, non-significant) across 319 subjects / 17 studies (proven). Buy for feel, not for force return.
- **A sauna is the single best first thermal purchase for a development facility, and it is cheaper than a real cold plunge.** A commercial infrared cabin runs $3,000–$10,000 (freestanding, plug-in, often no permits); traditional cabin $8,000–$17,000 installed. Unlike cold, heat does not blunt strength/hypertrophy adaptation and adds a genuine adaptive stimulus — plasma-volume expansion ~5.6%, dose-dependent all-cause mortality reduction in Laukkanen's 2,315-man cohort (proven for cardio-health; promising for muscle). (promising-to-proven)
- **A real chiller cold plunge is a $5,000–$16,000 capital item (Morozko starts ~$9,900, commercial builds $30k+), and for a development mission it is a lower priority than a sauna** — cold-water immersion blunts resistance-training hypertrophy (comparative cSMD ≈ −0.22, RT-alone SMD 0.36 → 0.14 with CWI) and does not restore strength (proven). Start with a $1,600–$2,700 inflatable-plus-chiller unit; defer the six-figure spa build.
- **Marc Pro ($699; Plus $1,399) is the highest-ROI "active" device for a throwing facility** — non-fatiguing e-stim, adopted by all 30 MLB teams and built into Driveline's protocols; the mechanism (blood flow without added fatigue) fits the post-throwing window better than ice (promising, not proven for hard outcomes).
- **Massage guns ($100–$600) are a legitimate cheap warm-up/ROM and soreness tool but a performance dead-end** — a 2024 RCT found no significant group×time effect on any performance/perceptual measure after 5-min application; large acute ROM gains, no reliable strength/power benefit, small risk of blunting explosive output if used immediately pre-throwing (promising for ROM/soreness; debunked for performance recovery).
- **Red/near-infrared light (photobiomodulation) is the most surprising "promising" on evidence but the hardest to dose right** — a 2025 DOMS meta-analysis (4 studies each arm) reports strength-recovery SMD 0.97–0.99 (large) at 24–48h and pain SMD −0.55/−0.56 at 72–96h, 660–950 nm; small samples, heterogeneous dosing keep it out of "proven" (promising).
- **PEMF is the clearest "spend last / spend never" line item** — trends toward improved 24–72h peak power and soreness, but reviews conclude effects on physical function are inconclusive and protocols are un-standardized; premium units are priced on belief, not data (plausible).
- **Game Ready ($2,500–$4,000) is a rehab/post-op device, not a daily-recovery device, and mostly belongs on a rental model** — cold+compression at 38–45°F is correct for an acute injury or post-surgical joint, and needs a prescription; it should not anchor a healthy-athlete recovery room (proven use-case, wrong budget line for general training).

## How To Read This Guide

Two questions decide every purchase, and they are different questions:

1. **Does the modality do something the evidence supports?** Graded inline below.
2. **Should *this* buyer own it, or rent/refer/skip it?** A device can be evidence-backed for a narrow use (Game Ready for post-op) and still be a bad purchase for a healthy-athlete development lab.

The organizing principle from the companion evidence doc still governs: **recovery and adaptation are a tradeoff, not a synergy.** Anything that blunts post-exercise inflammation and blood flow (ice, CWI, NSAIDs) makes an athlete feel better tomorrow at the cost of the training stimulus (proven). For a *development* facility whose product is adaptation, that pushes heat, movement, and e-stim up the buy list and pushes aggressive cold down it. The decision is almost entirely about calendar position — game week vs. build block.

**For Soto:** Neptune's differentiator is not the recovery room — commodity cage barns can buy a plunge too. The differentiator is that recovery is *prescribed by phase* out of the programming layer and *monitored* against workload. Buy the minimum hardware that lets you deliver a phase-aware protocol, and spend the saved capital on assessment tech (force plates, the TrackMan pipeline already in hand) that actually generates the data moat. The recovery room is a retention amenity; the assessment stack is the business.

## The Foundation Tier (Buy This First — It's Nearly Free)

Before any device, three interventions out-perform the entire hardware catalog and cost almost nothing in capital:

- **Sleep.** The single largest lever on next-day readiness and injury risk (proven). Driveline's guidance: 8–10 h for teens/youth, 7–9 h for young adults; failing any one of sleep/food/hydration measurably degrades performance (proven-by-consensus). Capital cost: a whiteboard, a check-in habit, and blackout discipline in any on-site nap/rest space.
- **Hydration + nutrition.** Protein timing, carbohydrate refuel, and fluid/electrolyte status are the substrate every device downstream is trying to optimize. Zero-capital education beats a $12,000 plunge that a dehydrated, under-fed athlete steps into.
- **Daily movement / active recovery.** Driveline explicitly *replaced* passive post-throwing recovery work with a mobility-focused cool-down circuit and moved arm-care into higher-intensity/lower-volume lifting (proven-by-adoption). At least 10 min of mobility on the two days after a start restores ROM ahead of the 72h bullpen (practitioner standard).

Both Driveline and Tread Athletics state the hierarchy directly: get the basics in order first; "gadgets and modalities" make up a *small* share of the recovery picture and only earn attention after sleep/food/hydration are handled (proven-by-consensus).

**For Soto:** This tier is where Neptune wins cheaply. Build sleep/hydration/nutrition tracking into the athlete dashboard on the Compete pipeline before buying a single machine. A red readiness flag driven by two nights of <7h sleep is worth more than any device and costs only schema and UI. This is also the honest answer to a budget-constrained facility: your "recovery tech" line item can start at essentially $0 and still beat a competitor who blew $40k on a spa.

## Compression: Normatec vs. Game Ready

**What the evidence says.** Intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) is a soreness-and-feel tool. The 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis (17 studies, 319 participants, PEDro mean 6.29): pain/soreness ES 0.49 at 0–2h, 0.64 at 48h, 0.37 at 96h (trivial-to-moderate); muscular-function ES 0.24 at 0–2h falling to 0.09–0.13 at 24–72h (trivial, non-significant); creatine-kinase effects "highly variable" and blood-lactate results contradictory (proven). Optimal protocol: ~80 mmHg for 20–30 min (range used 60–235 mmHg). Head-to-head, boots are not clearly superior to a well-fitted compression sleeve (promising). Translation: it makes legs feel fresher; it does not restore power or clear damage markers reliably.

**Normatec** (compression only, no cooling; ~$900–$1,500 for the Normatec 3): the right tool for daily athletic "flush," post-lift leg heaviness, and the perceived-recovery bump before a game. Legs/hips/arms coverage. Buy for the recovery lounge; expect a soreness/comfort dividend, not a performance one.

**Game Ready** (cold + compression, 38–45°F active cooling; ~$2,500–$4,000, prescription-gated for rental): a *rehab and post-operative* device — knee/shoulder/ankle/back after surgery or acute injury. It is genuinely good at that job (cold+compression for an angry joint) and genuinely miscast as a daily healthy-athlete recovery anchor. Because it is prescription-oriented and injury-specific, a rental/buy-back program (weeks-long rentals are the norm) beats ownership for most facilities — you don't want a $3,500 asset idle until someone has surgery.

**For Soto:** Normatec earns a spot in Neptune's lounge as a low-risk amenity that athletes *feel*; program it as a between-session or travel-day flush, never as the thing that "fixes" a build-block session. Game Ready should be a referral/rental relationship (partner PT clinic or rehab line item), not a capital purchase — put that ~$3,500 toward assessment tech instead. Flag in the programming layer: compression is fine any phase because it doesn't strongly blunt adaptation; that's its quiet advantage over cold.

## Thermal: Cold Plunge vs. Sauna

This is the biggest-ticket decision in the recovery room, and the evidence and the budget both point the same, counterintuitive direction: **for a development facility, buy heat before cold.**

### Sauna / Heat — the underrated first thermal buy

Heat *adds* an adaptive stimulus rather than subtracting one. Plasma-volume expansion ~5.6% (2025 Bayesian meta-regression, 211 heat-acclimation studies), most within the first 5–10 sessions (proven). Laukkanen's KIHD cohort (2,315 Finnish men, 20.7y follow-up) found dose-dependent reductions in sudden cardiac death, CHD, CVD, and all-cause mortality; ≥19-min sessions and 4–7×/week beat shorter/less-frequent exposure (proven association; observational). Critically, heat does **not** carry cold's hypertrophy-blunting signature and may even protect muscle during disuse (promising). Adaptations decay fast — roughly 1 day lost per 2 days without exposure.

**Price:** commercial infrared cabin $3,000–$10,000 (freestanding, plug-in, panels draw 1,500–3,000 W, reaches temp in 10–20 min, usually no construction/permits); traditional barrel $6,000–$11,000; traditional indoor cabin $8,000–$17,000 installed (ventilation + 6,000–18,000 W heater + wiring). Operating cost is low: infrared ~$5–$15/month at 3 sessions/week. Note the evidence base (Laukkanen, plasma-volume work) is largely built on **traditional** Finnish sauna, not infrared — infrared is cheaper and easier to install but the mortality/plasma data don't transfer one-to-one (plausible for infrared-specific benefit).

### Cold plunge — real but lower priority for a development mission

Cold-water immersion genuinely speeds return of *power* after eccentric/high-intensity work (power SMD 0.34→0.47→0.62 at 24/48/72h, GRADE high) and reduces DOMS (g ≈ −0.40) — but it does **not** restore strength (SMD 0.12–0.74, all non-significant) and it **blunts resistance-training hypertrophy** (2024 meta-analysis, 8 studies: RT-alone SMD 0.36 collapses to 0.14 with CWI; comparative cSMD −0.22) (proven). Best dose ~10–15 min at 5–15°C. For a facility whose product is *building* athletes, an always-available plunge used carelessly within hours of the strength/throwing stimulus is actively counterproductive.

**Price tiers:**
- **Inflatable + chiller: ~$1,600–$2,700 all-in** (Plunge Air <$1,200 tub-only; Nordik from $1,649; Ice Barrel $1,799 + separate chiller). Right entry point for a budget facility — real cold, real temperature control, low capital.
- **Turnkey chiller tubs: ~$5,000–$16,000** (Renu, Plunge, mid-Morozko).
- **Premium / commercial: Morozko from ~$9,900, high-use commercial builds $30,000+** (ice-making, dual-temp, app control, high-traffic filtration).

**For Soto:** If Neptune buys one thermal asset, buy the sauna. It aligns with a development mission (adds adaptation, doubles as summer heat-acclimation for in-season durability), has a stronger evidence-to-cost story, and — surprisingly — the entry commercial infrared unit is cheaper than a real commercial plunge. Add cold second, at the inflatable-plus-chiller tier, and gate it in the programming layer: contraindicate CWI within ~4–6h of any acquisition-block throwing/lifting session (rising Stuff+/velo targets), green-light it the night before a showcase or during in-season back-to-backs. Do not build the six-figure spa until the athlete base and the phase-logic both exist. The plunge is an Instagram amenity; the phase-aware prescription is the product.

## E-Stim: Marc Pro and the Death of Ice-After-Throwing

The RICE/ice-after-pitching model is retired on the adaptation front — Mirkin (who coined RICE) retracted "ice" in 2015, and post-exercise inflammation is now understood as the courier system for repair (macrophage IGF-1, IL-6-driven satellite-cell activation) rather than damage to suppress (proven that inflammation is required for adaptation). Driveline's own protocol shift moved away from passive endurance-style recovery work toward mobility and higher-intensity/lower-volume arm care that matches pitching demands (proven-by-adoption).

**Marc Pro** ($699; Marc Pro Plus $1,399; 3-yr warranty, 30-day return) is the device that fills the vacated post-throwing window: patented *non-fatiguing* e-stim that drives muscle contraction and blood flow without adding the fatigue a tired arm can't afford. Adoption is the strongest signal here — 100+ pro teams, all 30 MLB clubs, and integration into Driveline's daily protocols (promising; the *mechanism and adoption* are strong, but hard-outcome RCTs remain thin, so not "proven"). At $699 it is the single highest-ROI "active" recovery purchase for a throwing facility, and it scales — buy several before you buy one plunge.

**For Soto:** Marc Pro is the flagship active-recovery buy for Neptune. It fits the throwing population specifically (blood flow without fatigue in the post-outing window), it's cheap enough to own in quantity, and its adoption story sells itself to pro-aspiring clients and their parents. Program it as the default post-moderate/high-intensity throwing tool in place of ice, reserving true cold for feel-based in-season use. This is also on-brand for Trevor: it's the modality a modern, data-literate pro-development shop uses, and it maps cleanly to "we don't ice, we move blood."

## Percussion, Light, and Fields — the Cheap-to-Skip Tier

**Massage guns** ($100–$600; Theragun/Hypervolt). Large acute ROM gains and a real soreness/warm-up utility, but a 2024 RCT found *no* significant group×time interaction on any performance or perceptual measure after 5-min post-exercise application, and reviews repeatedly note thin, inconsistent evidence for strength/ROM adaptation (promising for ROM/soreness; debunked for performance recovery). Small risk: percussion immediately pre-performance can transiently blunt explosive output. Verdict: **buy — it's cheap, athletes like it, and it's a legitimate mobility/warm-up tool** — just don't sell it as recovery magic and keep it out of the 30 min before max-effort throwing.

**Red / near-infrared light (photobiomodulation)** (panels ~$300–$10,000+ depending on size/clinical grade). Genuinely surprising signal: a 2025 DOMS systematic review/meta-analysis reports muscle-strength recovery SMD 0.97 (24h) and 0.99 (48h) — large — and pain SMD −0.55/−0.56 at 72–96h, across 660–950 nm and 1–6 application points; a separate 2024 review of 34 RCTs found pre-exercise PBM improved muscle endurance and strength recovery and cut inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6) meaningfully. The catch: 4-study meta-analysis arms, heterogeneous and often under-reported dosing, and content-farm/vendor amplification. This keeps it firmly (promising) not (proven). Verdict: **plausible mid-tier buy once the essentials exist**, but demand dose discipline (specify wavelength, irradiance, and time-on-tissue) and treat the marketing skeptically.

**PEMF (pulsed electromagnetic field)** (units ~$1,000 to $10,000+, priced on belief). Post-exercise data show *trends* toward improved peak power at 24/48/72h and the only consistent soreness reduction in one small trial, plus reasonable evidence for soft-tissue pain — but reviews conclude effects on physical *function* are inconclusive and protocols are un-standardized (plausible). Verdict: **spend last or skip.** There is no data-driven reason for a budget-constrained development facility to buy PEMF before it has a sauna, a Marc Pro shelf, and its assessment stack.

**For Soto:** In the programming layer, treat this whole tier as optional adjuncts, never as prescribed interventions with performance claims. Massage guns fold into warm-up/mobility (cheap, buy a handful). Red light is a defensible "phase-2 amenity" with a real-but-shaky evidence base — if bought, log dose parameters so Neptune can actually audit its own protocol later. PEMF is the line item to cut when the budget tightens.

## The Buy Order for a New Facility (Limited Budget)

A concrete, evidence-and-dollar-ranked sequence for a development lab standing up its recovery offering:

1. **$0 — Systematize sleep, hydration/nutrition, and daily mobility.** Track it in the athlete dashboard. Biggest readiness lever, lowest cost. Do this before buying anything.
2. **~$700–$2,800 — Marc Pro units (1–3).** Highest-ROI active device, throwing-specific, replaces ice, pro-adoption sells it.
3. **~$1,000–$2,000 — Massage guns (several) + basic mobility/soft-tissue kit.** Cheap warm-up/ROM/soreness utility athletes will actually use daily.
4. **~$3,000–$10,000 — One sauna (start infrared for cost/install; traditional if the mortality/plasma evidence and budget justify it).** First thermal asset; adds adaptation, aligns with development mission, doubles as heat-acclimation.
5. **~$900–$1,500 — Normatec.** Recovery-lounge amenity for perceived recovery; any-phase safe.
6. **~$1,600–$2,700 — Inflatable + chiller cold plunge.** Add cold cheaply once phase-logic exists to gate it. Defer the $10k–$30k+ spa build until athlete volume justifies it.
7. **(Later / optional) — Red light panel** with disciplined dosing; **Game Ready** only via rental/referral for post-op cases; **PEMF** last or never.

Everything above #4 should be reconsidered against putting the same dollars into **assessment tech** (force plates, bat sensors, expanding the TrackMan/Compete pipeline). Assessment builds the data moat and the differentiated product; the recovery room is a retention amenity. In a genuine budget squeeze, a facility can deliver excellent recovery with items #1–#3 alone (~$2,000–$5,000 total) and out-perform a competitor who spent $40,000 on a hero cold plunge.

**For Soto:** This ranking *is* a Neptune spec. Encode it as the recommended buildout sequence, and tie each device to a phase rule in the programming templates (Marc Pro = default post-throw; sauna = build-block + heat-acclimation weeks; cold = pre-competition/in-season only, contraindicated near acquisition-block work; compression = any phase). The through-line for Trevor: the tech that photographs well (plunge, PEMF) is the tech with the weakest evidence and the highest price; the tech that wins (sleep systems, Marc Pro, sauna) is cheaper and more defensible — exactly the sort of contrarian, evidence-graded stance that separates a development lab from a commodity cage barn.

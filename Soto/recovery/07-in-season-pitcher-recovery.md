# In-Season Pitcher Recovery Protocols — Between-Start Routines, Reliever Bounce-Back, and Recovery Markers

## TL;DR

- **A single start measurably degrades the throwing arm for 24–72 hours.** Pitching drops dominant-shoulder internal rotation by ~9.5°, total rotational motion by ~10.7°, and elbow extension by ~3.2°, and these losses persist at 24 hours (Reinold et al., n=67 pro pitchers) (proven). A simulated 75-pitch game cuts dominant grip strength ~12.66% (55.7→48.6 kg) and roughly doubles forearm-flexor and biceps soreness (Baumert-style repeated-measures study, n=30) (proven).
- **The recovery clock is autonomic, not just muscular.** Resting HRV (lnRMSSD) is significantly suppressed on Day 2 (the day after a start) versus every other rotation day and rebounds by Day 3 (Cornell et al. 2017, n=8 pro starters) (promising). This is the physiological basis for keeping Day 1–2 low-intensity and putting the between-start bullpen on Day 3 (~72 hours), not Day 2.
- **The canonical 5-day starter cadence:** Day 1 flush + arm care + mobility, Day 2 light catch/mobility, Day 3 bullpen (~30–40 pitches) or heavy long toss, Day 4 taper/light lower-body tune-up, Day 5 start (proven as convention; individual optimization is promising).
- **Relievers pay a small, real velocity tax for back-to-back appearances.** ~300k PITCHf/x pitches show fastball velo of 91.6 on 1 day rest vs 91.0 back-to-back vs 89.2 on three straight days — a ~0.5 mph drop on consecutive days and ~1.5 mph over three (plausible). Command and stuff shape can move independently of velocity, so velocity alone under-reads reliever fatigue.
- **Soreness, fatigue, and pain are distinguishable by location, timing, and quality.** Fatigue = diffuse muscle fullness during/right after; soreness = diffuse tenderness onset 24–48h, resolves by 72h, eases with movement; pain = localized, sharp, in bone/joint, persists past 72h, worsens with activity (Plisky framework) (promising). Needing Advil/Tylenol reclassifies "soreness" as pain — a shutdown flag.
- **Objective daily markers beat feel.** Grip dynamometry, shoulder IR/ER strength (ArmCare hand-held strength ICC/validity r≈0.72–0.81 vs dynamometer — good; ROM validity poor, r≈0.23–0.47), and IR ROM are the practical recovery markers; velocity loss over 2–3 outings is a lagging red flag (proven for strength, debunked for ArmCare ROM).
- **The elbow-torque workload model is the professional standard.** Motus/PULSE measures peak valgus torque (N·m) per throw, normalized for height/weight, aggregated into acute (7-day) and chronic (28-day) load; keep acute:chronic ratio in ~0.7–1.3 (promising).
- **The stakes are historically high.** As of 2024, ~36% of active MLB pitchers have had Tommy John surgery; average fastball rose 91.9→94.2 mph (2008→2023) and injury-related IL days rose from 11,668 (1995–99) to 31,558 (2023) (proven). Recovery discipline is now a career-length lever, not a nicety.
- **Cold/ice is a comfort tool, not an adaptation tool.** Between-inning shoulder/arm cooling can preserve velocity and lower RPE in-game (promising), but chronic post-outing cold immersion blunts strength/hypertrophy signaling and much of its felt benefit is placebo (promising). Phase-change cooling at 15°C for 3h post-game accelerated Day-1 grip (107% vs 95%, d=1.29) and IR strength (95% vs 83%, d=0.91) recovery (promising).

## 1. What a Single Outing Actually Does to the Arm

Recovery programming only makes sense once you quantify the insult. Three independent measurement lines converge on the same picture: a start produces a real, measurable, multi-day physiological deficit.

**Range of motion.** The foundational study is Reinold, Wilk, Macrina, Fleisig, Andrews et al. (JOSPT / AJSM lineage), which tested 67 asymptomatic professional pitchers. Immediately after pitching, the dominant shoulder lost **9.5° of internal rotation, 10.7° of total rotational motion, and 3.2° of elbow extension** (all p<0.001), with **no change on the non-dominant side** — and the deficits were still present at 24 hours (proven). The authors attributed this to acute eccentric-contraction-driven muscular changes rather than capsular/bony remodeling, meaning it is a fatigue signature, not a structural one. Practically: the arm is physically shorter in its motion arc the day after you throw, and forcing full-intent throwing on top of an unrestored arc alters joint kinematics.

**For Soto:** IR-loss magnitude (~10°) is a clean, cheap between-start marker. Neptune's intake battery should log baseline dominant/non-dominant IR and total motion; a >10° IR deficit persisting past 48h is a "not ready to ramp" flag. This maps to a Triton-side "ROM recovery" field per athlete, alongside the Compete/TrackMan velocity trend.

**Neuromuscular / grip.** A repeated-measures simulated 75-fastball game (5 blocks × 15 pitches, 5-min rests; n=30 high-level amateurs, mean age ~21) found dominant-arm grip strength fell **12.66% (55.67→48.62 kg, p<0.001)** in a near-linear decline, while dominant forearm-flexor soreness rose from 1.65→4.19 and biceps soreness 1.81→4.31 on a 0–10 scale (~+25% each) (proven). Notably, pitch velocity dropped only 0.93% (119.9→118.8 km/h) across the same span — **velocity was the last thing to fall.** Grip and soreness degraded long before the radar gun showed it. Pressure-pain threshold showed no significant change and was judged not useful for acute monitoring.

**For Soto:** This is the single most important operational insight in the doc — velocity is a *lagging* fatigue indicator. A hand grip dynamometer (~$40–150) captures forearm-flexor-mass fatigue that the gun misses. For Trevor's own throwing and for Neptune athletes, a pre/post grip test is the cheapest high-signal fatigue check available.

**Muscle function over days.** The phase-change-material cooling RCT (n=16 college pitchers, 23 cooling vs 24 control games) measured shoulder IR/ER strength, empty-can, and grip on Days 1–2 post-outing. In the untreated control arm, Day-1 IR strength was still only **83% of baseline** and grip **95%** — i.e., substantial function loss persisting a full day out (proven). Serum creatine kinase and soreness did not differ by treatment (P=0.885 shoulder soreness), reinforcing that felt soreness and blood markers are noisier than direct strength testing.

**Bottom line:** ROM, grip/strength, and autonomic tone all take 24–72h to normalize. Any between-start structure that ignores this is guessing.

## 2. The Autonomic Recovery Clock (Why Day 2 Is Sacred)

Cornell, Paolone, and colleagues (2017) tracked resting HRV (lnRMSSD, middle 5 minutes of R-R data) **every day for a full season in 8 Single-A professional starting pitchers.** The finding: **Day 2 — the day after a start — showed HRV significantly suppressed below every other rotation day, returning to baseline by Day 3** (promising; small n, single level). Suppressed HRV means sympathetic ("stress") dominance and incomplete parasympathetic recovery: global systemic recovery has not yet "kicked in" ~24h after a maximal-intent outing, even when the athlete subjectively feels fine.

The training implication (argued well by Velo University off the Cornell data): **do not load heavy the day after a start.** Adding significant resistance-training or high-intent throwing stress on Day 2 competes with repair processes while the ANS is still in sympathetic dominance. Light movement, mobility, blood-flow "flush," and mental-skills work are the appropriate Day-2 stimulus; return to normal lifting once HRV recovers (~Day 3). The caveat the same authors stress: **HRV baselines are highly individual**, so rolling per-athlete baselines beat league rules of thumb.

There is a legitimate counter-camp (some Driveline/Cressey-adjacent practitioners lift *heavy lower body* the day after a start precisely because the arm is the fatigued tissue and a big lower-body stimulus is far from the next outing). The reconciliation: Day-2 *arm/high-CNS* load should stay low; Day-2 *lower-body* load can be moderate-to-high if the athlete's HRV and readiness support it. This is an individualization problem, not a universal rule.

**For Soto:** HRV (via a chest strap or Whoop/Oura-class wearable) is a plausible Neptune monitoring layer, but the honest grade is "promising, individualized." Don't sell it as deterministic. The defensible product move: rolling 7-day personal HRV baseline + a simple "green/yellow/red" readiness flag that gates high-intent throwing, fed alongside grip and ROM into a single Triton athlete-readiness tile.

## 3. The Starter's Between-Start Routine (Day-by-Day)

The professional convention across Jaeger, Driveline, Tread, and team programs is remarkably consistent for a standard **5-day rotation** (start every 5th day, 4 days between). Distances below assume a ~300 ft long-toss capacity; scale to the athlete.

**Day 1 — Outing day / immediate post (recovery flush).** Post-outing: stationary bike flush to promote circulation, arm-care/cuff maintenance in the training room, mobility to begin restoring the ~10° IR loss, and (optionally) isolated cooling or cold tub for comfort/perceived recovery. Reinold's data says the sooner IR/total motion is addressed, the faster full-capacity throwing returns. Prioritize sleep and protein this night (see §7).

**Day 2 — Light day (respect the HRV valley).** Light catch / low-intent long toss out to ~120–200 ft, "stretch only," plus ≥10 minutes of dedicated mobility. Keep arm intent and CNS load low (Cornell HRV suppression). If lifting, keep it low-arm-stress; heavy pressing/high-intent throwing is contraindicated today. Distance/volume adjusts down if the outing was long.

**Day 3 — Bullpen / heavy long toss (the workhorse day).** This is where the between-start pen lives — ~72h out, when ROM and HRV have recovered. Long toss to ~200–300 ft with pull-downs, or a mound bullpen of **~30–40 pitches** (frequently including pitch-design / command work and next-opponent game-planning). Putting the pen on Day 3 rather than Day 2 is the single most-repeated pro recommendation ("if you start Sunday, pen is Wednesday").

**Day 4 — Taper / tune-up.** Light catch, stretch, arm care, and a light lower-body "tune-up" the day before the start. Nothing that creates residual fatigue.

**Day 5 — Start.**

For **6-day and 7-day cycles** (common in college/pro with modern extra rest), the extra day(s) are inserted as additional light catch/recovery days between the Day-2 light day and the Day-3 pen, or split the pen and long-toss peaks further apart. Tread's in-season blueprint frames the whole thing as "maximize recovery time for the next outing while maintaining strength over a long season" — in-season lifting is **maintenance at ~85–90% of normal working loads, never to failure**, because the goal is preservation, not progression.

**For Soto:** This maps cleanly to a Neptune "rotation template" object: 4–6 day-slots each with prescribed intent %, distance band, pitch-count target, lift focus, and required recovery markers to advance. It also maps to Trevor's own maintenance throwing — a 5-day microcycle he can run solo, with grip + ROM gates instead of a pitching coach's eyeballs.

## 4. Reliever Bounce-Back Management

Relievers are the hard case: outings are short (often 10–25 pitches) but unpredictable in timing, and back-to-back or 3-in-a-row usage is routine. The classic Hardball Times analysis (~300k PITCHf/x pitches) quantifies the tax:

| Rest state | FB velo (mph) | Sinker vert drop (in) |
|---|---|---|
| Back-to-back | 91.0 | 0.6 |
| 1 day rest | 91.6 | 2.3 |
| 2 days rest | 91.4 | 3.1 |
| 3 straight days | 89.2 | — |
| 5+ days rest | — | 3.4 |

Takeaways (plausible; observational, no command layer): pitchers lose **~0.5 mph back-to-back and ~1.5 mph over three straight days**; slider shape is stable across rest states; and — counterintuitively — **sinker vertical movement is *enhanced* on back-to-back days** (fatigued, slightly lower-slot delivery adds sink). So a tired reliever can be *more* effective with a sinker even as velocity dips — meaning velocity alone mis-reads reliever readiness. A separate modern analysis of ~33,000 relief appearances found save-conversion (~2 of 3) did **not** measurably degrade with recent workload, cautioning that in-game *performance* is a poor proxy for *tissue* fatigue and injury risk.

**Management principles that follow:**
- **Cap consecutive-day exposure.** Back-to-back is manageable; three straight days is where velocity and (presumably) tissue cost climb sharply — the industry norm is to avoid 3-in-3 and to force a down day after back-to-backs.
- **Maintain, don't "save," the arm.** Jaeger's reliever protocol: stretch the arm to ≥75% of max distance *daily* even on non-appearance days, low-intent on the return throws, to keep the arm conditioned and avoid the "saved arm goes cold" negative cycle.
- **Track workload, not appearances.** A save that takes 8 pitches and a save that takes 28 are different insults; a warm-up that didn't result in an appearance ("dry hump") still costs throws. This is exactly what elbow-torque workload units (§6) exist to capture.

**For Soto:** For any Neptune relievers/short-stint arms, and for Trevor (a career reliever late — 21 saves in 2023 with Oakland), the reliever model = daily submax catch to maintain + hard consecutive-day caps + workload-unit tracking that counts warm-ups. Triton could compute a per-arm "consecutive-day load" and flag 3-in-3 risk from Compete/game logs.

## 5. Soreness vs. Pain Triage

The most consequential in-season decision is "is this normal or is this a problem?" The Plisky framework (a clean synthesis widely used by MDs/PTs) separates three states by **location + timeline + quality + response to movement**:

- **Fatigue:** muscles feel "full, blood-engorged," tired; occurs *during or immediately after* activity; may alter mechanics/command; expected. Green.
- **Soreness:** diffuse tenderness/tightness across *multiple* areas; onset **24–48h**, resolves by **72h**; **improves with movement/stretching**; expected. Green, self-guided.
- **Pain:** **localized to one spot**, sharp, comes on fast, typically **in bone or joint**, **persists past 72h**, and **worsens with more activity.** Red — evaluate.

**Location map:** Green zones = back of shoulder, glutes, abs (expected work areas). Yellow (assess movement) = bicep/forearm muscle belly, posterior-shoulder pinch. **Red zones = front of shoulder, and elbow/bony areas** — especially **medial (inside) elbow**, the UCL / flexor-pronator territory.

**Hard shutdown flags** (multiple sources converge):
- **Sharp/stabbing pain during throwing** → stop, "there is no reason to push through."
- **Pain lasting >48h after throwing**, or any pain that **doesn't warm out** and worsens as the outing continues.
- **Needing NSAIDs (Advil/Tylenol) for the sensation** — that reclassifies "soreness" as pain.
- **Velocity loss across 2–3 outings**, or loss of command/control.
- Pop/tear sensation, swelling, numbness/tingling, instability, or pain during everyday reaching/lifting.

The decision must be **multifactorial** — no single criterion. Diffuse posterior-shoulder tightness that eases with a mobility session on Day 2 is textbook soreness; sharp medial-elbow pain at first-pitch that grows across the pen is a red flag regardless of how "loose" the athlete feels.

**For Soto:** This is a codifiable triage tree — a Neptune intake/daily-check flow (location tap-map → onset timing → sharp vs dull → NSAID yes/no → warms-out yes/no) that outputs green/yellow/red and, on red, routes to evaluation before the next throwing slot. It's low-effort, high-liability-reduction, and pairs naturally with the grip/ROM markers. For Trevor personally: post-TJ (2017) medial-elbow signals get the lowest threshold — inside-elbow sharpness is never "soreness."

## 6. Objective Recovery Markers and Workload Monitoring

Feel is necessary but insufficient. The professional toolkit layers cheap→expensive:

**Grip dynamometry (cheapest, high-signal).** Pre/post and day-over-day grip captures forearm-flexor-mass fatigue that velocity misses (~12.66% acute in-game drop; recovery to ~95% at Day 1 untreated). One clinical dataset noted that pitchers who later suffered shoulder/elbow injuries showed grip drops up to ~25% by mid-game vs uninjured peers — grip trajectory as an early risk flag (promising). Cost: ~$40–150 for a hand dynamometer.

**Shoulder strength / ROM (ArmCare-class).** ArmCare's ~6-minute self-administered exam measures IR/ER/scaption strength and IR/ER/flexion ROM; its **Fatigue score** = strength lost from a single outing across tested muscles, and **Recovery score** = strength on subsequent days vs the athlete's average (positive = recovering/gaining). Independent validation (n=51, ages 14–23): **strength measures are reliable and valid** (intra-rater ICC 0.73–0.87; validity vs hand-held dynamometer r≈0.72–0.81, ICC 0.72–0.79 — good), but **ROM measures are poor** (validity r≈0.23–0.47, ICC 0.11–0.37) (proven). So: trust the strength/fatigue/recovery numbers, treat the app's ROM readings as directional only. Device+app is a few hundred dollars per unit.

**Elbow-torque workload (Motus/PULSE — the pro standard).** A compression-sleeve sensor below the UCL measures **peak valgus torque (N·m) per throw**, normalized to the athlete's height (m) and weight (kg). Throws aggregate into **Daily**, **Acute (7-day)**, **Chronic (28-day)**, and the **Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR)**; the suggested safe band is **~0.7–1.3.** The model's virtue is that it counts *every* throw (warm-ups, long toss, pens, dry warm-ups) as torque units, so it captures the true stimulus a pitch count hides. Modern critiques (ArmCare's "rethinking ACWR") note ACWR is a blunt instrument — a pitcher with a chronic load of 8 units could throw a 20-unit day back-to-back without ACWR exceeding 1.3, so ACWR must be read with absolute-load and individual context, not as a hard gate (plausible).

**Velocity (lagging).** Sustained velo loss over 2–3 outings is a real red flag but arrives *after* grip/ROM/strength have already degraded — use it as confirmation, not early warning.

**HRV / readiness (§2).** Rolling personal baseline; promising, individualized.

**For Soto:** The recommended Neptune stack, cheapest-first: (1) grip dynamometer + ROM goniometry at intake and as daily/weekly checks; (2) ArmCare-class strength testing (trust strength, discount ROM) weekly + post-outing; (3) TrackMan velocity/shape trend (already in Compete); (4) optionally PULSE/torque for higher-value arms. All of it collapses into one Triton "arm-readiness" object per athlete: `grip_pct_baseline`, `ir_rom_deficit_deg`, `fatigue_score`, `recovery_score`, `acwr`, `velo_trend`, `readiness_flag`. This is a genuine differentiator vs commodity cage facilities.

## 7. Recovery Modalities — What Works, What's Theater

**Sleep (highest-leverage, cheapest, most-proven).** <8h/night is associated with **~1.7× higher injury risk**; sleep extension improves reaction time, precision, and lowers cortisol; an 80-player MLB study linked daytime sleepiness to shorter careers and demotions, and plate discipline measurably decays across the 162-game grind from mental fatigue (proven for injury/performance association; causal chain promising). Nothing else on this list rivals sleep's evidence base. Practical: prioritize the night *of* and *after* a start; naps partially offset deficits.

**Nutrition / protein (proven fundamentals).** Adequate calories and protein are the substrate for tissue repair; Tread lists "sleep, calories, protein, active recovery, workload management" as the core recovery variables — deliberately unglamorous. Underfueling in-season is a common, correctable recovery failure.

**Active recovery / flush (promising).** Light aerobic flush (bike), submax catch, and mobility restore ROM and circulation; the combination of light exercise + short-term icing between starts has some support. Mobility specifically targets the ~10° IR/total-motion deficit and is the highest-yield modality after sleep/food.

**Cooling / ice (comfort tool, not adaptation tool).** In-game *between-inning* shoulder/arm cooling helped maintain velocity and reduced RPE in college pitchers (promising). But: chronic post-workout cold-water immersion **blunts strength/hypertrophy signaling**, and controlled work suggests much of ice's felt benefit is **placebo / subjective** rather than measurable physiology (promising→partly debunked for long-term adaptation). Phase-change-material cooling (15°C, 3h post-game inside a compression shirt) is the exception with real data: accelerated Day-1 recovery of **grip (107% vs 95%, d=1.29)** and **IR strength (95% vs 83%, d=0.91)**, though it did **not** move soreness or CK (promising). Net: use cold for comfort and in-game velocity maintenance; do **not** rely on it to build the arm, and avoid habitual immersion right after strength work you want to adapt to.

**Modalities with weak/placebo evidence (debunked or unproven for physiology):** cupping, most compression-garment claims beyond comfort, and cryo-chamber-as-recovery — Fangraphs' "baseball pseudoscience" review and Cressey's cryo analyses put much of this in the placebo/comfort bucket. They're not harmful, but they shouldn't crowd out sleep/food/mobility/workload.

**For Soto:** Neptune's recovery marketing should lead with sleep/nutrition/mobility/workload (defensible, proven) and treat cold/compression as optional comfort adjuncts, explicitly not sold as adaptation drivers. For Trevor's content, the "ice is mostly comfort + placebo, sleep is the real drug" angle is both accurate and audience-grabbing.

## 8. The Modern Stakes and How Recovery Fits the Injury Picture

Recovery discipline sits inside an injury environment that has gotten dramatically worse. As of 2024, **~36% of active MLB pitchers have undergone Tommy John surgery**; average four-seam velocity climbed **91.9→94.2 mph (2008→2023)**; and injury-related IL days rose from **11,668 (1995–99) to 31,558 (2023).** Average career length for debut cohorts fell from ~6.95 years (2000 debuts) toward ~3.19 years (2020 debuts). MLB's own review ties the rise to velocity and spin maximization and grip-intensive "designer" pitches (sweepers, power changeups) that load the flexor-pronator/UCL. Among elite amateurs, ~70% of 245 top pitchers showed abnormal elbow-MRI findings — damage is accruing before pro ball.

The uncomfortable truth for recovery programming: **between-start routines and recovery markers do not, by themselves, offset the primary driver (max-effort high-velo throwing).** ASMI's landmark finding — adolescents who routinely pitch with **arm fatigue are ~36× more likely** to need elbow/shoulder surgery — is the strongest argument that the recovery layer's real job is **fatigue avoidance**: don't throw high-intent on an unrecovered arm, and honor rest. The Pitch Smart / ASMI rest table encodes this for youth:

| Ages 7–14 | Rest | | Ages 15–18 | Rest |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1–20 pitches | 0 days | | 1–30 | 0 days |
| 21–35 | 1 day | | 31–45 | 1 day |
| 36–50 | 2 days | | 46–60 | 2 days |
| 51–65 | 3 days | | 61–80 | 3 days |
| 66+ | 4 days | | 81+ | 4 days |

These are floors, not targets, and they're the evidence-graded backbone (proven) of any youth/HS program Neptune runs.

**For Soto:** If Neptune serves youth/HS (likely, per the context doc's age-band assumption), Pitch Smart rest tables are non-negotiable baseline policy and a liability shield. For college/pro-offseason arms, the between-start template + workload-unit ACWR + fatigue-avoidance triage is the spine. Across all ages, the recovery product's north star is **"never throw high-intent on a fatigued, unrecovered arm"** — that's where the 36× ASMI multiplier lives, and it's the one recovery lever with real injury-prevention evidence behind it. For Trevor (TJ 2017), the personal application is conservative: honor the HRV Day-2 valley, gate high-intent throwing on grip/ROM recovery, and treat any medial-elbow sharpness as pain, not soreness.

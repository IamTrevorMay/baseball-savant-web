# Sleep for Athletes — Extension, Injury Risk, Screening, Circadian Timing & Wearables

## TL;DR

- **Sleep extension is the single best-validated non-pharmacological performance intervention in sport.** Stanford's varsity basketball players extending to a 10-hour-in-bed target for 5–7 weeks (gaining ~111 min/night of actual sleep) cut 282-ft sprint time from 16.2 s to 15.5 s (~4%), added +9.0% free-throw and +9.5% three-point accuracy, and improved reaction time and mood. (proven)
- **Chronic short sleep in youth athletes is one of the strongest modifiable injury predictors on record.** Milewski (2014, n=112 adolescents) found <8 h/night carried a 1.7× injury risk (95% CI 1.0–3.0, p=0.04) — a bigger independent signal than hours of training per week. (proven)
- **A single night of sleep loss is metabolically catabolic.** Lamon 2021 (n=13) showed cortisol +21%, testosterone −22%, and an 18% drop in postprandial muscle protein synthesis after one night of total deprivation — directly hostile to arm recovery and hypertrophy. (proven)
- **Fatigue erodes plate discipline across the 162-game grind.** MLB strike-zone judgment was measurably worse in September than April in 24 of 30 teams; a Vanderbilt fatigue index predicted the decline and correlated sleepiness with reduced career longevity. (promising)
- **Jet lag is worth real runs and can erase home-field advantage.** The 2017 PNAS study (40,000+ games, 1992–2011) found eastward travel hit harder than westward, with the dominant effect being pitchers surrendering more home runs. (promising)
- **Naps of 20–30 min in the 1–3 PM window sharpen sprint speed, reaction time, and alertness; 90-min naps backfire via sleep inertia.** Benefits are largest in sleep-deprived athletes — exactly the state of an in-season pitcher after a night getaway. (proven)
- **Chronotype × game time shifts batting output by 40–50 points.** Evening types hit .306 in 8 PM+ games vs .252 for morning types; morning types out-hit evening types in sub-2 PM games (.267 vs .259). (promising)
- **Modern wearables are excellent at total sleep time but mediocre at staging.** WHOOP tracks total sleep to within ~1.4 min of PSG; Oura Gen3 (OSSA 2.0) validated on 421,045 epochs shows no significant TST/deep/light difference vs lab; all devices sit near 79–80% sleep/wake and only 65–70% four-stage accuracy. Trust trends, not any single night's "REM." (proven)

## Why Sleep Is the Highest-Leverage Recovery Lever

Sleep is the only recovery modality that simultaneously drives hormone regulation, tissue repair, motor learning consolidation, immune function, and cognitive/perceptual sharpness — the exact stack a pitcher needs. Unlike cold plunges, compression, or most supplements, the effect sizes are large, replicated, and directional (more sleep → better, less → worse), and the intervention is free.

The mechanistic case is now well quantified. Lamon et al. (2021, *Physiological Reports*, n=13, randomized cross-over) subjected healthy young adults to one night of total sleep deprivation and measured the next day: **plasma cortisol +21%, testosterone −22%, and an 18% reduction in postprandial muscle protein fractional synthesis rate** measured 13:00–15:00 (proven). That is a single night. It pushes the body into a catabolic, anabolic-resistant state — the physiological opposite of what an athlete accumulating throwing volume wants. Chronic partial restriction compounds this by suppressing growth hormone and IGF-1 pulses that normally ride the first deep-sleep cycles.

The performance side is equally concrete. Mah et al. (2011, *SLEEP*, Stanford men's basketball, n=11, 2005–2008) is the flagship study. After a 2–4 week baseline, players targeted ≥10 h in bed for 5–7 weeks and gained a mean **110.9 min of actual sleep per night**. Results: **282-ft sprint 16.2 → 15.5 s; free-throw accuracy +9.0%; three-point accuracy +9.5%**, plus faster reaction time, lower daytime sleepiness (Epworth), and improved mood (POMS) (proven). The magnitude — near a full percentage-point-per-week of skill accuracy — is why nearly every pro performance department now treats sleep as a trainable KPI rather than a lifestyle footnote.

**For Soto:** Frame sleep to Neptune athletes as a *trainable input with a dose-response curve*, not "get your rest." The Stanford deltas (~4% sprint, ~9% skill accuracy) are the persuasion anchor — an athlete who won't chase a 1 mph velo gain in the gym will chase it in bed once the numbers are on the table. For Trevor specifically, the Lamon hormonal data (−22% testosterone, 18% MPS drop from one bad night) is the arm-health argument that lands: post-TJ tissue quality and 30-something recovery both lean harder on deep sleep than they did at 24.

## Sleep Extension & Banking: The Protocol Evidence

The Stanford protocol is the template: extend time-in-bed toward 9–10 hours for multiple weeks and let actual sleep drift up ~1.5–2 h. But the practically important nuance is *banking* (a.k.a. sleep extension prophylaxis): sleeping extra in the days *before* an anticipated period of restriction blunts the deficit. Studies of pre-emptive extension show attenuated reaction-time and vigilance decrements during subsequent restriction versus starting the restriction sleep-debt-neutral (promising). For baseball this maps onto the schedule perfectly: bank in the off-days and the day before a night getaway or a coast-to-coast trip.

Single-night extension also helps acutely. A 2025 randomized crossover (PMC12387293) found one night of sleep extension enhanced next-morning physical and cognitive performance across time-of-day in physically active students (promising) — useful for the "big day tomorrow" case where multi-week extension isn't available.

Dosing guidance that holds up:
- **Baseline target:** 8–10 h/night for developing (adolescent) athletes per AASM; elite adults generally 8–9 h, with individual need above population norms because of training load.
- **Extension block:** push time-in-bed to 9.5–10 h for 3+ weeks to realize the Mah-type gains; expect actual sleep to lag time-in-bed by ~1–1.5 h.
- **Banking window:** 2–4 nights of +1–2 h before known restriction.

**For Soto:** This is a Neptune programming template, not just advice. Build a "sleep block" into the periodized calendar the same way you build a deload — e.g., an extension phase during heavy velocity-development or return-to-throw mesocycles when tissue-repair demand peaks. Track adherence via the athlete's wearable TST trend (see wearables section) so it becomes a monitored variable in the assessment→programming→monitoring spine, not an honor-system suggestion.

## Sleep & Injury Risk: The Hardest Numbers in the File

The injury literature is where sleep advice stops being soft. Milewski et al. (2014, *Journal of Pediatric Orthopaedics*, n=112 adolescent athletes, mean age ~15) remains the headline: in multivariate analysis the two strongest *independent* injury predictors were hours of sleep and grade in school. **Athletes sleeping <8 h/night had 1.7× the injury risk (95% CI 1.0–3.0, p=0.04)**, and each additional grade added 1.4× (a training-age/exposure proxy) (proven). Critically, sleep out-predicted several training-volume variables — chronic under-sleeping was a bigger flag than simply "throwing/practicing a lot."

A newer prospective study (PMC9496483, track & field adolescents, n=19, three phases across a season) fills in mechanism with objective actigraphy. These athletes averaged only ~6h56m–7h33m/night — all below the AASM 8–10 h floor — with sleep efficiency 82.5–84.25% (below the 85% quality threshold) and WASO of 29–46 min (above the ≤20 min target). Wake-after-sleep-onset predicted prior injuries (OR 1.144; the WASO model correctly classified 84.2% of cases) (promising). The signal: it isn't only *duration* — *fragmentation* (WASO, low efficiency) independently tracks injury.

The plausible mechanistic bridge to arm injury runs through two channels: (1) the catabolic hormonal shift (Lamon: cortisol +21%, testosterone −22%) degrades connective-tissue repair between throwing sessions, and (2) fatigue-driven mechanical breakdown. Driveline's own framing is that pitching while fatigued is dangerous because mechanics change — "arm lag behind the body due to leg and core fatigue" during arm-cocking increases valgus stress (plausible). That mechanical story is consistent with the ASMI/AJSM literature on cumulative fatigue and UCL load, though a direct "hours of sleep → elbow torque" RCT does not exist.

**For Soto:** This is the strongest evidence-graded lever for Neptune's arm-care and workload-monitoring product. Two concrete builds: (1) In the intake battery, capture habitual sleep duration and a fragmentation proxy — an athlete chronically under 8 h is a flagged injury-risk profile before they throw a pitch. (2) On the Compete/Triton data side, if you're already ingesting WHOOP/Oura per athlete, a sleep-debt feature (rolling TST vs individual baseline) is a candidate covariate in any workload/ACWR or injury-flag model — the youth-injury literature says it should carry real signal, especially the WASO/efficiency dimension, not just total hours.

## Fatigue, Circadian Timing & In-Game Cognition

Baseball is a perception-under-fatigue sport, and the season-long data show it. Two studies (AASM/Vanderbilt, presented 2013) analyzed strike-zone judgment across the year: **plate discipline degraded roughly linearly from April to September, and was significantly worse in September in 24 of 30 teams** (promising). The proposed driver is cumulative fatigue from travel density and few off-days — reaction time and rapid ball/strike judgment are among the first faculties to erode under sleep debt. A companion finding: a player's baseline sleepiness predicted his *career longevity* — sleepier players washed out sooner (promising, observational).

Chronotype interacts sharply with game time. A 2010–2011 analysis (n=16 MLB players across 7 teams, 2009–2010 seasons) found:
- Early games (<2 PM): morning types .267 vs evening types .259.
- Mid-day (2:00–7:59 PM): evening .261 vs morning .252.
- Late (≥8 PM): **evening types .306 vs morning types .252** — a ~54-point swing (promising).

Peak performance for most athletes clusters mid-afternoon to early evening (core body temperature peak), which is why the largest chronotype gaps appear at the temporal extremes. Note a contrasting finding in pro basketball (Clocks & Sleep 2021): chronotype did *not* affect in-game performance in evening (>18:00) games — so the effect may be sport- and task-specific, strongest in the fine perceptual timing of hitting (plausible).

**For Soto:** Two applications. (1) **Triton feature idea:** a chronotype-adjusted split. Day/night splits already exist; layering a simple morningness-eveningness questionnaire (rMEQ, 5 items) onto player profiles could surface lineup/usage edges the raw day/night split misses — an evening-type reliever is a different asset in a 1 PM getaway game than at 8 PM. (2) **Neptune assessment:** add rMEQ to intake; it's free, 2 minutes, and informs both training-time scheduling (train near the athlete's peak when developing skill) and travel guidance.

## Napping: Dose, Timing & Sleep Inertia

Naps are the highest-ROI acute tool for the in-season athlete because the population is chronically debt-loaded. The evidence converges on a tight prescription:

- **20–30 min, 1–3 PM.** Post-lunch-dip naps in this window improve alertness, short-term memory, 8-choice reaction time accuracy, and sprint performance. In partial-sleep-deprivation studies, 2-m sprint fell 1.060 → 1.019 s and 20-m sprint 3.971 → 3.878 s after a short nap (proven).
- **Avoid ~90 min pre-performance.** A 90-min nap decreased repeated-sprint performance and increased sleepiness via sleep inertia — waking mid-deep-sleep leaves you groggier than no nap (proven).
- **Buffer 30 min before activity** to clear inertia; benefits are largest for the already-sleep-deprived (proven).

Systematic reviews (Lastella/Vitale et al.) confirm daytime napping generally improves or maintains athletic and cognitive performance in athletes, with the strongest, most consistent effects on sprint and reaction-time tasks after nights of insufficient sleep (promising).

**For Soto:** Standard Neptune/road protocol for pitchers on night-game or travel days: a 20–25 min nap between ~1:00 and 3:00 PM, phone on airplane, room dark, alarm set, then ≥30 min buffer before catch play. For Trevor, this is the pragmatic hedge for content/streaming late nights — a short early-afternoon nap recovers alertness and reaction time without wrecking that night's sleep the way a long or late nap would.

## Travel & Jet-Lag Management

The jet-lag evidence is unusually clean because MLB is a natural experiment. Song, Severini & Allada (2017, *PNAS*, 40,000+ games, 1992–2011): **jet-lag effects were stronger eastward than westward, and the dominant effect was pitchers giving up more home runs** — enough, in the authors' framing, to "erase home-field advantage" for jet-lagged home teams (promising). The asymmetry is physiological: the human circadian clock free-runs slightly longer than 24 h, so westward travel (lengthening the day) is easier to absorb than eastward (compressing it). Rule of thumb from chronobiology: ~1 day of adjustment per time zone crossed, worse going east.

Management protocol (evidence-graded, from sleep-medicine and team practice):
- **Pre-adjust light & clock 2–3 days out** toward the destination time zone — for eastward travel, advance bedtime and seek morning light; for westward, delay and seek evening light (promising).
- **Anchor to destination local time on arrival**; eat and train on local schedule immediately (plausible).
- **Strategic light is the master lever** — timed bright-light exposure and avoidance shift the clock faster than anything else; melatonin (0.5–3 mg) at destination bedtime can assist phase-advance for eastward trips (promising).
- **Bank sleep before departure** and nap tactically on arrival day (see nap section).
- **Caffeine as a countermeasure**, not a masking agent — timed early in the destination day, avoided within ~6 h of target bedtime.

**For Soto:** Neptune is presumably mostly regional/travel-ball, so full transmeridian jet lag is rarely the issue — but *social jet lag* and getaway-day short sleep are. The transferable protocol is destination-anchoring and pre-trip banking for the occasional showcase/college-visit trip. If Trevor travels for appearances/content across zones, the eastward-is-worse asymmetry and 2–3 day pre-shift are the actionable pieces. There's also a **Triton angle**: the PNAS methodology (quantifying jet-lag run value from schedule geometry) is replicable on the 7.4M-pitch DB — a "travel-adjusted" context feature for game logs (miles/zones since last home game) is a novel covariate for park/context modeling.

## Sleep Hygiene Protocol (Environment & Behavior)

Sleep hygiene is the low-cost foundation — necessary but not sufficient. The best-synthesized source is Vitale et al. (2019, "Sleep Hygiene for Optimizing Recovery in Athletes," PMC6988893). Evidence-graded protocol:

- **Temperature:** ~18°C for males, ~19–20°C for females (≈60–68°F) improved sleep efficiency and reduced awakenings (promising). Cool, dark, quiet is the trifecta.
- **Light — evening:** avoid blue/screen light in the last 1–2 h before bed; melatonin suppression is dose- and wavelength-dependent. Mitigations: brightness down, night mode, blue-blocking glasses (promising).
- **Light — daytime:** ~2 h of bright/daylight exposure improves subsequent sleep duration and quality and anchors circadian phase (promising). Morning outdoor light is the strongest free zeitgeber.
- **Caffeine:** even 6 h pre-bed measurably disrupts sleep; practical cutoff ~2:00–4:00 PM (proven for the 6-h window; the athlete conflict — caffeine as a performance aid vs sleep disruptor — is real for night games and needs individualized timing).
- **Consistency:** fixed sleep/wake times (including weekends) reduce social jet lag; irregularity is independently associated with worse outcomes (promising).
- **Wind-down & environment tools:** eye masks, earplugs, white noise; avoid heavy late meals and late-evening high-intensity training that elevates core temp and delays onset.
- **Alcohol:** worth flagging — it shortens onset but fragments the second half of the night and suppresses REM, degrading recovery quality even when duration looks fine (proven).

**For Soto:** These become Neptune's athlete "sleep starter kit" checklist and a facility-design input. If Neptune ever adds recovery/lodging or a nap space: dimmable/circadian lighting, blackout capability, and a 60–68°F set point are cheap, evidence-backed specs. The daytime bright-light finding argues for genuinely well-lit training bays (or outdoor throwing) — it's a recovery intervention disguised as a facility feature.

## Athlete Sleep Screening

General-population sleep questionnaires (PSQI, Epworth) are *not* valid/reliable in athletes — training load, travel, and unusual schedules distort them. The purpose-built tools:

- **ASSQ (Athlete Sleep Screening Questionnaire)** — Samuels et al., clinically validated (2018, *Sports Medicine – Open*, PMC5986689). Self-report, deployable online, produces a **Sleep Difficulty Score (SDS)** binned into clinical categories with cut-offs that map to specific interventions. In validation, **~25% of athletes were flagged as needing further clinical sleep assessment**, with good agreement between ASSQ category and a sleep-medicine physician's rating (proven as a triage instrument). It's the standard first-pass screen — cheap, fast, sensitive enough to route the quarter of athletes who need real help.
- **ASBQ (Athlete Sleep Behavior Questionnaire)** — behavior-focused (18 items), better for identifying *modifiable* habits (screens, caffeine, irregular schedule) to target with the hygiene protocol above.

Screening cadence: baseline at intake, re-screen at load inflection points (start of heavy blocks, in-season peaks, post-travel).

**For Soto:** ASSQ is a plug-and-play addition to Neptune's intake battery — it's a validated, purpose-built instrument that produces a routable score, exactly the kind of structured input the assessment→programming→monitoring spine wants. Pair it with ASBQ so you get both the *"who needs a doctor"* triage (ASSQ) and the *"what habit to fix"* targeting (ASBQ). Digitize both as intake forms feeding the athlete profile; the SDS becomes a monitored field alongside TrackMan/force-plate metrics.

## Wearable Sleep Tracking: What to Trust

Wearables are how any of this becomes measurable at scale — but their accuracy is task-dependent, and misreading a stage number is the common failure mode.

Validated accuracy (2024 evidence, vs polysomnography, the gold standard):
- **Total sleep time (TST):** genuinely good. WHOOP showed the least disagreement of the wrist devices — **TST within −1.4 min** of PSG; light −9.6 min, deep −9.3 min (systematic review, JMIR mHealth 2024). Oura Gen3 with OSSA 2.0, validated on **421,045 thirty-second epochs across 96 participants** (Univ. of Tokyo), showed **no significant difference from PSG for TST, sleep-onset latency, WASO, light, or deep sleep** — only a slight REM underestimate of 4–6 min (proven).
- **Sleep/wake classification:** Apple Watch, Oura, and WHOOP all cluster around **79–80% accuracy** (proven).
- **Four-stage staging (REM/deep/light/wake):** the weak link — roughly **65–70% accuracy**; Oura led a 2024 head-to-head, ~5% better than Apple Watch and ~10% better than Fitbit. **REM is the least reliable stage across all devices** (WHOOP off by ~21 min in one review) (proven).

Practical reading: trust **duration and trends**; treat any single night's stage breakdown (especially "REM") as a soft estimate. The value is longitudinal — a rolling TST and efficiency baseline per athlete, watching for *deviations from that individual's norm*, not comparing one athlete's "deep sleep %" to another's.

Ecosystem context: Driveline logs **WHOOP data daily** to build a recovery/readiness profile, combining sleep with HRV — the established pro-dev-lab pattern. This is directly the Neptune model.

**For Soto:** Two builds. (1) **Device policy:** standardize on one platform per athlete cohort (WHOOP or Oura both validate well on TST) so baselines are comparable; don't mix devices within a monitoring cohort. (2) **Triton/Compete data model:** ingest nightly TST, efficiency, and HRV per athlete and compute *individual rolling baselines* — the actionable signals are sleep-debt (TST vs baseline) and fragmentation (efficiency drop), which feed both readiness flags and the injury-risk covariate discussed above. Explicitly *do not* surface single-night stage percentages as decision inputs to athletes — the ±20 min REM error will generate noise and false anxiety. Report TST, consistency, and efficiency; hold staging as trend-only. This mirrors the "trust the model, discount the bro-science" bar Trevor holds everything to.

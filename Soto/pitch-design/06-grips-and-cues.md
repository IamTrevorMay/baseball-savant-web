# Grips and Cues — the Hand-Level Toolkit of Pitch Design

## TL;DR

- **The hand is the last controllable link in the kinetic chain**: spin axis correlates strongly with hand/finger orientation just before and at release (proven — Driveline high-speed video + TrackMan work), and grip adjustments as small as 2–3 mm can shift a pitch's movement profile by ~2 inches of break (promising — practitioner-reported, not peer-reviewed).
- **Spin efficiency is trigonometry, not magic**: efficiency = cos(gyro degree) × 100. A 45° gyro angle → ~71% efficiency; 75° → ~26%. Benchmarks: 4-seam FB ≥90%, 12-6 CB ~78%, sweeping CB 65–75%, sweeping SL 15–35%, gyro SL <10% (proven — definitional math + Rapsodo/MLB pitch-tracking averages).
- **Finger force at release is large and bimodal**: instrumented-ball research on 11 pitchers found index/middle finger forces peak around 97 N each, with peaks ~38–39 ms and ~6–7 ms before release; tangential (friction) force is what converts hand speed into spin (proven — Kinoshita et al., Human Movement Science 2017).
- **Friction is a real performance variable**: with a wet ball, fingertip slip distance jumps to 21.6 ± 5.3 mm vs 8–9 mm with rosin/pine resin (~140–160% more slip), significantly cutting velocity, spin (r = −0.523 slip-to-spin), and command (proven — 2025 PMC study, n=6 semipro).
- **Pronation/supination bias should gate arsenal design**: pronators own the "pronator's triangle" (ride/run FB, gyro SL, CH — e.g., Scherzer's 15" HB changeup); supinators own multiple breaking balls and cut (Cole's −10.6" HB / −16.5" IVB curve) — and forcing pitches against bias costs shape and may add arm strain (promising — strong practitioner consensus from Bannister/Tread, thin peer-reviewed base).
- **The kick change (2023, Leif Strom @ Tread) is grip-first design for supinators**: spiked middle finger "kicks" the axis forward, ring finger kills efficiency; Clay Holmes threw one at 88 mph with −10" vertical break, and Tylor Megill's ran a ~50% whiff rate in early 2025 (proven usage/outcome data; mechanism promising).
- **Cue-first beats grip-first for retention once the shape exists**: external-focus cueing outperforms internal focus across 73 studies / 1,824 participants for performance (Hedges' g ≈ 0.38) and 40 studies / 1,274 participants for learning, independent of skill level (proven — 2022 Psychological Bulletin meta-analysis). Grip-first is still the faster on-ramp for creating a brand-new shape.
- **The 2024–2026 meta rewards seam-orientation literacy**: sweepers rose to ~7.5% of MLB pitches by 2025, splitters doubled from ~1.6% (2022) to 3.3% (2025) — and 6.8% of 2025 postseason pitches — and Driveline shipped computer-vision seam/axis tracking for bullpens in April 2026 (proven usage data).

## 1. The Causal Chain: Grip → Hand Orientation → Axis → Movement

Pitch design at the hand level runs on one chain: the grip (finger placement + seam orientation in the hand) plus wrist orientation at release set the spin axis and gyro degree; axis + spin rate + seam orientation in flight set movement via Magnus force and seam-shifted wake (SSW); movement + velocity + release traits set pitch quality.

Key quantitative anchors:

- Spin axis in three dimensions is significantly correlated with the orientation of the hand and fingers just before and at ball release (proven — Driveline's "Mastering the Axis of Rotation," built on 6,752 MLB player-seasons 2015–2018 plus Edgertronic capture). This is why grip/wrist work is the highest-leverage intervention: you cannot consciously steer the ball after it leaves, but you can pre-set the hand.
- Arm slot biases the ceiling: low-slot pitchers present a hand/wrist plane more parallel to the ground → natural run and sweep; over-the-top slots present a plane that maximizes backspin/topspin → carry fastballs and 12-6 curves (proven — same MLB dataset). Design with the slot, not against it.
- Tilt/degree conversion for reading TrackMan/Statcast: 1 hour of tilt = 30° of spin direction; 1 minute = 0.5°.
- Efficiency = cos(gyro°) × 100, and it is non-linear: going from 45° to 75° of gyro drops efficiency from ~71% to ~26%. Small hand-orientation changes near the middle of that curve produce big movement changes — which is why sliders are the most grip-sensitive pitch in the arsenal.
- Efficiency also drifts in flight: breaking balls with 1,500+ rpm of gyro spin gain 8–10% efficiency by the plate; under 500 rpm of gyro they gain ~1% (promising — Driveline analysis). "Late break" on gyro-heavy breakers is partly this effect.

**For Soto:** Triton's Stuff+ (100 + veloZ×4.5 + moveZ×3.5 + extZ×2.0) sees movement outcomes but not their causes. A facility-side "release fingerprint" layer — spin direction, gyro degree, efficiency, and (with video) seam orientation per pitch from Compete TrackMan sessions — is the diagnostic bridge: when a Neptune athlete's slider Stuff+ is low, the fix is almost always at the hand, and these are the fields that say which fix.

## 2. Grip Library by Pitch Type (Seam Orientation + Pressure Map)

Consensus grip library from Driveline, RPP, PRP, and Tread material. All movement numbers are MLB-average scale (RHP convention; 2020 MLB averages from PRP unless noted).

**Four-seam fastball** — index/middle across the horseshoe perpendicular to the seams; thumb under center. MLB avg (RHP): 93.8 mph, 2,308 rpm, 1:03 tilt, 15.8" IVB, 8.0" HB. Target ≥90% efficiency. To add ride: fingers closer together, thumb tucked, pure 4-seam orientation (promising). Pressure: fingertips, ball spun off the pads; middle finger is the last contact and the dominant spin contributor (proven — instrumented-ball data).

**Two-seam/sinker** — fingers along the narrow seams; 2-seam orientation exposes more smooth-leather surface to the airflow, which is what enables SSW run/drop beyond the Magnus prediction (proven — Utah State/Smith SSW research line). Cue family: "palm inside the ball," "throw the inner half." One-seam orientations (one seam catching air) are the aggressive SSW sinker variant (promising).

**Cutter** — 4-seam grip shifted slightly off-center with a subtle offset wrist; targets ~40–60% efficiency, 7–10" IVB, small glove-side cut (practitioner targets — PRP). Cue: "throw the arm side of the baseball," "backspin a slider." Good gateway breaking ball for pronation-biased arms who can't supinate a true slider (promising).

**Gyro slider** — middle finger pressed on a seam, ball released off the side like a football/bullet. Target <10% efficiency, gyro degree near 90°, ~5–10 mph off the fastball. MLB SL avg: 84.9 mph, 2,451 rpm. Cues: "throw the side of it," "twist the doorknob," "throw it like a dart/football," "think fastball longer." A tighter overall grip with less index/middle fingertip pressure biases toward gyro (promising — Rapsodo/vendor guidance).

**Sweeper** — 2-seam-style orientation gripped to put a seam under the middle finger and produce sidespin plus SSW; 15–35% efficiency; 15–22" of glove-side sweep with minimal depth (proven — MLB Statcast glossary). Usage climbed to 7.17% of MLB pitches in 2024 and ~7.5% in 2025. Cues: "come around the ball," "3-to-9 o'clock," "show the back of your hand to the catcher." Looser grip with more fingertip pressure biases from gyro toward sweep (promising).

**Curveball** — middle finger along a seam, index stacked or spiked (knuckle curve), thumb ridge on the back seam. MLB avg: 79.3 mph, 2,550 rpm, 7:21 tilt, −8.9" IVB. 12-6 versions run ~78% efficiency; sweeping curves 65–75% (avg ~68%). Cues: "throw the front of it," "turn it late," "break it from the catcher's mask to the dirt." Spiking the index finger consolidates pressure on the middle finger and typically raises spin (promising).

**Changeup family** — Driveline's grip census: the most common grip (~35% of athletes) is a two-seam orientation with ring+middle on the seams, index slid to the side of the ball, thumb underneath. Variations: 4-seam CH, off-seam grips, and a splitter-hybrid wide grip. Targets: 8–12 mph off the fastball, tilt 1:30–2:30 drifting toward 3:00 with more sidespin. Whiff payoff for separation is real: ≥10 mph differential → 34.2% whiff vs 26.1% at <8 mph (proven — pitch-tracking aggregates). Pressure map: middle finger + thumb carry the ball; index is a passenger. Cues: "let it roll off the middle finger," "throw it with your ring finger," "swipe the inside of the ball," "pronate sooner," "hit the catcher's feet."

**Kick change** — the marquee 2023–2025 grip innovation (Leif Strom, Tread Athletics, 2023; derived from Edgertronic footage of a Shaun Anderson changeup at −5.1" VB / 13.2" HB). Changeup grip with the middle finger *spiked* (raised off the ball): the spike "kicks" the spin axis forward at release while the ring finger cuts efficiency, producing splitter-like depth at changeup-plus velocity — without requiring pronation, which is why it works for supinators. Outcomes: Clay Holmes 88 mph / −10" VB exhibition version; Hayden Birdsong (first MLB user, June 2024) 46.7% whiff / .188 BA; Tylor Megill ~50% whiff early 2025; Griffin Canning's ERA fell 5.19 → 2.50 after adopting it. Grip variants scale with hand size (bigger hands spike more aggressively; some spike beside the seam) (proven adoption/outcomes; mechanism promising).

**Splitter** — wide two-finger grip outside the seams; ball leaves off the *inside* of the fingers, not the tips, which is the spin-kill mechanism. Cue: "throw the area between your fingers at the target." Finger span/flexibility is a genuine anatomical screen — narrow-handed athletes should be routed to kick change instead (promising). Usage doubled from ~1.6% of MLB pitches in 2022 to 3.3% in 2025, and hit 6.8% in the 2025 postseason (Gausman at 41.4% playoff usage) (proven).

**For Soto:** this table is the seed for a Neptune "grip card" system — per-athlete photos (top + side), seam orientation, pressure map, current cue, and the TrackMan session ID from Compete where the variant was tested. Store grip metadata on `compete_pitch_sessions` so shape deltas are attributable to specific grip experiments.

## 3. Seam Orientation and Seam-Shifted Wake: the Second Movement Engine

Since ~2019 (term coined by Andrew Smith, Utah State), it's established that seam position relative to airflow can force early boundary-layer separation and push the ball in directions raw spin axis doesn't predict (proven — wind-tunnel + Hawk-Eye validation). Grip-design implications:

- **4-seam orientations are Magnus-dominant** (seams busy everywhere, little smooth-surface asymmetry): 4-seam FB, cutters, most gyro sliders. **2-seam orientations expose surface for SSW**: sinkers, changeups, splitters, curve variants. **1-seam orientations are the extreme SSW case**: hard sweepers, SSW sinkers, some splitters (promising — practitioner synthesis of the aero research).
- SSW scales inversely with velocity — more flight time, more wake deflection — so it matters most on offspeed (plausible).
- Wrist orientation interacts: supinated wrists at release generate more SSW-friendly seam presentations at a velocity cost; pronated wrists produce run with the seam-shift positioned differently (plausible — practitioner observation, not yet well quantified).
- The practical magnitude claim from the seam-effects community: changing only finger pressure or the cue can turn the same nominal pitch from ~20" of sweep to ~20" of run (promising — extreme but directionally consistent with observed SSW pitch pairs).
- Measurement caught up in 2026: Driveline's computer-vision system (April 2026) reads spin axis and seam orientation from bullpen video via deep learning, giving real-time seam feedback without Hawk-Eye. In their public test case (recreating Tatsuya Imai's "backward slider"), a data scientist iterated ~50 throws with CV-guided grip/release tweaks, reproducing the arm-side run but not the gyro component (proven that the tool exists; efficacy promising).

**For Soto:** TrackMan reports axis and efficiency but *inferred* axis vs observed movement lets you estimate SSW: the gap between spin-based expected movement and actual movement is a computable "seam effect" residual. That's a cheap, shippable Triton metric on both Statcast (`pitches` has the ingredients) and Compete data — and a differentiator metric for Neptune bullpens before any camera investment.

## 4. Finger Pressure and Friction: What Peer-Reviewed Work Actually Shows

This is the most under-taught, best-measured layer:

- **Force magnitude/timing**: instrumented-ball measurement (tri-axial transducer inside a regulation ball; 11 pitchers throwing four-seamers) found index and middle finger resultant forces are bimodal — first peak ~38–39 ms before release, second ~6–7 ms before release — with amplitudes around 97 N per finger. The late surge in *tangential* force as the ball rolls off the fingertips is what generates spin (proven — Kinoshita et al., Human Movement Science 2017).
- **Force strategy ↔ efficiency**: newer work (Motor Control, 2026) links individual finger-force control strategies to fastball spin efficiency — pitchers differ systematically in how they distribute and time fingertip force, and those strategies map to useful-spin outcomes (promising — recent, small-n).
- **Friction is load-bearing**: under wet-ball conditions fingertip slip distance during release was 21.6 ± 5.3 mm vs 8.9 ± 3.5 mm (rosin) and 8.2 ± 2.2 mm (pine resin) — 142–164% more slip — with significantly lower velocity, lower spin (slip-to-spin r = −0.523), and worse command; the fingertips failed to "catch the seam" (proven — 2025, n=6 semipro). Practical: grip strength/friction management (rosin habits, sweat management, seam-catching grips) is a legitimate performance intervention, not superstition — and it's the physics behind the 2021 sticky-stuff spin drops.
- **Pressure as a design dial**: practitioner consensus treats finger pressure as the fine-adjustment knob between grips — e.g., index-finger pressure through release adds break on breaking balls; middle-finger-dominant pressure with a dead index defines the changeup; fingertip vs pad contact toggles sweep vs gyro on sliders (promising — consistent across Driveline/RPP/Rapsodo, unquantified).

**For Soto:** two Neptune protocol notes. (1) Standardize rosin availability and note ball condition in Compete session metadata — a humid-day session can masquerade as a 100-rpm spin regression. (2) Spin rate per mph (Bauer units) tracked per athlete over time in Triton is a cheap proxy for grip/friction consistency; flag session-over-session drops >5% for a hand-level review before touching mechanics.

## 5. Pronation/Supination Bias: the Gating Variable for Grip Selection

The dominant arsenal-design framework of the 2020s, popularized by Brian Bannister and operationalized by Tread Athletics:

- **Definitions**: pronation bias = tendency to get inside the ball at release (palm turning out/down); supination bias = knuckles working toward the outside of the ball, palm turning up/in. Everyone pronates *after* release during deceleration; the bias is about orientation through the release window (proven anatomy; bias taxonomy promising).
- **Pronator's triangle** (Tread/Bannister term): fastball ride/run + gyro slider + changeup — a mix a pronator can execute at high quality. Case data: Luis Castillo's 4-seam at 44.4% usage, 33% whiff, .278 wOBA against from a 5.23-ft slot (8th-lowest of 179 qualified SP) at 96.6 mph; Jesús Luzardo's gyro slider at 51.8% whiff (2023). Max Scherzer (pronator): elite CH (15" HB, 1.2" IVB, 83.8 mph) and firm cutter, mediocre CB/SL shapes.
- **Supinator profile**: multiple quality breaking balls plus cut; weak traditional changeups. Gerrit Cole: SL at −4.9" HB / 0" IVB / 89.1 mph and CB at −10.6" HB / −16.5" IVB / 82.9 mph, but a below-average changeup shape (14.8" HB, 8.9" IVB). Burnes and Bradish are canonical supinators.
- **Design rule**: give each bias its missing pitch *without* fighting the bias — supinators get arm-side action via SSW pitches whose grips do the pronation for them (kick change, seam-effect sinker, splitter); pronators get glove-side action via gyro sliders and cutters rather than big sweepers (promising — strong consensus, thin controlled evidence).
- **Assessment**: no validated clinical test exists; practice is to read Edgertronic release frames, movement-profile fingerprints (which shapes come easy), and observed comfort across grip trials. One school (Baseball ActionID) argues the bias is an innate motor preference rather than a mechanical artifact — coherent but unvalidated (plausible).
- **Injury claim**: "throwing against your bias adds arm strain" circulates widely (promising at best — no prospective injury data; treat as a programming heuristic, not a medical fact).

**For Soto:** a bias classifier is buildable from Statcast today — cluster pitchers on movement-profile asymmetry (arm-side vs glove-side shape quality relative to slot) and validate against known pronator/supinator exemplars above. That becomes (a) a Triton scouting field, (b) a Neptune intake finding that gates which grip cards even get trialed, and (c) context for Trevor's own arsenal history (his plus curveball + four-seam profile reads supinator-leaning — testable against his own Statcast rows in `pitches`).

## 6. Cue-First vs Grip-First: Design Philosophy

Two entry points for changing a pitch:

**Grip-first** — change the physical interface (finger placement, seam orientation, spike, span) and let the new shape fall out. Strengths: fastest way to *create* a shape that doesn't exist, especially against bias (the kick change is the flagship: the grip mechanically kills spin so the supinator doesn't have to learn pronation). The Mets' 2025 adoption chain (Holmes → Megill grip adjustment in one bullpen: "that made it a lot more consistent") shows grip-first transfer between athletes. Weakness: grips that fight the athlete's release fingerprint degrade under fatigue and competition.

**Cue-first** — hold the grip, change the intention: "throw the front of it," "hit the catcher's feet," "show the back of your hand." Strengths: preserves arm speed and intent, and the motor-learning evidence is firmly on its side for retention — external-focus instruction beats internal focus with overall Hedges' g ≈ 0.38 across 73 performance studies (n=1,824) and holds for learning across 40 studies (n=1,274), independent of age and expertise (proven — Psychological Bulletin 2022 meta-analysis); distal external cues ("break it into the dirt") beat proximal ones (SMD ≈ 0.23) (promising). Internal, body-part cues disrupt automaticity — but novices may transiently need internal grip/wrist scaffolding before externalizing (promising).

**Synthesis (practitioner consensus + motor-learning literature):**
1. Use grip-first to *find* the shape (biggest, fastest lever; measurable same-session on TrackMan).
2. Use pressure tweaks as the mid-grain dial between grips.
3. Convert to one external, outcome-anchored cue per pitch to *keep* the shape ("one clear external cue beats three competing ones").
4. Cues are not one-size-fits-all — RPP and Driveline both emphasize per-athlete cue trial with immediate ball-flight feedback; the ball flight is itself the best external focus target.
5. Iterate in tight loops: modern bullpen design (Driveline CV, Edgertronic + TrackMan) exists to keep the try→measure→adjust loop inside seconds, not sessions.

**For Soto:** encode this as the Neptune pitch-design SOP: grip card → 5–8 throw block → shape readout from Compete → pressure/cue adjustment → converge on one external cue, logged with the session. Cue text belongs in the athlete record next to the grip photo; a shape that can't survive cue-only maintenance for two sessions gets its grip revisited.

## 7. Tech Stack and Cost Reality for a Facility

- **TrackMan** (Neptune already has via Compete): ~$25,495 for TrackMan 4-class hardware; the gold standard for spin/movement per pitch.
- **Rapsodo Pitching**: ~$3,000 — adds measured (not inferred) spin axis + efficiency at a fraction of TrackMan cost; the standard second station for pitch-design bays.
- **Edgertronic high-speed camera**: from ~$5,500 (SC1, 1280×1024, up to ~17,800 fps) — the release-frame ground truth that made modern pitch design possible; a phone at 240 fps is the free-tier substitute for gross grip checks but can't resolve seam orientation at release.
- **Driveline computer-vision seam/axis tracking** (April 2026): deep-learning seam + axis overlay from bullpen video, real-time; positioned to replace manual Edgertronic frame-reading for iteration speed (promising — new).
- **Instrumented balls** (PitchLogic-class, low hundreds of dollars) measure spin/efficiency cheaply but change ball feel; useful for remote athletes, not for grip-feel work.

**For Soto:** Neptune's minimum viable pitch-design bay is TrackMan (owned) + one Edgertronic-class camera (~$6k) + rosin discipline + the Compete pipeline. The Triton-side differentiator is software, not hardware: per-athlete grip cards, seam-effect residuals, bias classification, and session-over-session shape tracking — none of which the hardware vendors ship.

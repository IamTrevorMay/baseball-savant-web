# Vision and Perceptual Training for Hitters — Evidence, Hype, and Honest Grades

## TL;DR

- **Elite hitters have genuinely elite eyes, but that is a selection fact, not a training result.** The average MLB player tests around **20/12–20/13** visual acuity, **81%** are 20/15 or better, and **~3.9%** hit the 20/8 human limit (Laby et al.). Contrast sensitivity and distance stereo acuity are also well above population norms. This describes *who survives to the majors* — it does not prove you can train an amateur into it (proven that the correlation exists; unproven that acuity training causes hitting).
- **The physics make "keep your eye on the ball" literally impossible for the last several feet.** A pitch crosses the plate at an angular velocity that exceeds smooth-pursuit limits; elite batters foveate the release point, ride the ball with head-plus-eye tracking for the early flight, then make a predictive saccade to the projected contact zone (Bahill & LaRitz; Toole & Fogt 2021 review) (proven).
- **The strongest *predictive* evidence is for pitch-recognition and oculomotor skill, not raw acuity.** In 34 Single-A hitters, video-occlusion pitch-recognition scores correlated most strongly with **season walk rate** (Müller & Fadde). In 71 pro batters, smooth-pursuit accuracy and oculomotor processing speed predicted highest league level attained and O-Swing/Z-Swing propensity (Liu et al. 2020) (promising).
- **Vision-training *outcome* studies are real but methodologically soft.** The famous UC Cincinnati result — team BA **.251 → .285 (+34 pts)**, SLG +32, K **−8.6%** — was a single-season before/after with no randomized control (Clark et al. 2012, PLOS ONE) (plausible, not proven). UltimEyes/UC Riverside reported **31%** acuity gains and an estimated **4–5 extra wins**, but the study was run by the product's own creators (promising-but-conflicted).
- **The best-designed intervention is VR, and it points to *adaptive* over *repetitive* training.** Rob Gray's 80-player randomized study: adaptive-VR hitters beat controls on in-game OBP (**d ≈ 1.8**), and at 5-year follow-up **40%** reached post-secondary competition vs **5–15%** for other groups (χ²=7.9, p=.047) (promising).
- **The field's central honest finding: near transfer is real, far transfer is not.** Stroboscopic and generic "brain training" reliably improve the trained task and some lab perceptual measures (near transfer), but a 2024 *Sports Medicine* review concluded there is **no supporting evidence** that generic perceptual/cognitive training transfers to actual sport performance (far transfer) (proven, in the sense that the null is well-supported).
- **Task-specific beats generic every time.** The winners — occlusion pitch-recognition, adaptive VR against a real pitcher library, live-arm plus machine variability — all share *representativeness*. The losers are context-free Gabor patches and strobe goggles doing random drills. Design the training to look like the at-bat (plausible → promising).
- **Price reality for Neptune's stack:** Senaptec Strobe **$299** (Classic), WIN Reality VR on a Meta Quest with a 600+ pitcher library on subscription, S2 Cognition a ~20-minute cognitive eval used in MLB/NFL draft rooms. Cheap-to-mid hardware; the value is the protocol, not the gadget.

## 1. What "elite vision" actually means in a hitter

The starting point for any honest discussion is that professional hitters are visual outliers. The canonical dataset comes from Daniel Laby and colleagues, who tested hundreds of professional players over multiple seasons: mean visual acuity clusters around **20/12 to 20/13**, roughly **81%** of pros test **20/15 or better**, and about **3.9%** reach **20/8** — the practical ceiling of the human eye. For context, one large Indian screening study found roughly **1 in 9,411** people with 20/10 vision; pros are drawn heavily from that tail. They also show superior **contrast sensitivity** (seeing a slightly-off-white ball against a busy background) and superior **distance stereo acuity** (depth judgment at range), with ~78% testing above general-population norms (proven that the profile exists).

The crucial interpretive move — and where hype merchants get slippery — is causal direction. This is a *survivorship* profile. Players who could not see the ball washed out of the funnel years earlier; the ones left standing in MLB batter's boxes are the visual elite by selection. Nothing in the acuity data says that raising a 20/20 amateur to 20/12 would raise his batting average. Static acuity is close to a floor requirement, not a dial you turn for performance (plausible).

More interesting than static acuity is **dynamic visual acuity (DVA)** — resolving a moving target. A clean controlled experiment (Uchida et al., 8 college baseball players vs. 8 non-athletes) found players resolved small targets moving at **404 ± 74°/s** vs. **315 ± 69°/s** for non-athletes under free eye movement (large targets: **545°/s vs. 417°/s**; p ≤ .006). But under **fixation** (eyes held still), the groups were indistinguishable (**94°/s vs. 92°/s**). The conclusion is important and under-appreciated: the athletes' DVA advantage comes from **better eye-movement control (tracking)**, not from a faster or sharper retina/image-processing system (proven). That reframes "vision training" as largely an **oculomotor and anticipation** problem, not an optics problem.

**For Soto:** When Neptune builds an intake assessment battery, static acuity is a screen ("does this athlete need a referral to an optometrist?"), not a KPI. The trainable, hitting-relevant signal lives in DVA, smooth-pursuit tracking, and pitch recognition. Don't sell acuity gains; screen for acuity deficits, then train tracking and recognition.

## 2. The physics of gaze: why you can't literally watch the ball hit the bat

A 90–95 mph fastball travels ~55 feet in roughly **400 ms**. For the early portion of flight the ball's angular velocity across the batter's visual field is modest and trackable, but as it nears the plate the required tracking speed spikes far beyond the ~**70–100°/s** ceiling of human smooth pursuit. The classic Bahill & LaRitz demonstration showed even a professional hitter cannot maintain foveal lock through the final several feet — the ball simply moves across the retina too fast.

What elite hitters actually do, per the Toole & Fogt 2021 review of head and eye tracking in batting and the earlier eye-movement work: they **fixate the anticipated release point** during the wind-up, then after a latency of roughly **150 ms** following release they begin **tracking the ball with a combination of head rotation and eye rotation** — notably moving the *head* more than the eyes toward the ball. Foveal gaze stays on the ball through the early-to-middle flight, and then most hitters execute a **predictive (anticipatory) saccade** to the location where they expect the ball to be at contact, rather than trying (and failing) to smooth-pursue it all the way in (proven). A minority of elite hitters can generate an unusually late predictive saccade or even a brief catch-up, but nobody watches the bat-ball collision with the fovea.

Novice hitters, by contrast, tend to move their eyes **before** release, or to fixate the wrong place entirely (the pitcher's head or torso) rather than the release window. There is also a "near-far" finding: professional hitters make multiple near (home plate) and far (pitcher) gaze shifts before the pitch, and players with better offensive stats (BA/OBP/SLG) shifted near-far **more frequently** before swinging — consistent with active timing/rhythm preparation rather than a passive stare (promising).

**For Soto:** This is the mechanism that makes "keep your eye on the ball" a coaching myth worth retiring at Neptune. The trainable behaviors are (a) a stable, correctly-placed fixation on the release window, (b) efficient head-led early tracking, and (c) accurate **prediction** of the contact zone — which is fundamentally a pitch-recognition/anticipation skill, not a "watch harder" skill. Any Neptune eye-tracking assessment (Pupil Labs, Tobii, or the S2 laptop battery) should score fixation location at release and predictive-saccade accuracy, not just "did they look at the ball."

## 3. Pitch recognition and anticipation: the strongest predictive signal

If acuity is mostly a floor and tracking is mostly physics-limited, the part of "vision" with the clearest link to run production is **anticipation** — reading spin, trajectory, and release cues early enough to make a good swing decision.

The landmark applied study here is **Müller & Fadde**, who tested **34 professional Single-A hitters** (each with ≥100 plate appearances) on a video **occlusion** pitch-recognition task: pitch clips cut off at four points in ball flight, with the hitter judging pitch type and location from the pre-occlusion cues. The novel contribution was correlating those recognition scores against **season batting statistics** — and the strongest correlation was with **walk rate** (promising). That makes mechanistic sense: recognition is a *swing-decision* skill, and swing decisions show up first in chase/walk numbers, only later in contact quality.

This dovetails with **Liu, Edmunds, Burris & Appelbaum (2020)**, who took spring-training visual/oculomotor evaluations on **71 professional batters** and matched them to pitch-by-pitch season data. The tests that mattered were the **eye-tracking** ones: **smooth-pursuit accuracy and oculomotor processing speed** significantly predicted **highest league level attained** and **O-Swing / Z-Swing propensity** (whether a batter swings at balls out of the zone vs. strikes in it), though not Z-contact (Z-Miss). The larger **Burris et al. (2018, Scientific Reports)** dataset of **252 professional players** similarly found that batters with better dynamic visual/sensorimotor abilities produced better plate-discipline outcomes (promising). The through-line across all three: the *decision* metrics (chase, walk, O-swing) are where visual/cognitive skill shows up, not raw contact.

**For Soto:** This maps cleanly onto Triton metrics. The outcomes these studies predict — O-Swing%, Z-Swing%, chase, walk rate, and league-level progression — are exactly the swing-decision fields Triton already computes for pitchers-faced and can compute for Neptune's own hitters via the Compete pipeline. A Neptune "plate-vision" dashboard should headline chase rate and Z-swing decision quality, benchmarked against level, and treat a high occlusion/recognition score as a *leading indicator* that should later show up as improved chase. This is the single most defensible vision-adjacent product Neptune can build.

## 4. Stroboscopic training: real near transfer, unproven far transfer

Stroboscopic eyewear (Senaptec Strobe, formerly the Nike Vapor Strobe) uses liquid-crystal lenses that flicker between clear and opaque, intermittently removing visual information so the athlete must extract more from each glimpse and lean harder on prediction. The Senaptec Strobe Classic retails at **$299** (Pro/Elite and the segmented "Quad Strobe" cost more), with volume discounts for facilities buying 5–10+ units. It is cheap, durable, and genuinely fun to use, which is a large part of why it spread through pro clubhouses.

The evidence is a textbook case of the near/far transfer split. **Near transfer is well supported**: strobe training reliably improves the trained perceptual functions — motion sensitivity, transient attention, short-term visual memory, anticipatory timing, and lab-measured DVA (promising-to-proven for near transfer). Multiple lab studies show strobe groups beating controls on catching, timing, and foveal perceptual tasks.

**Far transfer — actual hitting — is not established.** The 2024 systematic reviews are blunt about this: strobe work produces near transfer to perceptual-cognitive function "yet the evidence for a far transfer to sport performance is lacking." No randomized study has cleanly shown that a block of strobe drills raises game batting output against a real control (debunked as a standalone claim; the strong version "strobe glasses make you a better hitter" is not supported).

The productive use is *not* strobe-goggles-doing-random-drills. It is wearing strobes **during representative hitting tasks** — soft-toss, machine, or live BP — so the near-transfer stimulus is coupled to the sport skill itself. That converts a generic perceptual drill into something closer to sport-specific practice, which is where the transfer evidence actually lives.

**For Soto:** Stock strobes at Neptune as an inexpensive *adjunct to hitting reps*, not a standalone "vision station." Marketing should be honest: "we use strobes during live BP to stress your timing and recognition," not "strobe training adds 30 points of average." Log usage but don't promise a specific batting delta — you can't cite one. Trevor, as a pitcher, can also use strobes during catch-play/command work for the same near-transfer timing benefit, with the same honesty caveat.

## 5. VR and adaptive training: the best-designed evidence in the field

Virtual reality is where the methodology gets good, and the reason is that VR can be made **representative** (a real pitch, real distance, real spin, real decision) and **adaptive** (difficulty auto-tuned to the hitter).

The cornerstone is **Rob Gray's** randomized study of **80 high-school hitters** split into four groups of 20: (1) **adaptive VR** (pitch speed, height, and lateral location auto-adjusted to the batter via a staircase), (2) repetitive VR batting practice, (3) extra real batting practice, (4) no extra training. Protocol: two 30–45-minute sessions/week for 6 weeks. Results: the adaptive-VR group improved on 7 of 8 batting-performance assessments and, critically, showed transfer to **in-game on-base percentage** vs. repetitive VR (**d ≈ 1.2**) and control (**d ≈ 1.8**). At a **5-year follow-up**, **40%** (8/20) of the adaptive-VR group had advanced to post-secondary competition vs. **15%** (real BP), **5%** (repetitive VR), and **5%** (control) — a statistically significant distribution (χ²=7.9, p=.047) (promising; small n, single lab, but randomized with real-world follow-up). The headline lesson is not "VR works" but "**adaptive/variable** practice beats **repetitive** practice" — the adaptive VR group beat *real* repetitive batting practice too.

The commercial heir is **WIN Reality**, which runs on a consumer Meta Quest headset plus a phone app, with a **600+ pitcher library**, age-appropriate mound distances, and pitch-tracking. Its published numbers are **internal, uncontrolled marketing data**: an average **+12.5% OBP**, Z/O swing ratio improving **~2.55 → 2.90**, **~+35 points of batting average**, and **~+50 points of OPS**. Treat those as vendor claims, not evidence (plausible at best; no independent control). WIN's own honest line — "it does not replace swing mechanics coaching, live at-bats, or cage work" — is the right framing. The underlying construct (see many pitches, make many graded swing decisions, adaptively) is exactly what Gray's controlled data supports; the specific percentages are not independently verified.

**For Soto:** VR is the highest-upside item in Neptune's perceptual stack because it is representative and adaptive by construction, and because it scales rep volume for swing *decisions* without arm/body load. Two design rules straight from the evidence: (1) make it **adaptive** — do not let athletes camp on the same easy pitch; and (2) capture the decision data. If Neptune uses WIN Reality or a similar system, pipe swing-decision output into the Compete/Triton layer and validate it against the athlete's *actual* chase and Z-swing on live BP and in games. That converts a marketing gadget into a measured Neptune metric and lets you publish your *own* honest effect size instead of quoting someone's.

## 6. Perceptual learning and cognitive eval: UltimEyes and S2 Cognition

Two other product categories deserve honest grading.

**Perceptual learning (UltimEyes / UC Riverside).** Aaron Seitz's group trained UC Riverside baseball players on Gabor-patch detection tasks (contrast targets tuned to early visual cortex) for **25 minutes/day, 4 days/week**. They reported a **31%** improvement in visual acuity in the 19 trained players vs. 18 untrained, fewer strikeouts, more runs created, and — after adjusting for maturation — an estimated **4–5 additional team wins** (Deveau, Ozer & Seitz, 2014). The result is real and the paper is peer-reviewed, but there are two large caveats: the study was run by the people who **commercialized the product** (UltimEyes was a UCR startup), and the design lacked a proper randomized placebo-perceptual control. The FTC later scrutinized broad "improve your vision" app marketing in this space. Grade: promising-but-conflicted; the acuity gains are believable, the win-total attribution is a stretch (promising / partly plausible).

**Cognitive evaluation (S2 Cognition).** Founded 2015 by neuroscientists Scott Wylie and Brandon Ally out of Vanderbilt, S2 runs a ~**20-minute, four-battery** laptop evaluation of "Core 4" split-second cognitive skills (visual processing speed, decision-making, reaction) and is used in **MLB/NFL/NCAA draft and player-development** rooms. The pitch: mechanics being equal, the sub-500-ms recognize-decide-time chain determines outcomes, and S2 quantifies it. As an **assessment** with predictive/draft value, this aligns with the Liu/Burris finding that oculomotor and cognitive processing predict plate discipline and league level (promising *as measurement*). As a **training** claim — that repeating S2-style drills improves hitting — it faces the same far-transfer wall as everything else (unproven as training). The honest split: S2 is a good *scouting/screening* instrument, a weak *training* modality.

**For Soto:** For Neptune's intake, a cognitive-eval battery (S2 or an in-house build) is defensible as **assessment and athlete-profiling** — it feeds the "who is this hitter, where's the bottleneck" picture and can be correlated against Triton swing-decision data over time. Do not sell it as a training program that raises average. If Neptune collects enough paired eval-plus-outcome data through the Compete pipeline, that dataset itself becomes a differentiated asset — an in-house validity study nobody else at the amateur level has.

## 7. The honest verdict: near transfer is easy, far transfer is the whole game

The unifying framework — and the most important thing in this document — comes from the 2024 skeptical literature, especially the *Sports Medicine* review "There is No Supporting Evidence for a Far Transfer of General Perceptual or Cognitive Training to Sports Performance" (Fransen) and the *International Review of Sport & Exercise Psychology* systematic review (86 articles).

- **Near transfer** = training improves the trained task and closely-related lab measures. This is common and well-supported. Strobe glasses improve strobe-adjacent perception; Gabor training improves contrast detection; VR reps improve VR hitting; S2 drills improve S2 scores.
- **Far transfer** = those gains show up in *actual competition*. This is **rare** in all of human cognition, and the reviews conclude the claims of far transfer from **generic** perceptual/cognitive tools are "currently unsubstantiated." One study explicitly found dynamic vision training did **not** transfer to generalized visual-motor skills vs. a placebo (proven null for the generic case).

The prescription the skeptics actually endorse is not "do nothing." It is to abandon the search for a magic generic transfer and instead build a **sequence of near transfers** that marches progressively toward the real task — exactly how a strength coach progresses movement patterns. In hitting terms: recognition drills → occlusion → strobe-during-BP → adaptive VR against a real pitcher → live variable BP → game. Each step is only a *small* transfer gap from the next, and representativeness (does the drill look and decide like a real at-bat?) is the design variable that predicts transfer.

This also explains why the "winning" studies win. Occlusion recognition (Müller/Fadde) uses **real pitch video** and predicts a **real decision stat** (walk rate). Adaptive VR (Gray) uses a **real pitch decision** under **variable** conditions and transfers to OBP. The "losing" interventions are context-free: Gabor patches, random strobe drills, generic reaction lights. Same effort, different representativeness, opposite transfer.

**For Soto — the operating doctrine for Neptune and Triton:**
1. **Assess, don't over-train, the eyes.** Screen static acuity (refer out deficits). Measure DVA, smooth pursuit, and — most valuably — **pitch recognition via occlusion** and **swing-decision quality** from Compete/Triton data. These are the metrics with predictive support.
2. **Buy cheap hardware, sell the protocol.** Strobes ($299) and a Quest-based VR system are inexpensive. The differentiator is a *representative, adaptive, progressively-sequenced* curriculum and the Triton data layer that measures whether chase rate and Z-swing actually move.
3. **Promise decision-metric change, not batting average.** The defensible claim to a Neptune athlete is "we will improve your swing decisions (chase, O-swing, walk rate), and we'll show you the numbers" — because that is where the evidence actually lives. Never quote a vendor's "+35 points of average."
4. **Build your own effect size.** Neptune's edge is that every athlete's perceptual training can be paired with their real swing-decision outcomes in Triton. Run it as a rolling internal study; publish an honest number. That is worth more than any gadget.
5. **For Trevor personally:** the pitcher-side value is timing/rhythm (strobes during catch and command work, VR for sequencing intuition) and the credibility of being able to explain to athletes *why* "keep your eye on the ball" is a myth — the release-point fixation + predictive-saccade story is a better teach and it's backed by the gaze literature.
